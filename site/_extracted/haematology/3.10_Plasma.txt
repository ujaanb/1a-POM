    3.10 Plasma

           What is plasma?
             • The liquid component of the blood.
             Approximately what percentage of the blood does plasma account for?
             • 55%
           What is serum?
             •  Plasma minus the clotting factors.
         How is serum obtained from plasma?
             • The plasma is collected within a tube, and no anticoagulants are
               added
             •  This allows the clotting factors to combine together and form a clot,
                which is removed to obtain serum

         How much water is present in the average male?
             •  40L.
           What are the four main fluid components where water is found in the body?
             •  Intracellular fluid
             •  Interstitial fluid (ECM fluid)
             •  Transcellular fluid (i.e CSF, synovial fluid)
             • Plasma (blood)
           Rank the prevalence of these fluid components in our bodies from highest to
              lowest.
             •  Intracellular fluid (23L)
             •  Interstitial fluid (15L)
             •  Blood plasma (3L)
             •  Transcellular fluid (1L)

              Recall five functions of plasma.
             •  Clotting - it contains clotting factors
             •  Immunity - it contains antibodies and complement factors
             •  Osmotic pressure maintenance - Proteins such as albumin help to
                maintain colloidal osmotic pressure
             •  Metabolism - molecules to be metabolised often move around in the
               plasma
             •  Endocrine - many hormones travel in plasma to the target organs
             •  Excretion - waste products of metabolism (i.e urea) travel through
                the plasma to the site of excretion

           What technique can be used to assess the levels of different proteins
             present in the plasma?
             •  Gel electrophoresis.
             • A patient’s electrophoresis pattern can be compared to this typical
               one to assess for plasma protein deficiencies





           What are the two major types of plasma protein?
             • Serum albumin
             •  Globulins
           What is serum albumin?
             • The most abundant (55%) protein in the plasma.
              Recall the two roles of serum albumin.
             •  Transport of lipids, hormones and ions
                 ◦  Fatty acids released by lipolysis from the breakdown of
                        triglycerides in adipose tissue are transported by albumin for use
                   by tissues in 𝛃-oxidation.
             •  Maintaining osmotic pressure of the plasma.
                 ◦   It does this using its colloidal osmotic pressure (COP) effect
                  and drawing water into the blood

           What is the second most abundant plasma protein type?
             •  Globulins

              Recall the four main types of globulin.
             •  Alpha-1 globulin
             •  Alpha-2 globulin
             •  Beta globulin
             • Gamma globulin
              Recall one alpha-1 globulin.
             •  Alpha-1 antitrypsin. (A1AT)
           Where is alpha-1 antitrypsin (A1AT) produced?
             •  Alpha-1 antitrypsin (A1AT) is an alpha-1 globulin produced in the
                     liver

           What does alpha-1 antitrypsin (A1AT) do?
             •  Alpha-1 antitrypsin (A1AT) is used to inhibit the function of
                  proteolytic enzymes such as neutrophil elastase, preventing excess
              damage to tissues

           What is neutrophil elastase, and what does it do?
             •  Neutrophil elastase is a protease used to break down a key
                  extracellular protein called elastin which gives tissues their elastic
                   qualities

          When is neutrophil elastase released?
             •   It is released by neutrophils during inflammation
           What can alpha-1 antitrypsin (A1AT) deficiency hence lead to?
             •  Deficient A1AT can compromise the lung where degradation of lung
                  tissue leads to a loss of elasticity and respiratory problems
                 ◦  i.e COPD
             Describe the role of alpha-1 globulins.
             •  To inhibit enzymes that are involved in the breakdown of proteins
                  (proteases)
                 ◦  i.e neutrophil elastase released by neutrophils during
                     inflammation which can break down the elastin protein.

              Recall two types of alpha-2 globulin.
             •  Haptoglobin
             •  Alpha-2 macroglobulin

           What is the role of haptoglobin?
             •   It binds onto haemoglobin released by erythrocytes and the resulting
                haptoglobin:haemoglobin complex is removed by the spleen
             •  High haptoglobin can indicate high haemoglobin levels in the plasma
         How can haptoglobin be used clinically?
             •  High haptoglobin can indicate high haemoglobin levels in the plasma
               and hence haemolytic anaemia

           What is the role of alpha-2 macroglobulin?
             •   It is a protease inhibitor which can stop fibrinolysis, so that fibrin is
                not broken down
                 ◦   It does this by inhibiting plasmin - the principle fibrinolytic
                 enzyme

              Recall two types of beta-globulin.
             • Complement C3/C4
             •  Transferrin
           What is the role of transferrin?
             •  To transport iron that is released from the stores of ferritin or
                 dietary iron to wherever it is needed metabolically

              Recall two types of gamma-globulin.
             •  Immunoglobulins
             •  C-reactive proteins.
         How can C-reactive proteins be used diagnostically?
             •  Increases in the relative amounts of C-reactive proteins can indicate
                inflammation (and hence infection)

            Hence, what can increases in the relative amounts of gamma-globulin
              indicate?
             •  Gamma-globulins include C-reactive proteins and immunoglobulins
             •  Increases in the relative amounts of gamma-globulin can indicate
                   infection, inflammation as well as myeloma (cancer of B plasma cells)

           What is the most abundant cation in the plasma?
             • Na+
           What is the most abundant cation in red blood cells?
             • K+

           What mechanism in the body takes up over 1/3 of ATP at resting state?
             • The Na+/K+ pump.
         How much Na+ is pumped per K+ and in which directions?
             • 3 Na+ pumped out for every 2 K+ pumped in.
         Why is it important for cells that this pump keeps on operating?
             •  To maintain osmotic pressure within cells.
             •  Lack of operation can lead to influx of sodium ions and hence water,
                  leading to cell lysis by bursting
             •  Cells can even shrink if extracellular Na+ gets too high, which also
                causes problems such as death

           Which ion is used as an important cofactor for many enzymes?
             • Mg2+ (magnesium)
             • When intracellular
           What is meant when plasma is referred to as a “biomarker”?
             •   It describes how the proteome of the plasma (levels of proteins
                 within it) can be analysed and how different proteins can give us
                  indications of certain diseases etc.
                 ◦  i.e gamma-globulins indicate infection, myeloma etc.
             •  Links with disease, lifestyle and medications can also be made to the
               proteome

              Recall the main clinical use of plasma.
             •  Passive immunotherapy
           What is passive immunotherapy involving plasma?
             •   It can be used to transfuse plasma (or immunoglobulins directly) to
                 patients who require higher levels of immunoglobulins, for example
                 ◦  Antibodies can be administered intravenously to give protection
                      against common infections.
             •  This is a form of passive immunity, as the antibodies are being
                 transferred to the recipient, in contrary to a vaccine which is active
               immunity as the antibody is produced by the patient’s immune
                system.

              Recall the two main types of passive immunotherapy involving plasma.
             •  Intravenous Immunoglobulin G (IVIG)
                 ◦  This protects against most common pathogens. IgG is given to
                        patients.
             • Hyperimmune globulin
                 ◦  i.e a patient has too much of an immunoglobulin, so some is taken
                     out and frozen to be given to another patient who needs it

         How can donors be selected for immunotherapy?
             •  Donors can be screened for high levels of IgG against particular
                pathogens
             •  Those with high levels can be selected as donors.
