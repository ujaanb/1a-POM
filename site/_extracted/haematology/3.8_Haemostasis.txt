        Primary Haemostasis

              What is haemostasis?
                 •  Haemostasis is the “halting/inactivation of blood” following
                   trauma to blood vessels

                  Recall two reasons why haemostasis is important.
                 •  To prevent blood loss from normal blood vessels
                 •  To prevent blood leaking from injured blood vessels
            How is equilibrium an important aspect of haemostasis?
                 •  There needs to be a balance between coagulation
                       factors/platelets and fibrinolytic factors/anticoagulant proteins
                 •  This is because they both need to be in the right abundance to
                     prevent blood flow in the area of injury, but to allow blood flow in
                     other areas of the body.

                  Recall three benefits of equilibrium in haemostasis.
                 •  To allow the stimulation of blood clotting processes following
                         injury (coagulation - coagulation factors, platelets)
                 •  To limit the response to injury to a single area in the body
                           (thrombosis - platelets)
                 •  To start the process that leads to the breakdown of the clot that is
                   formed during healing (fibrinolysis - fibrinolytic factors)





                 Describe the vague process that occurs in a blood vessel in response
                  to endothelial cell injury.
                 •  Vasoconstriction occurs
                 •  Unstable platelet plug forms in blood vessel to prevent blood loss
                 •  Stabilisation of the plug with fibrin occurs - coagulation
                 •  Dissolution of blood clot occurs alongside vessel repair in
                        fibrolysis

              What is the open canalicular system?
                 •  This is the system used by platelets to release the contents of
                    the granules they need to release after adhesion

               Which other molecule is produced following platelet adhesion to the

            How is thromboxane A2 produced?

                 •  This forms the platelet plug
            How do ADP and thromboxane have this effect?
                 • ADP binds to the p2y12 receptor
                 • Thromboxane A2 binds to the thromboxane A2 receptor

              What occurs as a result of this platelet activation by ADP and
               thromboxane A2?
                 •  Platelet activation leads to a change in the conformation of the
                         GlpIIb/IIIa (2b/3a) receptor on platelets
                     ◦  This process is known as “inside out” or “flip-flopping”
              What does the GlpIIb/IIIa receptor do?
                 • The GlpIIb/IIIa receptor provides binding sites for fibrinogen
            How does fibrinogen then act to progress primary haemostasis?
                 •   It binds to the GlpIIb/IIIb receptor and this leads to “outside-in”
                       signalling which allows platelets to link together and form the
                       platelet plug

              What is another role of thromboxane A2, other than activating
                   platelets?
                 •   It acts as a vasoconstrictor, and this promotes the formation of
                       platelets in the initial response to blood vessel injury.

              What is prostacyclin?
                 •   It is a prostaglandin that is used as a vasodilator in response to
                     the dissolution of blood clots, inhibiting platelet aggregation
                     ◦  PGI2 is used in endothelial cells
            How is prostacyclin produced?
                 • By endothelial cells from arachidonic acid
                 •  This makes sense as endothelial cells will be present when the
                    blood vessel isn’t damaged and conditions need to return back to
                     normal.

              What is the difference between a prostacyclin and a prostaglandin?
                 • A prostacyclin is a type of prostaglandin that has vasodilatory
                      effects
                     ◦  i.e thromboxane A2 is also a type of prostaglandin, but this
                       has vasoconstrictory effects

            How does aspirin work?
                 •   It irreversibly blocks the action of Cyclo-Oxygenase (COx)
                   which is a vital component in the production of thromboxane A2
                 •  This means that thromoboxane A2 cannot bind to the
                  thromboxane A2 receptor and cannot activate platelets as it
                     normally does, reducing the formation of blood clots.

            Why does aspirin not inhibit the action of prostacyclin, since it also
               needs COx for its synthesis?
                 •  Endothelial cells are able to produce COx on their own (due to
                 them having nuclei), meaning that they are not as affected by
                     the action of aspirin
                 •  Overall there is hence a net decrease in platelet aggregation
                 and the formation of blood clots.

            How does clopidogrel work?
                 •   It works by preventing ADP from binding to its p2y12 receptor
                 •  Similarly, this prevents the activation of platelets and the
                       positive feedback loop is not achieved to recruit more and more
                       platelets to the site of vessel damage

        Coagulation

              What happens in coagulation?
                 •  Thrombin is generated, and this converts fibrinogen to fibrin,
                  used to stabilise the platelet plug.





            Why is coagulation required?
                 •  Coagulation is required to stabilise the platelet plug in larger
                   blood vessels
                 •  In smaller vessels, the unstable platelet plug is sufficient, but
                     not in larger ones.

              What are clotting factors?
                 •  Clotting/coagulation factors are proteins in the blood that help
                  form blood clots to stop bleeding when there is injury to blood
                     vessels

              What are the three sites where clotting factors are produced?
                 •  Liver
                 •  Endothelial cells - von Willebrand factor.
                 •  Megakaryocytes (platelet progenitors)
              Where are most clotting factors made?
                 •  Liver
              Where is von Willebrand factor made?
                 •  Endothelial cells and megakaryocytes
              What is Factor II?
                 •  Prothrombin.

               Which factors are involved in initiation?
                 •  VIIa
                 •  IX/IXa
                 •  X/Xa
                 •   II/IIa
              What does Factor VIIa do in initiation?
                 •  Factor VIIa binds to Tissue Factor (TF) to initiate the cascade of
                     reactions in initiation

              What does Factor IX/IXa do in initiation?
                 •  Factor IX turns into Factor IXa after being activated by factor VIIa
                     binding to Tissue Factor (TF)
                     ◦   It does not activate Factor Xa yet, as Factor VIIIa is required
                             for this
                     ◦  Factor IXa is hence produced in preparation for it being
                          required in amplification

              What does Factor X/Xa do in initiation?
                 •  Factor X turns into Factor Xa after being activated by factor VIIa
                     binding to Tissue Factor (TF)
                 •  Factor Xa then converts Factor II into Factor IIa to produce a
                             little bit of thrombin
                     ◦  Factor Va is not needed for this initiation
              What does Factor II/IIa do in initiation?
                 •  Factor II (prothrombin) is converted to Factor IIa (thrombin) in
                           initiation by action of Factor Xa
                 •  This small amount of thrombin produced by initiation can start
                    the amplification phase

              What happens in the amplification phase?
                 •  Thrombin produced in propagation activates cofactors 5→5a
                 and 8→8a as well as factor 11→11a and platelets
                 •  Factor 11 is used to activate Factor 9 to 9a which alongside
                    Factor 8 can be used to generate 5 times more Factor 10a from
                      Factor 10
                 •  This amplifies thrombin production from prothrombin by ~10^5
                      leading to rapid fibrinogen cleavage into fibrin
                 •  This stabilises the unstable platelet plug to form an insoluble
                         fibrin clot





               Which factors are involved in amplification?
                 •  Factor IIa (thrombin)
                 •  Factor V/Va
                 •  Factor VIII/VIIIa
                 •  Factor IXa
                 •  Factor Xa
                 •  Factor XI/XIa
              What does Factor IIa do in amplification?
                 •  Factor IIa, produced in small amounts during initiation, is used
                     during amplification to activate Factor V→Va, Factor VIII→VIIIa,
                    Factor XI→XIa and platelets

                 Describe the importance of Factor VIIIa in coagulation.

              What characteristic of platelets allows them to accelerate these

                     ◦   It is believed that clotting factors work on the phospholipid

                  Explain how the protein C pathway works to inhibit coagulation.
                 •  Thrombin binds to thrombomodulin (endothelial cell receptor
                       that helps anticoagulation)
                 •  This activates Protein C
                 •  Protein C inactivates Factors Va and VIIIa in the presence of
                     Protein S
                 •  This means Factor II cannot be converted to Factor IIa any
                      longer and thrombin is no longer produced from prothrombin
                     ◦  This is because Factor Va and VIIIa help Factor Xa activate
                          Factor II to Factor IIa





                 •  Clotting hence reduces as thrombin cannot do fibrinogen → fibrin
              What is the direct role of protein C in the inhibition of coagulation?
                 •  To inactivate Factors Va and VIIIa which prevents the activation
                      of Factor II (prothrombin) to Factor IIa (thrombin)

              What is heparin?
                 • An anticoagulant drug that works as a potentiator of
                    antithrombin

            How does heparin work?
                 •   It potentiates the action of antithrombin which leads to the
                      direct inactivation of Factors Xa and IIa.
                 •   It does this by its pentasaccharide chain binding to
                     antithrombin, altering antithrombin’s conformation to make it
                   have a greater affinity for Factor Xa
                 •  Inactivation of Factors Xa and IIa both directly lead to no
                   thrombin being produced and no further clotting

                      Is heparin an indirect or direct inhibitor?
                 •  Indirect
                 •   It potentiates the action of antithrombin, which is a direct
                           inhibitor. So heparin itself is indirect

            A heparin chain is only 4 units long. What effect will this have on its
                  function?
                 •  Heparin can function to inactivate Factor Xa even when it is short,
                    but to inactivate Factor IIa, it needs to be longer
                 • The chain being short will lead to it inactivating Factor Xa, but
                    not Factor IIa

            How do longer chains of heparin work?
                 •  Longer chains (pentasaccharides) of heparin can wrap around
                    antithrombin and thrombin and inactivate both Factors IIa and
                 Xa

            How is heparin administered?
                 •  Intravenously or by subcutaneous injection
              What is warfarin?
                 • An anticoagulant drug that works as a Vitamin K antagonist
            How does warfarin work?
                 •   It works by competing with Vitamin K to interfere with protein
                      carboxylation
                 •  This prevents the synthesis of Factors II, VII, IX and X by the
                          liver
                 •  This reduces the synthesis of coagulation factors and hence
                   reduced clotting initiation, activation and propagation

            How is warfarin administered?
                 •  Orally
                 •   It takes several days to take effect
            Why does warfarin take several days to have its effect?
                 •  Warfarin works by antagonising Vitamin K which prevents
                      synthesis of new clotting factors
                 •  This means existing clotting factors will still be able to function
                           until they run out, at which time the effects of Warfarin will be
                         visible

          Fibrinolytic System and Tests of Coagulation

              What is plasmin?
                 • The principle fibrinolytic enzyme
                 •   It is not specific to fibrin, so it can break down other plasma

            How is plasmin activated?

            How does plasminogen bind to fibrin?
                 •  Plasminogen binds to the lysine residues of fibrin
