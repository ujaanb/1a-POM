           What are the two essential characteristics of haematopoietic stem cells?
             •  They self-renew (they continue to increase in number after usage to
                 avoid depletion)
                 ◦  i.e some daughter HSCs do not differentiate, and instead they
                    remain as HSCs to avoid depletion in numbers
             •  They can differentiate to form any blood daughter cell through
                    different paths

           Where does haematopoiesis occur in foetuses?
             • Mesoderm (middle layer of the three germ layers) (<3 weeks old)
             •  Liver takes over from 6-8 weeks
             • Bone marrow takes over from 10 weeks onwards til adulthood





           Where does haematopoiesis occur in children?
             •  Mainly in the bone marrow
           Where does haemopoeisis occur in adults?
             •  Mainly in the bone marrow
             •  Specifically the pelvis, vertebrae and sternum.
           What is the difference between the sites of haematopoiesis in adults and
              children?
             •  In children, haematopoiesis occurs in all bone marrow
             •  In adults, haematopoiesis occurs in the bone marrow found at the
                   pelvis, vertebrae and sternum

           What is a mesenchymal cell?
             • Mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs) are connective tissue cells that
               have the ability to self-renew and also exhibit multilineage
                   differentiation.
                 ◦  i.e haematopoietic stem cells

           What causes leukaemia or bone marrow failure in haemopoeisis?
             • When the regulation usually performed by growth factors/transcription
                  factors is disrupted, the balance between proliferation and
                   differentiation is upset
                 ◦  This means there may be an abnormal amount of blood cells
           What are haemopoeitic growth factors?
             •  They are glycoprotein hormones which bind to cell surface receptors
               and regulate HSCs
                 ◦  They do this by inducing an intracellular cascade of reactions
                       that leads to altered gene expression by transcription factors

           What is the function of haemopoietic growth factors?
             •  They are involved in the regulation of proliferation and differentiation
                 of HSCs which is key to the function of a mature blood cell
                 ◦  i.e determine which type of blood cell is eventually formed, and
                       rate of proliferation
             •   If this is not regulated, it could lead to leukaemias and bone marrow
                    failures

           Which growth factor is used to regulate proliferation of red blood cells?
             •  Erythropoeitin (EPO)
           Which growth factor is used to regulate proliferation of platelets?
             •  Thrombopoeitin (TPO)
           Which growth factor is used to regulate proliferation of granulocytes?
             •  Granulocyte colony stimulating factor (G-CSF)
             •  Granulocyte-macrophage colony stimulating factor (G-M CSF)
           What is a progenitor in differentiation?
             • A cell from which a variety of daughter cells can arise depending on
                 the paths taken.

           What are the two main progenitors that stem to form haemopoeitic cells?
             • Lymphoid cell progenitors
             •  Myeloid cell progenitors

              Recall two structural characteristics of reticulocytes.
             •  They have a larger ribosomal size, and hence the RNA content is
                higher in them (picked up by methylene blue stain)
                 ◦  This is because the organelles have not disappeared yet in a
                       reticulocyte, but they should have disappeared in a fully
                      differentiated cell
                 ◦ RNA content is higher due to ribosomal RNA (rRNA)
             • The cells themselves are also larger than normal red blood cells.
           What is a polychromatic red blood cell?
             •  This is an immature red blood cell (reticulocyte) that is much larger
                than usual and is characterised by its blue/gray/purple tint colour on
               a blood film
             • The nucleus:cytoplasmic ratio may also be larger, as a result of the
                immaturity of the cell





              Recall the differentiation path that a red blood cell takes from HSC to
              erythrocyte.
             • HSC —> Myeloid progenitor —> Proerythroblast —> Erythroblast —>
                Erythrocyte

           What is anaemia?
             •  Anaemia is a reduced concentration of haemoglobin in a given
               volume of blood.
             •   It is usually caused by lower rates of red blood cell production, which
                 leads to the above effect
             •  This can mean oxygen is not transported around the body as
                   efficiently as there are less binding sites

           What are the three mechanisms through which anaemia is caused?
             •  Increased destruction of erythrocytes
             • Reduced production of erythrocytes
                 ◦  Both will eventually lead to a decrease in haemoglobin
                     concentrations.
             •  Increased blood loss (i.e menstrual/gastrointestinal bleeding)

             Deficiency of which three molecules can lead to anaemia?
             •  Iron
             •  Vitamin B12
             •  Folic acid (folate)

           What are the two main causes of microcytic anaemia?
             •  Iron deficiencies
             •  Decreased iron availability
              Recall three causes of iron deficiency.
             •  Increased blood loss (i.e menstruation, gastrointestinal bleeding,
               hookworm)
             • Reduced dietary intake
             •  Increased iron requirements elsewhere in the body
                 ◦  i.e pregnancies, young children who are growing
           What is meant by anaemia caused by decreased iron availability?
             •   It means the patients are not iron deficient, but their RBCs are
                unable to access the iron properly
                 ◦  This is associated with chronic diseases and inflammation
           What is often seen in the blood films of patients with iron
             deficiencies/reduced iron availability other than microcytosis?
             •  Alongside the microcytic properties, the red blood cells are also
               hypochromic due to the reduction in haemoglobin (red pigment)
                production

           What is the main cause of macrocytic anaemia and what does this lead to in
           RBCs?
             •  B12/folic acid deficiencies
                 ◦  This is because B12 and folic acid are required for thymidine
                 and hence DNA production and so RBCs are unable to divide
                      properly
             •  This is megaloblastic anaemia
           Where is erythropoietin (EPO) produced?
             • By the cortical cells of the kidney
          When is erythropoietin produced, and how is this effective?
             •  In response to hypoxia (low oxygen levels) or anaemia
             •  This leads to a good demand-supply feedback loop as it will in turn
                 lead to increased haemoglobin presence in the blood, which can
                 transport oxygen to where it is needed.





           What are the two major functions of iron in the body?
             • Oxygen transport in haemoglobin (binds to oxygen using Fe)
             •  Production of mitochondrial proteins
                 ◦  i.e cytochromes a, b and c in electron transport chain
                 ◦  cytochrome P450 in hydroxylation reactions for drug metabolism
             Describe the difference between ferric and ferrous iron.
             •  Ferrous iron is in the Fe2+ state and is derived from animal meat
             •  Ferric iron is in the Fe3+ state and is found in other foods
         Why is ferric iron converted to ferrous iron and what is required for this
            process to occur?
             •  Ferrous iron is more easily absorbed in the duodenum
             •  Vitamin C can be used to convert ferric iron to ferrous iron

         Why is ferrous iron better absorbed than ferric iron?
             •  Iron in haem is in the ferrous state (Fe2+) and haem iron is the best
               absorbed form
             • Fe3+ iron (ferric) is not found in haem and so is not well absorbed
           Where is iron absorbed in the body?
             • The duodenum (inside small intestine)
         How much iron is normally absorbed in the body?
             •  1-2 mg per day
                 ◦ The normal Western diet contains 10-20 mg of iron per day
           What is ferritin?
             •  Ferritin is a protein inside cells that stores iron.
           What is the name of the molecule used to regulate iron absorption in the
           body?
             •  Hepcidin
           What is hepcidin?
             •   It is a protein that is used to regulate iron absorption by small
                  intestine and release by macrophages

           What effect does hepcidin have?
             •  Hepcidin, when upregulated, works to inhibit further iron absorption
                 to the blood

          When is hepcidin upregulated/downregulated?
             • When ferritin (iron) stores are full, hepcidin is upregulated in the
                        liver. This decreases absorption of iron into the blood.
             •  But when iron is needed (i.e for erythropoeisis), there is
                downregulation of hepcidin and iron absorption is increased.

             Describe the mechanism through which hepcidin works to inhibit iron
              absorption.
             •  Hepcidin binds to its receptor ferroportin, which prevents the efflux
                 of iron from duodenal enterocytes (intestinal cells) into the blood
                    after absorption from the small intestine
                 ◦ The enterocytes eventually die and the iron is lost into the gut
                  lumen and not absorbed into the blood

           What happens to hepcidin production in states of inflammation and what
            can this lead to in some sick patients?

                     production
             •  In sick patients such as those with chronic diseases where
                 inflammation is prolonged, they can suffer from anaemia of
                inflammation/anaemia of chronic disease as a result of this hepcidin
                  upregulation causing reduced iron availability

           Where is folic acid absorbed in the body?
             •  In the duodenum (small intestine)
         How is vitamin B12 absorbed in the body?
             •  Vitamin B12 is cleaved by hydrochloric acid in the stomach
             •  This vitamin B12 binds to Intrinsic Factor to form B12-IF
             •  B12-IF travels to the ileum (final portion of the small intestine) where
                            it is finally absorbed

         How else can vitamin B12 deficiency arise?
             •  Lack of intrinsic factor (IF) secretion - pernicious anaemia
             •  Malabsorption of B12-IF in ileum caused by coeliac disease/surgery
             •  Lack of acid in the stomach
                 ◦  This means B12 cannot be cleaved
         How and where are red blood cells destroyed?
             • By phagocytic cells (macrophages) in the spleen.
           What happens to the iron when red blood cells are destroyed?
             •   It is recycled as it returns to the bone marrow.
         How long is the regular erythrocyte lifespan?
             •  120 days.
           What three things can cause impaired erythrocyte function?
             •  Impaired membrane integrity (shape of erythrocyte and ability to
                 contain cell contents)
             •  Haemoglobin structure and function (to carry oxygen)
             •  Cellular metabolism (RBCs need ATP)
           What is haemolysis?
             • The destruction (lysis) of blood cells.
                 ◦   It can arise due to the blood cell being dysfunctional
           What is the shape of an erythrocyte, and how does this help it perform its
              function?
             •  Biconcave
             •  This helps it to manouevre its way through narrow blood vessels
           What property of the erythrocyte allows it to maintain this shape?
             •  Cytoskeleton

           What are spherocytes, and how do they come about?
             •  They are cells that are approximately spherical in shape
             •  They come about by the loss of cell membrane on an erythrocyte
               caused by disruption in the vertical linkages of the membrane
             •  This means less of the cell membrane is able to wrap around the
                contents of the cell, and the wrapping becomes far tighter, causing
                 the cell to become smaller and rounder.
                 ◦  There is loss of cell membrane without the loss of an equivalent
                  amount of cytoplasm so the cell is forced to round up
             •  They come about in hereditary spherocytosis (autosomal dominant)
           What do spherocytes look like on a blood film?
             •  Consequently, the cells lack central pallor as the cytoplasm takes up
              more of the area of the cell due to the tight packing of cellular
                contents





           What is the fate of spherocytes?
             •  Haemolysis
                 ◦  They are prematurely removed by the spleen.

                 erythrocyte caused by disruption in the vertical linkages of the
             membrane
             •  Elliptocytes come about by the loss of cell membrane on an
                 erythrocyte caused by disruption in the horizontal linkages of the
             membrane
