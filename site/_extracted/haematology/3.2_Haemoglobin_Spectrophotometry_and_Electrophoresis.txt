           What is the purpose of electrophoresis?

         How many RBCs are typically in one litre of blood?
             •  3.5 x 10^12
                 ◦  Each RBC has about 640 million haemoglobin molecules
           What is the function of a haem group in haemoglobin?
             •  To carry oxygen.
           What does a haem group in haemoglobin contain?
             • Fe2+ surrounded by a porphyrin ring.





         How does haemoglobin aid in the transportation of oxygen?
             • The Fe2+ of the haem group can bind to oxygen and form
               oxyhaemoglobin
             • The O2 is then transported from the lung to whichever tissue requires it
             •   It detaches from the Fe2+ and deoxyhaemoglobin is reformed.

             Describe how the protein (globin) part of a haemoglobin molecule is
              structured.
             •   It usually forms using four of eight possible functional globin chains
             •  These chains are arranged in “clusters” based on the genes involved in
                 the coding.
             •  These are alpha clusters and beta clusters
           Which globin chains are used to form haemoglobin A (normal adult
            haemoglobin)?
             • 2 alpha and 2 beta subunits





           What is haemoglobin A2?
             • A form of adult haemoglobin that is typically found in higher
              amounts in sufferers of beta-thalassaemia
                 ◦  All of us have haemoglobin A2, just in lower amounts
           Which globin chains are used to form haemoglobin A2 (adult haemoglobin)?
             • 2 alpha and 2 delta chains




           Which globin chains are used to form haemoglobin F (foetal haemoglobin)?
             • 2 alpha and 2 gamma subunits.
                 ◦ “gamma” sounds like “mama” which is what babies say





           What is myoglobin?
             • An oxygen-binding protein found in muscle cells
          When referring to oxygen and haemoglobin binding to each other, what
           does P50 mean?
             • The partial pressure value at which 50% of haem groups are able to
              be bound to oxygen.
                 ◦  Partial pressure is often measured in torr

           Which of myoglobin and haemoglobin have a higher affinity for oxygen and
          how can we use P50 to determine this?
             •  Myoglobin (the sigmoid curve has shift to the left)
             • The P50 value of myoglobin is much lower than than of
                haemoglobin. This means a higher proportion of myoglobin binds to
               oxygen at lower partial pressures.
                 ◦  However, it also means myoglobin is poor at releasing oxygen
                    under these lower partial pressures





             Describe the mechanism through which haemoglobin is able to bind with
            oxygen and release it relevantly.
             •  Pressure is very high (~ 100 torr) in the lungs, and at these levels,
               oxygen is very easily saturated (bound) by haemoglobin where oxygen
                      is collected from
             • When it reaches the tissues around the body, the pressure is much
                 lower and so a lot of this oxygen is released to where it is required, but
                not all of it is

             Describe what is meant by “positive cooperativity” when it comes to oxygen
             binding by haemoglobin.
             • When one haem group is able to bind onto oxygen, this makes it
                 easier for the other haem groups to also bind oxygen, until all the
             haem groups are bound/saturated.

           What is 2,3-BPG (2,3-bisphosphoglycerate)?
             •   It is an allosteric effector (allo - other, steros - structure) which
                binds to deoxyhaemoglobin at a site distant from the haem groups
             •   It is found in red blood cells and is used to stabilise the structure and
               reduce the haemoglobin’s oxygen affinity (at lower pressures)

           What is an allosteric effector?
             • A component which can bind to another component to alter the latter’s
                  structure whilst having a functional impact on the latter component.

             Describe the importance of 2,3-BPG (2,3-bisphosphoglycerate).
             •   It is important because without it, oxygen affinity at the lower
                pressures found in the tissues would be too high, and so oxygen
                detaching from the structure would be a difficult process.

         How does haemoglobin F help with this maternal-foetal oxygen exchange?
             •   It has a gamma chain which has an amino acid alteration compared to
                 the beta chain present in haemoglobin A
             •  This means that it has a lower affinity for 2,3-BPG than haemoglobin
            A
                 ◦  Since 2,3-BPG is an allosteric effector that decreases
                   haemoglobin’s affinity for oxygen, a lower affinity from HbF for
                   2,3-BPG means it has a higher O2 affinity
             •  This aids with the uptake of oxygen from maternal RBCs.





           What is the Bohr effect?
             •   It describes how H+ and CO2 (products of lactate regeneration and
                 pyruvate decarboxylation) can act as allosteric effectors by binding
                 to oxyhaemoglobin
             •  They do this by decreasing the affinity of oxyhaemoglobin for
               oxygen and aiding greater release into tissues at lower pressures

             Describe the mutation that causes the erythrocyte abnormality seen in
               sickle cell anaemia.
             •  Glutamate is altered to valine at position 6 in the haemoglobin
                 polypeptide sequence.
             •  Glutamate is a negatively charged (hydrophilic) amino acid but valine is
                  neutral
             •  This means that the haemoglobin chain in non-sufferers is more
                 negative than that in sickle cell anaemia sufferers

             Describe how electrophoresis can help us to discover this abnormality.
             •  Negatively charged specimen (i.e normal HbA) will move to the positive
                 electrode in electrophoresis and vice-versa
             • Once we notice that a sample of haemoglobin does not move to the
                   positive electrode as much as it should (i.e HbS which stays in middle),
             we can begin to suspect an alteration in the amino acid sequence
                which has affected the polarity of the protein.

               In electrophoresis of haemoglobin samples, what is the medium we used?
             •  Cellulose acetate.
           What is the pH of the buffer solution and why?
             •  9.5.
             • We use this pH because the haemoglobin has an overall negative
                charge in these conditions and so migrates to the anode.

           Which surface of the glossy and matt filter paper should you apply solution

             •  Matt surface
           What was the purpose of our first spectrophotometry experiment where we
           measured the absorbances of oxyhaemoglobin at different wavelengths?
             •  To identify the wavelength which gives us the highest absorbance
               and hence would be best to use when analysing oxyhaemoglobin
                concentrations for the next experiment

              Explain what we did in our second experiment to determine the
            oxyhaemoglobin concentrations of two unknown solutions.
             • We used a variation of known concentrations of haemoglobin
                  solution and measured their absorbances
                 ◦  i.e 0.2mg/ml, 0.4mg/ml, 0.6mg/ml, 0.8mg/ml, 1.0mg/ml
             •  This was done to create a standard curve of these known
               oxyhaemoglobin concentrations (x-axis) against absorbance (y-
                  axis)
             • We then got the two solutions of unknown oxyhaemoglobin
                concentration, measured their absorbances and using the standard
                curve we could determine their oxyhaemoglobin concentrations

              Explain how spectrophotometry can be used in the study of proteins.
             •  Studying the absorbance of different proteins can help us to
               understand their concentrations (using the Beer-Lambert Law)
             •  Understanding the concentrations of substances in non-suffering
                   individuals and comparing them to those in sufferers, can help us to
                understand abnormalities in different clinical contexts.

           What is the Beer-Lambert Law?
             •   It explains how the absorbance of a solution is directly proportional
                 to the concentration of the absorbing material within it and to the
                distance (or path length) travelled by the light through the sample.

                                                   ℓ             •   It describes the equation A = E × c ×
             • A is the absorbance of the solution
             • c is the concentration of the absorbing substance (typically in mol/L)
                 ℓ is the path length (in cm)             •
             • E is the extinction coefficient, a constant for the substance being
               measured at that particular wavelength.

           What is a pulse oximeter used for?
             •  To measure oxygen saturation (what proportion of haemoglobin is
              bound to oxygen)
             • Normal oxygen saturation is between 96% and 99%
                 ◦   If there is low oxygen saturation (<94%), this means more
                   haemoglobin is unbound to oxygen and this could be indicative
                      of hypoxaemia (low blood oxygen)

         Why are low levels of carbon monoxide so dangerous to the body?
             •  Haemoglobin has a 200-fold greater affinity for carbon monoxide
                 than oxygen
             •  This means it can readily outcompete the oxygen for binding to the
             haem groups of haemoglobin
             •  This can lead to insufficient oxygen being delivered to cells and tissues
                around the body, and hence death
                 ◦  Levels of 0.2% carbon monoxide can lead to death within an hour
                      or two.

           What is methaemoglobin (MetHb)?
             •  This is a form of haemoglobin that contains the oxidised, ferric form
                 of iron (Fe3+) instead of the normal ferrous Fe2+
                 ◦   It is generated when this Fe2+ is oxidised to Fe3+
           What are the clinical consequences of having methaemoglobin and why?
             • Fe3+ binds to oxygen more strongly than Fe2+
             •  This means that methaemoglobin’s (Fe3+) affinity for oxygen is
             much higher, even at lower partial pressures
             •  This is impaired oxygen binding, and can lead to not enough oxygen
