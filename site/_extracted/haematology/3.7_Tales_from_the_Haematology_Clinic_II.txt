           What is the underlying cause of all leukaemias?
             •  Leukaemia results from a number of mutations occurring in a
                   primitive (not fully developed) cell that, as a result, has a growth or
                  survival advantage over normal cells that have not undergone
                mutation
             •  That single cell gives rise to a clone that steadily replaces normal
                   cells
             • The abnormal behaviour of the leukaemic clone can include growth
                 that occurs without a dependence on growth factors, continued
                   proliferation without maturation, and a failure to undergo normal
                    cell death (apoptosis).

           What happens to other haematopoietic cells in leukaemias?
             • When a patient has leukaemia, abnormal immature white blood
                   cells (called blasts) multiply uncontrollably, filling up the bone marrow
             •  This prevents production of other cells important for survival, namely
                 red blood cells and platelets leading to infections (where there is lack
                 of neutrophils), anaemia (where there is lack of erythrocytes) and
               abnormal bleeding (lack of platelets).
             •   It can also lead to increased bruising (due to lack of platelets) and
              bone marrow pain (due to expansion)

               In medicine, what is the difference between acute and chronic?
             •  Acute refers to something that happens quickly over a short period of
                time
             •  Chronic refers to something that happens slowly over a longer period
                 of time

           Which of the four leukaemia types is most common in babies?
             •  Acute Lymphoblastic Leukaemia (ALL)
                 ◦  This is because there is an increased chance that the mutation
                    behind the proliferative disorder seen in the leukaemia occurred
                     before birth

           What is a smudge cell?
             • A squashed, mature lymphocyte





           Which diagnostic technique may be used to confirm the type of leukaemia
             diagnosis?
             •  Flow cytometry
                 ◦  To distinguish between T and B cell leukaemias
             •  Cytogenetic/molecular analysis for prognoses
                 ◦  To help make treatment plans for individuals
             • Bone marrow aspirate
               In diagnosis of which leukaemias can flow cytometry be extremely useful
           and why?
             •  Acute/chronic lymphoblastic/lymphocytic leukaemias
             •  This can be really helpful because we can run the cells through the
                cytometer to determine if T cells or B cells are behind the mutation

         Why might both intrathecal and intravenous systemic chemotherapy be
             required to treat leukaemia?
             •  Lymphoblasts may infiltrate into the Central Nervous System (CNS),
               where they are protected from the systemic chemotherapy (known
                as a ‘Sanctuary’ site)
                 ◦  Intrathecal chemotherapy will turn this protected sanctuary site
                        into an unprotected one

           What is hepatomegaly?
             •  Enlargement of the liver.
           What does it mean to be febrile?
             •  To show signs of having a fever

         How is chronic lymphocytic leukaemia (CLL) staged?
             • CLL is staged according to the degree of lymph node/liver/spleen
                 involvement, and whether Hb and platelet count are reduced (in
              more advanced disease)

           What adaptations do lymphoblasts have that can allow them to escape
             treatment methods of leukaemias?
             •  They infiltrate into the CNS where they are protected from systemic
               chemotherapy.
                 ◦  Intrathecal chemotherapy can be used to combat this
            Diagram showing neutrophil precursors.





         How can you tell the difference between acute lymphoblastic and acute
             myeloid leukaemia?
             •  In acute myeloid leukaemia (AML), the blast cells should have
                granules due to them being granulocytes
             •  In acute lymphoblastic leukaemia (ALL), the blast cells should not
               have granules

         How can antigenic stimulation be related to acute lymphoblastic leukaemia
             (ALL)?
             •  Antigenic stimulation normally leads to rearrangement of DNA in
                lymphocytes so that antibodies of greater affinity are produced
                 ◦  Affinity maturation
             •   If this process goes wrong, a lymphoid stem cell may acquire a
                malignant phenotype.

           What is toxic granulation?
             • The presence of large purple or dark blue cytoplasmic granules in
                 neutrophils, (bands and metamyelocytes - precursors)

           What is toxic granulation a sign of?
             •  Usually a bacterial infection or inflammation
                 ◦  i.e neutrophilia
           What can scalloped (curved) margins and ‘hugging’ of the surrounding red
            blood cells by lymphocytes indicate?
             •  Infectious mononucleosis (mono)
             •  This is caused by Epstein-Barr virus.





           What is acute myeloid leukaemia (AML)?
             • A leukaemia that results from the increase in myeloblasts and other
                granulocyte precursors

           Which leukocyte type is increased in number due to bacterial infections?
             •  Neutrophils and monocytes
           Which leukocyte type is increased in number due to viral infections?
             •  Lymphocytes
           Which leukocyte type is increased in number due to parasitic infections?
             •  Eosinophils
           What is the difference between acute and chronic leukaemias?
             •  Acute leukaemia results from a failure earlier in the life cycle of a
                leukocyte (many blasts)
                 ◦ The leukocytes are typically immature. They are unable to
                   develop fully into a mature state, but retain their proliferative
                    capacity whilst being dysfunctional
             •  Chronic leukaemia results from a failure in the life cycle after the
                   cells have matured (not many blasts)
                 ◦ The leukocytes are typically mature. But communication with
                     other cells is impaired

           What is the difference between chronic myeloid leukaemia (CML) and
             chronic lymphocytic leukaemias (CLL) in terms of cell functionality?
             •  In CML, the mature end cells are still able to function
             •  In CLL, the lymphocytes are functionally useless and there is a loss
                 of normal immune function

           What happens to lymphocytes during viral infection?
             • When lymphocytosis is due to a viral infection, there are often
                   ‘atypical lymphocytes’
             •  For example, the scalloped margins and ‘hugging’ of the
                surrounding red blood cells is a characteristic finding in infectious
               mononucleosis (‘glandular fever’), resulting from Epstein‒Barr virus
                  infection





           What genetic feature is found in chronic myeloid leukaemia (CML)?
             • BCR-ABL1 fusion gene
                 ◦  Its presence can signify CML
                 ◦ NOTE: BCR = B cell receptor
           What does the BCR:ABL1 gene do?
             • The BCR:ABL1 gene encodes a protein with uncontrolled tyrosine
                kinase activity, which gives rise to leukaemic clones
             •  This means that leukocytes can proliferate without the need for
               growth factors

         How can the BCR:ABL1 gene’s effects be inhibited?
             •  Tyrosine kinase inhibitors may cause remission, and potentially
                 cure, of the disease
             •  This is because the BCR:ABL1 gene encodes a protein with
                 uncontrolled tyrosine kinase activity, which gives rise to leukaemic
