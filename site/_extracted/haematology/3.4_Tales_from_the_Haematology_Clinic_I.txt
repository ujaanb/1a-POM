           What is anaemia?
             •  Anaemia is a reduction in the amount of haemoglobin in a given
               volume of blood below what would be expected in comparison with a
                  healthy subject of the same age and gender
             •  Reduction in haemoglobin concentration
           What other visual characteristic is usually associated with microcytic
             erythrocytes?
             •  They are hypochromic (pale)
         Why is this the case?
             • The microcytic cells usually have less haemoglobin and so are less red
                 than normal erythrocytes
             • The central pallor of the microcytic cells is also typically much
                  larger (more than the typical 1/3 of area) and so this leads to a paler
                     cell that has less haemoglobin

           What is a clinical sign seen in patients with low iron?
             •  Fatigue
             •  Dizziness
           What is ferritin?
             •  Ferritin is a protein that is used to store iron
                 ◦   It can be tested for when you want to do a test for iron levels
           What are the three common causes of iron deficiency anaemia?
             •  Increased blood loss
             •  Insufficient iron intake
             •  Increased iron requirements (i.e pregnancy)
         How can low iron be treated?
             •  Iron replacement therapy
                 ◦  i.e Ferrous sulphate tablets

           What is beta-thalassaemia?
             •  Beta-thalassaemia is an autosomal recessive disorder which affects
                the blood

           What happens in beta-thalassaemia?
             •  In normal individuals, the globin protein of haemoglobin consists of
              two alpha and two beta chains
                 ◦  This forms HbA
             •  In individuals with beta-thalassaemia, the globin protein of
                haemoglobin consists of two alpha and two delta chains
                 ◦  This forms HbA2
              Recall two common Full Blood Count characteristics seen in iron deficiency
           and beta-thalassaemia.
             •  Both will cause a decrease in mean cell volume (MCV)
             •  Both will cause a decrease in mean cell haemoglobin (albeit for
                    different reasons)

              Recall three ways you can distinguish between iron deficiency and beta-
             thalassaemia.
             •  Iron deficiency will lead to low ferritin, beta-thalassaemia will not
             •  Iron deficiency will cause lower total Haemoglobin (Hb), beta-
                 thalassaemia will not
             •  Beta-thalassaemia will cause a higher RBC count, iron deficiency will
               cause a lower RBC count
             •  Beta-thalassaemia has HbA2, iron deficiency does not
                 ◦  Alpha-thalassaemia however, has normal HbA

           What is transferrin?
             • A protein produced by the liver that regulates iron absorption into the
                blood

           What causes changes in transferrin levels?
             •  High iron levels indicate low transferrin levels
             • Low iron levels indicate high transferrin levels
           What could cause a defect in haem synthesis?
             •  Iron deficiencies
           What is the name given to the reduced synthesis of globin?
             •  Thalassaemia
             •  Beta-thalassaemia is when you have less beta chains of globin, and so
                haemoglobin synthesis is impaired
                 ◦ The alpha chains then combine with delta chains to compensate
                        for this lack

         How is vitamin B12 absorbed in the body?
             •  Vitamin B12 combines with intrinsic factor made in the gastric parietal
                   cells to form B12-IF
             •  B12-IF then binds to receptors in the ileum
              Recall three causes of vitamin B12 deficiency.
             •  Inadequate intake
             •  Malabsorption (i.e Coeliac disease)
             •  Inadequate secretion of intrinsic factor (pernicious anaemia)
             •  Lack of hydrochloric acid in the stomach (achlorhydria)
           What is a symptom associated with vitamin B12 deficiency?
             •  Pins and needles
             •  Fatigue (seen in all anaemia)
             •  Vitamin B12 and folate is required for the essential functioning of the
                  central nervous system (CNS)

         How do sickle cells form?
             •  They result from the polymerisation of Haemoglobin S, which is less
                  soluble than haemoglobin A
                 ◦  Only happens in deoxyhaemoglobin

         How is haemoglobin S formed?
             •  Haemoglobin S is formed when one or more copies of an abnormal
                 beta-globin chain are inherited

           What is polychromasia?
             •   It is when there is an increased blue ‘tinge’ to an erythrocyte
                 (polychromasia - many colours)
             •   It indicates that the red cell is young and reflects increased
                  erythropoeisis as a result of haemolysis

           What is another special consideration about polychromatic cells?
             • The cells are typically larger than normal red blood cells.
           What are target cells?
             •  Erythrocytes with an accumulation of haemoglobin in the central
                    pallor.
                 ◦  i.e they have a “target” in the middle due to this haemoglobin





             Describe the mutation that occurs in erythrocytes that leads to sickle cell
            anaemia.
             • A negatively charged glutamate residue is replaced by an uncharged
                  valine residue at position 6 on the beta-globin chain
             •  This makes the (haemo)globin more hydrophobic, and hence sticky.
           Where does this mutation occur?
             • On the beta-globin chain
           What two medications do patients with sickle cell disease need to take?
             •  Penicillin V
             •  Folic acid

         Why do people with sickle cell anaemia need to take Penicillin V?
             •  Patients with HbSS are functionally hyposplenic due to infarction of
                 the spleen,
                  usually by the age of 5 years. This puts them at increased risk of
                  infections by encapsulated bacteria.
                 ◦ The spleen is involved in immune defence
             •  To reduce this risk they require vaccination against and to take daily
                 prophylactic (preventative) antibiotics

         Why do people with sickle cell anaemia need to take folic acid?
             •  In haemolytic anaemias such as HbSS the increased red blood cell
                 turnover and
                 production increases the folic acid requirement.
                 ◦  Folic acid is required for erythropoiesis
           What is a megaloblast?
             • An abnormal erythroblast that is larger than normal.
           What is megaloblastic anaemia?
             •  Anaemia that is caused by vitamin B12/folic acid deficiencies
           What is haemolytic anaemia?
             • A condition where red blood cells are destroyed at a much faster
                  rate than they are replaced.

           What is a reticulocyte?
             • An immature blood cell that can develop into a mature one
                  (erythrocyte)
             •  Levels can increase with red cell production, as more erythrocytes are
                being developed.

           What is reticulocytosis and how is it caused?
             •   It is an increased number of reticulocytes
             •   It may be due to increased bleeding or erythrocyte destruction
                 (haemolysis)

           What is a poikilocyte?
             • A red blood cell that has an abnormal shape.
           What is the distinct property of Haemoglobin S that impairs its function?
             •   It has a much lower affinity for oxygen than haemoglobin A

           What are Howell Jolly Bodies?
             • Remnants of DNA from nuclei that haven’t been fully expelled by
                 erythroblasts during their maturation in the bone marrow

             Describe the pathophysiology of sickle cell disease.
             •  Deoxyhaemoglobin is present when O2 has been released from
               haemoglobin to tissues
                 ◦  Gets worse in states of hypoxia, such as exercise
             •  This causes polymerisation of HbSS within red blood cells, leading to
                    “sickle cells” forming
             •  This causes increased intravascular haemolysis and vaso-occlusion
             •  Severe intravascular haemolysis can lead to anaemia, gallstones,
                  jaundice and folic acid deficiency
             •  Vaso-occlusion can lead to infarctions of various tissue such as brain,
                  spleen, kidney, bone, retina and lung





           What effect does hookworm have on sufferers?
             •   It is an infection that feeds on the blood, hence reducing blood levels.
           What is haemoglobin A2?
             •   It is an abnormal form of adult haemoglobin that typically forms in
                   larger amounts in thalassaemia sufferers.

           What is hepcidin?
             • A protein that is used to regulate iron levels in the body.

          When are hepcidin levels increased/reduced?
             •  Increases in iron levels in the plasma and iron storage stimulate the
                production and release of hepcidin from hepatocytes
             •  This blocks further iron absorption from the diet and its further
                storage in the small intestine
             • When there is a need for erythropoiesis, hepcidin levels are reduced
               and iron absorption is increased into these cells.

             Describe the mechanism through which hepcidin works to inhibit iron
              absorption.
             •  Hepcidin binds to its receptor ferroportin, which prevents the efflux
                 of iron from duodenal enterocytes (intestinal cells) into the blood
                    after absorption from the small intestine
                 ◦ The enterocytes eventually die and the iron is lost into the gut
                  lumen and not absorbed into the blood

         How many genes do we have that code for alpha globin?
             •  Four
               In alpha thalassaemia, what is the silent carrier state?
             • An individual with one abnormal alpha globin gene is said to be a
                   silent carrier of alpha thalassemia
             •  They will typically not have any health problems

               In alpha thalassaemia, what is the thalassaemia trait?
             • An individual with two abnormal alpha globin genes is said to have
                thalassaemia trait
             •  They can either have the cis trait, on the same chromosome, or the
                  trans trait, on different chromosomes





               In alpha thalassaemia, what is Haemoglobin H disease?
             • An individual with three abnormal alpha globin genes is said to have
               Haemoglobin H disease
             •  This can result in splenomegaly, bone deformities and fatigue

           What are the two types of beta thalassaemia?
             •  Beta thalassaemia trait/minor where no real health problems are
                experienced
             •  Beta thalassaemia major (Cooley’s anaemia) where the lack of beta-
                  globin causes a life-threatening anaemia and haemoglobin function is
                  greatly impaired





           What is haemoglobin E trait?
             •  This is a mutant form of beta-globin
