    2.9 Extracellular Matrix

           What is the extracellular matrix (ECM)?
             • A complex network of carbohydrates and proteins that fill up the
               spaces between cells.
             •   It’s made up of fibrillar and non-fibrillar components
           What does the term “fibrillar” mean?
             •  Fibre-making.
              Recall three roles of the ECM.
             •  Provides physical support for cells
             •  Influences the growth and adhesion of cells as well as their
                    differentiation status
             •  Development, tissue function and organogenesis
              Recall three types of tissue that fall under the umbrella term connective
               tissue.
             • Bone
             •  Cartilage
             •  Fat
             •  Blood
             •  Lymphatic tissue.
             •  Tendon
             •  Skin
           Which collagen types are fibrillar?
             •  Types I, II and III
           Which collagen types are non-fibrillar?
             •  Type IV
           What is collagen IV used for?
             •  To constitute the basement membrane
           What constitutes the connective tissue?
             •  Extracellular matrix
             • Component cells (i.e macrophages, epithelial cells etc.)
             •  Fluid

             Describe the basic structure of a collagen molecule.
             •   It is made up of three alpha-chains which form a triple helix
             • The alpha chains can either be different (Type I) or all the same (Type
                                II, Type III)

           Which three chains comprise a Type I Collagen fibre?
             •  [a1(I)]2 [a2(I)]
           Which three chains comprise a Type II Collagen fibre?
             •  [a1(II)]3
           Which three chains comprise a Type III Collagen fibre?
             •  [a1(III)]3
           What is a heterotrimeric collagen?
             • A collagen molecule made up of one or more types of alpha chains
             •  Type I is an example.
           What is a homotrimeric collagen?
             • A collagen molecule made up of one type of alpha chain
             •  Type II and Type III are examples.
             Describe the structure of an alpha chain in collagen.
             •   It is made up of repeating glycine-x-y units
             •  Typically the x and y are proline and hydroxyproline

         Why is glycine repeated every three monomers in an alpha chain?
             •   It is the simplest amino acid (H chain as the R group)
             •  This means it is also the smallest and can pack tightly inwards in the
                   inside of the triple helix
             • The collagen needs to be compact so this is a helpful property.
             Describe the levels of assembly of collagen fibres from smallest level to
             collagen fibre.
             •  Three alpha chains (of same or different type, depending on the type
                  of collagen being made) are assembled together and condensed to
                form a collagen fibril
             •  Collagen fibrils are cross-linked to form collagen fibres which add
                    tensile strength to the structure

           What is a post-transcriptional modification made to collagen fibres that will
           be secreted by cells?
             •  N-termini and C-termini (non-collagenous domains/propeptides) are
               removed
             •  This is the case for Type I,II and III collagens, those that remain in the
                    extracellular matrix

             Describe the steps involved in collagen fibre biosynthesis.
             • The collagen pro-alpha-chain is synthesised within the ribosome
                   (protein synthesis)
             • The alpha-chain undergoes hydroxylation of selected proline and
                  lysine residues
             • The alpha-chain undergoes glycosylation of selected hydroxylysine
                 residues
             • The pro-alpha chain combines together with other pro-alpha-chains
                  to form a triple helix (procollagen)
             • The procollagen migrates to the cytoplasm and packaged into a
                  vesicle
             • The triple helix (procollagen) is secreted via exocytosis
             • The procollagen undergoes cleavage of the N and C (termini)
                propeptides (non-collagenous)
             • The triple helices condense together to form collagen fibrils
             • The collagen fibrils combine together to form collagen fibres





           What happens to our collagen fibres as we age?
             • The extent of cross-linking reduces and hence the strength of the
                 collagen and tissues decreases

           Which molecules are involved in the cross-linking of collagen fibres?
             •  Proline, hydroxyproline, lysine and hydroxylysine.

           What is the importance of the hydroxylation of proline and lysine in collagen
            assembly?
             •  To allow them to form hydrogen bonds with each other in cross-
                   linking

           What is a hydroxylase?
             • An enzyme that is used to hydroxylate a molecule
              Recall the two cofactors required by proline and lysine hydroxylases to
             hydroxylate proline and lysine residues of procollagen.
             •  Vitamin C and Fe2+ are both cofactors which are heavily involved in
                 the action of hydroxylases

           What can a lack of vitamin C hence result in?
             • A lack of vitamin C will hence lead to a lack of hydroxylation of
                  proline and lysine which means cross-linking between collagen
                  fibres cannot occur
             •  This leads to weakened tissue stability as seen in scurvy
           What are Ehlers-Danlos syndromes?
             •  These are a group of syndromes that are caused by mutations which
                 negatively affect collagen production, structure or processing
             •   It can lead to symptoms such as stretchy skin and loose joints

           What is the basement membrane (basal lamina)?
             •   It is a specialised layer of extracellular matrix that is found on the

           What are integrins?
             •  These are cell surface receptors/proteins used to interact with
                  extracellular matrix molecules such as fibronectin

         How can the glomerular basement membrane be affected in diabetic
             nephropathies?

                 the filtration unit of the kidney)
             •  Diabetic nephropathies can arise when this membrane thickens due
                 to excess ECM deposition (Type IV Collagen) and renal filtration is
                   restricted.

           What does a lack of laminin-alpha2 lead to and why?
             •  Muscular dystrophy
             •  Laminin-alpha2 is one of the three units used to form the main
                 heterotrimer laminin-211
             •  Laminin-211 usually interacts with integrins and dystroglycans, and
                muscles connect to laminin, using it as an anchor
                 ◦  Integrins and dystroglycans are key to skeletal muscle
                     contraction and development
             • A failure of laminin-211 (caused by a lack of laminin-alpha2) to
                  interact with the alpha7beta1 integrin and the alpha-dystroglycan
              means there is a subsequent lack of interaction between muscle
                   cells and integrins/dystroglycans
             •  Overtime this causes progressive muscle weakness and
                degeneration.
