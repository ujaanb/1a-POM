    2.6 Integration of Metabolism

           What are the five main metabolic tissue types in the body?
             •  Heart
             •  Brain and nervous tissue
             •  Liver
             •  Adipose
             •  Muscle
           Which two molecules does muscle rely on for ATP?
             •  Carbohydrates and fatty acids
           What adaptation do muscle cells make in response to moderate exercise
             conditions?
             •  There are an increased number of glucose transporters on the
                 surfaces of the muscle cells allowing more to be taken up by them for
                   glycolysis

         How does metabolism adapt to the requirements of vigorous muscle
             contraction?
             •  During light contraction, the ATP requirements are met by oxidative
                 phosphorylation
             •  During vigorous contraction, oxygen becomes a limiting factor and so
              ATP is made via muscular glycogen breakdown into pyruvate which
               can be used in lactate formation (which enables NAD+ production and
               hence glycolysis, producing ATP)

           Which molecules do the brain and nervous tissue rely on for ATP?
             •  Glucose mainly, in some cases it can use ketone bodies.
           Which molecules can the brain and nervous tissue NOT rely on for ATP?
             •  Fatty acids.
         Why is hypoglycaemia in the brain dangerous?
             •   It can cause comas and faintness
         Why is hyperglycaemia in the brain dangerous?
             •   It can cause irreversible damage.
           What role does adipose tissue play in metabolism?
             • The long term storage of triglycerides.

           Which molecules do the heart tissue rely on for ATP?
             •  Fatty acids
             •  Carbohydrates
             •  Ketone bodies
           What is a structural adaptation of heart tissue which allows it to effectively
            perform its function?
             •   It has lots of mitochondria which can be used for aerobic metabolism
             •  This is important as the heart is designed solely for aerobic
               metabolism, since it constantly needs energy to contract

         How does skeletal muscle tissue cope with the demands placed on it by
             vigorous muscle contraction?
             •  Oxidative phosphorylation can no longer meet its requirements during
                 vigorous contraction
             •  Hence, skeletal muscle uses anaerobic respiration to produce
                  lactate from pyruvate which helps it to regenerate NAD+
                 ◦ NAD+ can be used in glycolysis to continually make ATP
           What role does liver tissue play in metabolism?
             •  Carbohydrate (glycogen) storage
             •  Gluconeogenesis
             •  Lipoprotein production (VLDL and HDL)
           Which tissues are involved in resting metabolic rate?
             •  Brain
             •  Heart
             •  Liver
         How do the liver and pancreas work hand in hand to maintain blood glucose
              levels?
             •  Liver is involved in absorption of glucose and its conversion to
                glycogen so that it can be stored compactly
             •  Pancreas is involved in the release of hormones insulin and glucagon
                which decrease and increase blood glucose levels respectively

         How and where can excess glucose-6-phosphate be stored?
             • As glycogen in the liver and muscles.

         How and where can excess acetyl CoA be stored?
             • As triglycerides in the adipose tissue
                 ◦  Acetyl CoA → Fatty acids via lipogenesis
                 ◦  Fatty acids + glycerol = Triglycerides
           What happens to acetyl CoA during fasting?
             •   It is converted into ketone bodies
             •  This is so there are enough fuel sources for the brain in the fasting
                  state

           What is glycogen?
             •   It is a polymer of glucose which consists of 1-4 and 1-6 glycosidic
                bonds.

         How can nucleotides be produced from carbohydrate metabolism?
             •  Via the pentose-phosphate pathway (PPP) from glucose-6-
               phosphate

         How can glycogen be produced from carbohydrate metabolism?
             • From excess glucose-6-phosphate
         How can ketone bodies be produced from carbohydrate metabolism?
             • From acetyl CoA in the fasting state
                 ◦  In the fasting state, most acetyl CoA is converted to ketone
                   bodies instead of entering the TCA Cycle

         How can amino acids be produced from carbohydrate metabolism?
             • From pyruvate and TCA Cycle intermediates
         How can fatty acids be produced from carbohydrate metabolism?
             • From acetyl CoA via lipogenesis
                 ◦  Malonyl CoA + Acetyl CoA
         How can cholesterol be produced from carbohydrate metabolism?
             • From acetyl CoA
                 ◦  Step 1: Acetyl CoA + Acetyl CoA = Acetoacetyl CoA
           What is the purpose of gluconeogenesis?
             •  To generate glucose for the brain during fasting periods when it may
                 get dangerously low
             •   It is an alternative to glycogen breakdown and acetyl CoA conversion
                  to ketone bodies which can be used to prevent hypoglycaemic comas

         How can glycerol be used in gluconeogenesis?
             •   It is converted to dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP)
             •  This is an intermediate in glycolysis so things become easy from there





          When exactly is lactate needed to be generated from pyruvate?
             • When the rate of glycolysis exceeds the rate of the TCA Cycle +
                   oxidative phosphorylation
             •  This can happen in cases where oxygen is the limiting factor (i.e during
                  exercise) because oxidative phosphorylation requires O2 to function.

           What is the Cori cycle?
             • The process which involves the “re-conversion” of lactate back to
                 pyruvate using lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) in the liver
                 ◦  This pyruvate can be used to regenerate glucose in
                   gluconeogenesis
             • The regenerated glucose is then metabolised again anaerobically to
               reproduce lactate and regenerate NAD+
             •  Overall, lactate is converted to glucose which is metabolised again to
                produce pyruvate and then lactate again. Then it all starts again.

           What is aim of the Cori cycle?
             • The purpose of the Cori cycle is to provide energy to the muscles
                during exercise while removing lactate and preventing lactic
                 acidosis.

         How is lactate used in gluconeogenesis?
             •  Lactate is used to regenerate pyruvate which can enter
                gluconeogenesis

         How are amino acids used in gluconeogenesis?
             • Some amino acids are used to regenerate oxaloacetate which can
                 enter gluconeogenesis

         How is glycerol used in gluconeogenesis?

                which can enter gluconeogenesis

         How does gluconeogenesis become an energetically favourable reaction
             despite delta G being positive for glycolysis reversal?
             •  Gluconeogenesis uses energy from six phosphoanhydride bonds
                (6x ATP) to make the reaction energetically favourable





           Which reactions need to be bypassed in order for gluconeogenesis to go
           ahead (because they are irreversible)?
             •  Those glycolysis reactions catalysed by kinases (hexokinase (I),
                phosphofructokinase (III), pyruvate kinase (X)
             •  Also, the reaction in the mitochondria that produces oxaloacetate
               from pyruvate using pyruvate carboxylase

         How can fats be used as fuel sources in humans?
             •  Triglycerides are broken down into fatty acids + glycerol
             •  Glycerol can then be used to generate DHAP and hence glucose via
                gluconeogenesis
             • FATTY ACIDS ARE NOT ABLE TO GENERATE GLUCOSE but can be
              used as fuel via beta-oxidation





           What are two requirements on muscle cells that allow them to contract?
             •  Increased ATP
             •  Increased number of glucose transporters
           What are the two ways in which adrenaline helps muscle to cope with
             increased ATP demands during aerobic respiration?
             •  Increased gluconeogenesis by the liver which increases rate of
                  glycolysis
             •  Increased fatty acid release by adipocytes which increases rate of
                   lipolysis
             •  Both of these mechanisms involve increasing ATP levels
         How can lactate help humans to cope with increased demands of exercise?
             •  Lactate production from pyruvate regenerates NAD+ which can allow
                   glycolysis to continue
             •  Pyruvate production from this lactate can then be used in
                gluconeogenesis to form more glucose
             •  This is the Cori Cycle
           What is the typical range for blood glucose?
             •  4.0-5.5 mM/L

           Where are the two main isoforms of hexokinases found?
             •  Muscle (I) and liver (IV)
           What is Michaelis constant (Km)?
             • The concentration of substrate which allows an enzyme to function
                  at half its maximal rate
             •   It can be used to compare relative enzyme activity
           Which of the two hexokinase isoforms are more active and explain how you
           know this?
             • HK I
             • HK I has a Km of around 0.1 mM but HK IV has a Km of around 0.4 mM
             •  This means that HK I is more sensitive to glucose and so is more active
                   at lower concentrations

           Where is negative feedback involved in the control of glucose-6-phosphate
             production from glucose?
             •  Muscle
             Describe how negative feedback is involved in the control of glucose-6-
            phosphate production from glucose.
             •  Glucose-6-phosphate (G-6-P), the product of this reaction, inhibits
             Hk I, preventing further conversion of glucose into G-6-P

           What is glucose-6-phosphatase used for?
             •  Catalysis of the conversion of glucose-6-phosphate back to glucose
                   (reversal of glycolysis Step 1)

         How does insulin work?
             •   It stimulates uptake and use of glucose when glucose levels are high
             •   It also stimulates its storage as glycogen and fat
         How does glucagon work?
             •   It is secreted when glucose levels fall and stimulates production of
                glucose via gluconeogenesis
             •   It also stimulates breakdown of glycogen and fat
         How does adrenaline work?
             •   It has strong and fast metabolic effects to mobilise glucose and fatty
                acids for ‘fight or flight’
             •   It increases lipolysis and gluconeogenesis and hence muscular
                  glycolysis during exercise

         How do glucocorticoids work?
             •  They are steroid hormones used to increase the synthesis of
                metabolic enzymes concerned with glucose availability
             •  Essentially used to increase and maintain glucose levels
               In the fed state, as well as glycogen synthesis increasing in muscle and liver
                cells, what other metabolic processes are stimulated?
             •  Glycolysis (in liver cells) which leads to
             •  Acetyl-CoA production and
             •  Lipogenesis (from acetyl CoA)
             •  Overall, anabolic pathways are stimulated in the fed state in liver cells,
                  to save energy for later

           Which organ is involved in the secretion of glucagon and insulin?
             •  Pancreas
               In the fasting state, describe what happens in terms of hormones and
             energy.
             •  Glucagon secretion increases, insulin secretion decreases leading to
                glycogen —> glucose and gluconeogenesis
             •  Fatty acids break down as an alternative for ATP production to
                 preserve glucose for the brain
             •  Glycolysis and lipolysis in skeletal muscle increases due to adrenaline
             •  Gluconeogenesis and glycogen breakdown increase
               In the fasting state, what role does adrenaline play?
             •  Adrenaline stimulates glycogen breakdown as well as glycolysis and
                   lipolysis

           What further changes occur in the starving (prolonged fasting) state?
             •  Adipose tissue hydrolyses triglycerides to provide fatty acids for
               metabolism (beta-oxidation)
             • TCA Cycle intermediates reduced to provide substrates for
                gluconeogenesis
             •  Protein breakdown provides amino acid substrates for
                gluconeogenesis
             •  Ketone bodies are produced from fatty acids and amino acids to
                  temporarily aid the brain’s glucose requirements (brain is picky so
                    this only happens when necessary)

         Why are fatty acids broken down for ATP production instead of glucose in
             the fasting state?
             •  To preserve as much glucose as possible for the brain
             •  After prolonged periods of fasting, fatty acids are converted to ketone

           What is the difference between Type I and Type II diabetes?
             •  Type I diabetes is a failure to secrete enough insulin
             •  Type II diabetes is a failure to respond appropriately to insulin levels.
             •  Both have the effect of blood glucose levels getting too high, and
                   individuals being treated as though they are in the starving state

           What is ketoacidosis?
             • When too many ketone bodies are produced
             •  This happens to diabetes mellitus sufferers
           What tissue is the major source of ketone bodies in the fasting state?
