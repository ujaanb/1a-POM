           What role do bile salts play when interacting with fats?
             •  They emulsify and solubilise fats in the intestine, aiding their digestion
               and absorption
             •  They do this by breaking them down into smaller molecules, making
                 the digestion/absorption job easier as lipases can act on a greater
                 surface area on the fats
             •  They aid lipases





           What can a lack of bile salts cause?
             •   It results in the majority of fats passing through the gut undigested and
               unabsorbed
             •  This, in turn can cause steatorrhea (fatty stool)
           What is an amphipathic/amphiphilic molecule?
             • A molecule that has both hydrophobic and hydrophilic properties
              Recall one amphipathic/amphiphilic molecule.
             • A chylomicron
           What is Orlistat (tetrahydrolipstatin)?
             • A drug which is prescribed to inhibit gastric and pancreatic lipases
               (enzymes that break down lipids)

         How does Orlistat work?
             •   It works by inhibiting these lipases which results in a 30% reduction in
                   fat absorption, most of which is excreted by the faecal route
             •   It can be effective in treating obesity for up to two years

         How do chylomicrons have apoproteins in their structures?
             •  They receive them from HDLs after the release of apoproteins into the
                bloodstream

         Why do HDLs donate apoproteins to chylomicrons?
             • The apoproteins are really important to the chylomicron’s structure
               because it is what allows the other cells in the body to recognise that
                          it is a lipoprotein

           Where are chylomicrons made?
             •  In the small intestine by enterocytes
         How are chylomicrons transported to the bloodstream?
             •  Via the lymphatics
           What are lipoprotein lipases used for?
             •  They are used to access and break down the contents of lipoproteins
               such as the triglycerides
                 ◦  Specifically, the FFAs which can be used in beta-oxidation
           Where are these enzymes located?
             • On the capillary endothelial cells lining the adipose, heart and
                  skeletal muscle tissues.

           What happens to the components of the triglyceride after the lipoprotein
              lipase has performed its function?
             •  Glycerol is returned to the liver for gluconeogenesis
             •  Fatty acids undergo beta-oxidation
         How does the liver interact with chylomicrons?
             •  After chylomicrons have been broken down and have transported the
                     lipids to the relevant locations, the liver absorbs the remnants
             •  This is important as we don’t want apoproteins and phospholipids
                accumulating in the blood, so the liver acts as a salvage organ

           What is the role of a chylomicron?
             •  To transport dietary lipids
           What is the role of VLDL?
             •  To transport endogenous (self-made) lipids

           What is cholesterol?
             •   It is a steroid whose function is to increase or decrease cell
             membrane stiffness, depending on temperature and the nature of the
               membrane.
             •   It is classed as a sterol (type of steroid) because of its hydroxyl group
           Where is the majority of the cholesterol in our bodies stored?
             •  In our cell membranes (90%)
             •   It can increase or decrease membrane stiffness, depending on
                temperature and nature of membrane

           What is dietary cholesterol uptake limited to in humans?
             •  ~0.5g/day.
         How is the majority of the cholesterol in our bodies obtained then?
             • The liver carries out de novo synthesis of cholesterol from acetyl
               CoA.

           What are the three steps through which cholesterol is synthesised?
             •  1 - Synthesis of isopentenyl pyrophosphate (5C)
             • 2 - Condensation of six isopentenyl pyrophosphate monomers →
                squalene
             • 3 - Cyclisation and demethylation of squalene (30C) → cholesterol
                (27C)

           Where do each of these steps take place?
             •  1 - Synthesis of isopentenyl pyrophosphate (6C) - CYTOPLASM
             • 2 - Condensation of six isopentenyl pyrophosphate monomers to form
                squalene - CYTOPLASM
             • 3 - Cyclisation and demethylation of squalene (30C) to form
                  cholesterol (27C) - ENDOPLASMIC RETICULUM

              Biosynthetic pathways need reducing powers to proceed. Where does this
               typically come from?
             • NADPH.
             Describe what happens in the first stage of cholesterol biosynthesis.
             • Two acetyl CoA (2C) molecules are condensed together to form
                 acetoacetyl CoA (4C)
                 ◦ One CoA molecule is lost in this reaction catalysed by beta-
                     ketothiolase
             •  Acetoacetyl CoA (4C) is condensed with acetyl CoA (2C) to form 3-
                Hydroxy-3methylglutaryl CoA (6C) (HMG-CoA)
                 ◦  This reaction is catalysed using HMG-CoA synthase
             • HMG-CoA (6C) is reduced to form mevalonate (6C)
                 ◦  This is done using the major biosynthetic reducing power NADPH
                  and the enzyme HMG-CoA reductase
             •  Mevalonate (6C) is sequentially phosphorylated by various kinases
                 before being decarboxylated to form 3-isopentenyl pyrophosphate
                (5C)

           What is special about the reduction of HMG-CoA to mevalonate?
             •   It can be inhibited by cholesterol, bile salts or mevalonate itself
                 (product inhibition)
             •   It is a key regulatory step and it can be described as a rate-limiting
                 step.

           What is an alternative fate for HMG-CoA?
             •  Formation of ketone bodies
             Describe how negative feedback is involved in the rate limiting step of
              cholesterol biosynthesis.
             •  Cholesterol, mevalonate and bile salts are involved in inhibiting HMG-
             CoA reductase which is key for reducing HMG-CoA (6C) to
                mevalonate (6C)
             •  This is so not too much cholesterol is synthesised and to prevent
                 hypercholesterolemia.

           What type of molecule is 3-isopentenyl pyrophosphate (5C)?
             • An isoprene.
         How are isoprene units related to coenzyme Q (ubiquinone)?
             •  Isoprene units are present on coenzyme Q and this gives them the
                  properties that allow it to be confined (held) to the inner
                 mitochondrial membrane for oxidative phosphorylation
             •  This is because isoprene units have lipophilic properties
             Describe what happens in the second stage of cholesterol biosynthesis.
             •  Isopentenyl pyrophosphate (5C) is isomerised to form dimethylallyl
               pyrophosphate (5C)
             •  Dimethylallyl pyrophosphate (5C) undergoes condensation with
                 isopentenyl pyrophosphate (5C) to form Geranyl pyrophosphate
                (10C)
             •  Geranyl pyrophosphate (10C) undergoes further condensation with
                 isopentenyl pyrophosphate (5C) to form farnesyl-pyrophosphate
                (15C)
             • Two molecules of farnesyl-pyrophosphate (15C) condense to form
                squalene (30C)

             Describe what happens in the third stage of cholesterol biosynthesis.
             •  Squalene (30C) is reduced by NADPH in the presence of O2 to form
                squalene epoxide (30C)
                 ◦  This is catalysed by squalene monoxygenase
             •  Squalene epoxide (30C) is then isomerased by squalene-epoxide
                  lanosterol cyclase catalyses the formation of lanosterol (30C)
             •  Lanosterol (30C) is reduced and demethylated three times to form
                 cholesterol (27C)

         How do VLDLs work in action?
             • When triglyceride/cholesterol levels are low, the lipoprotein lipases of
                 the relevant tissues recognise the VLDLs and break them down to
                 release the free fatty acid (FFA) and cholesterol content
                 ◦ The cholesterol can be used in various ways (steroids, vitamin D
                + bile salt production)
                 ◦  Likewise, the free fatty acids can be used as fuel (beta-
                      oxidation)
                 ◦ The glycerol can be recouped by the liver for gluconeogenesis
                                 (it is converted to DHAP using glycerol-3-phosphate
                    dehydrogenase)
             •  Hence, the role of VLDL is to increase triglyceride and cholesterol
                  levels in the areas that require them

             Describe the mechanism by which HDLs function.
             •  They return cholesterol from the peripheral tissues to the liver where
                            it can be used or disposed of (reverse cholesterol transport)
             • By doing this, they help to lower total serum cholesterol





             Describe the mechanism by which LDLs function.
             •  They transport cholesterol from the liver to the peripheral tissues
                around the body
             • By doing this, they help to increase total serum cholesterol
