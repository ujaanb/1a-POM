             Describe how pyruvate is converted to acetyl CoA.

           What happens when acetyl CoA (2C) enters the mitochondrial matrix for the
          TCA Cycle?
             •   It reacts with oxaloacetate (4C) to produce citrate (6C)
             Describe briefly what happens in the TCA Cycle.
             •  Acetyl CoA (2C) enters from the Link Reaction and reacts with
                oxaloacetate (4C) to form citrate (6C)
             • Redox and decarboxylation reactions occur which produce NADH,
              CO2, GTP and FADH2 molecules using the citrate and convert it back
                 to oxaloacetate (4C)
             •  This oxaloacetate (4C) has now been reproduced and can be used in
                 another round of the TCA Cycle.

             Describe the molecules that are produced via one TCA Cycle.
             • 2x CO2 produced from citrate (6C) —> oxaloacetate (4C)
             • 3x NADH from different reactions (2 arise alongside CO2 removals)
             •  1x FADH2 from 4C to 4C reaction
             •  1x GTP from 4C to 4C reaction





         How much ATP (equivalents) can hence be produced via one TCA Cycle?
             •  12x ATP.
         How does the TCA Cycle help to produce energy, if no ATP is even
            produced?
             • The reduced coenzymes (3x NADH, 1x FADH2) that are produced in
                 the TCA Cycle help to produce the ATP in oxidative phosphorylation
                 ◦  They supply the H+ and e- used in the electron transport chain
           What stops the TCA Cycle from occurring under anaerobic conditions?
             • The TCA Cycle requires oxidised coenzymes (NAD+/FAD) for it to
                 proceed, so it can reduce them to NADH/FADH2 during the cycle
             •  Since these oxidised coenzymes can only be produced via their
                  reoxidation during oxidative phosphorylation (where oxygen accepts
                    their electrons), a lack of oxygen would mean the coenzymes continue
                  to exist in their reduced state
             • Hence the TCA Cycle requires oxygen to go ahead.
           Where are the enzymes used in the TCA Cycle stored?
             •  In the mitochondrial matrix as they are soluble proteins.

         How do amino acids enter the TCA Cycle?
             •  Proteins are down into amino acids
             • The carbon skeletons of these amino acids can form acetyl CoA or
                other intermediates present in the TCA Cycle
                 ◦ The intermediates formed can then either be used to make the
                 same products from the TCA Cycle or in gluconeogenesis
                     (reverse of TCA Cycle)

           What does the term glucogenic imply?
             • The amino acids that form TCA Cycle intermediates can produce either
                glucose or ketone bodies
             • Those that produce glucose are described as glucogenic
           What does the term ketogenic imply?
             • The amino acids that form TCA Cycle intermediates can produce either
                glucose or ketone bodies (i.e acetyl CoA)
             • Those that produce ketone bodies are described as ketogenic
           What is a transamination reaction?
             • A reaction that involves the transfer of an amine group from one
                molecule to another.

         How is a transamination reaction useful for amino acid metabolism?
             •  Transferring amine groups from amino acids to keto acids allows
              amino acids to be metabolised without harm to the cell
                 ◦  This is because metabolising amine groups can be toxic to
                humans
             •  Overall, it helps us to metabolise amino acids efficiently.
           What are aminotransferases?
             • Enzymes that are involved in the catalysis of transamination
                  reactions.

           Which two molecules are involved in transamination reactions for amino
             acids?
             • Amino acids and ketoacids

             Describe the function of the glycerol-phosphate shuttle.
             • NADH adds its electrons to Dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP),
                converting it to glycerol-3-phosphate and NADH becomes oxidised
                 to NAD+
                 ◦  This reaction is catalysed by cytoplasmic glycerol-3-phosphate
                  dehydrogenase
             •  Glycerol-3-phosphate adds these electrons back to FAD, reducing it
                  to FADH2 and converting itself back to Dihydroxyacetone phosphate
              (DHAP)
                 ◦  This reaction is catalysed by mitochondrial glycerol-3-
                  phosphate dehydrogenase
             • FADH2 then passes its electrons onto coenzyme Q (ubiquinone - via
               complex II in the electron transport chain) where they can be used in
                   oxidative phosphorylation





             Describe the path that the electrons from NADH take through the glycerol-
            phosphate shuttle.
             •  Electrons are transferred from NADH to DHAP by cytosolic glycerol-3-
               phosphate dehydrogenase in order to convert the DHAP to glycerol-
               3-phosphate
             • The electrons are then are transferred to FAD by mitochondrial
                glycerol-3-phosphate dehydrogenase when glycerol-3-phosphate is
                converted back to DHAP
             • The FADH2 can then transfer these electrons to coenzyme Q
                 (ubiquitone) via complex II in the electron transport chain.

           What is the role of the malate-aspartate shuttle?
             •  Its role is to transfer NADH (electrons) from the cytosol into the
                 mitochondrial matrix so that it can be reoxidised in oxidative
                phosphorylation
             •   It does this for the liver, kidney and heart
         How can oxaloacetate be converted to malate for the malate-aspartate
              shuttle?
             •  Reduction via the addition of two hydrogen atoms (H2)
             Describe the function of the malate-aspartate shuttle.
             •  Aspartate reacts with alpha-ketoglutarate in a transamination reaction
                  to form oxaloacetate and glutamate via aspartate transaminase
             •  Oxaloacetate is reduced to malate via malate dehydrogenase
                 ◦  During this reaction, the NADH is oxidised to form NAD+ and the
                     electrons are transferred to the malate
             •  Malate takes the electrons through the malate-alphaketoglutarate
                   antiporter whilst alphaketoglutarate moves in the opposite direction
             •  Malate is then oxidised back to oxaloacetate via malate
               dehydrogenase and NAD+ is reduced again to form NADH
                 ◦  Oxaloacetate is then reacted with glutamate to form aspartate
                  and alphaketoglutarate via aspartate transferase
                 ◦ The aspartate travels through the aspartate-glutamate
                      antiporter (while glutamate goes the other way) and another
                      cycle can begin.

             Describe what occurs in the beta-oxidation cycle for fatty acids.
             •  During the beta-oxidation cycle, a series of oxidation + hydrolysis,
                  oxidation and thiolysis (reaction with HS-CoA to shorten the chain)
                  reactions occur to the original acyl CoA molecule
             •  During this process 1x FADH2 and 1x NADH are produced
             • The acetyl CoA species can also be used to produce more ATP
                through the Krebs Cycle
             •  Overall, acyl CoA’s (derived from fatty acids) are broken down to
                 acetyl CoA (2Cs) and a shortened acyl CoA (by 2Cs as it has lost
                 acetyl CoA) which can be used in another beta oxidation cycle,
                  whilst producing 1x NADH and 1x FADH2 in the process

             Describe the role and function of the carnitine shuttle.
             •  Acyl CoA is generated in the first step of fatty acid beta-oxidation
             •  Its role is to transport acyl CoA from the cytosol to the mitochondrial
                   matrix, where it can be metabolised
             •   It uses enzymes called carnitine acyltransferases to convert carnitine
                 to acyl carnitine outside and acyl carnitine to carnitine inside so that
                 the acyl group can be transported into the matrix by a translocase
             • The acyl group carried by the acyl carnitine is then added to CoA in
                 the matrix to reform the acyl CoA





           What type of genetic disorder is primary carnitine deficiency?
             •  Autosomal recessive
             •  Mutations in a gene known as SLC22A5 which encodes a carnitine
                 transporter result in reduced ability of cells to take up carnitine,
               needed for the β-oxidation of fatty acids.

         How does primary carnitine deficiency affect individuals?
             • The transporter deficiency means that less acyl CoA is transported into
                 the mitochondrial matrix as less acyl carnitine goes through the
                   carnitine shuttle
             •  This ultimately means less fatty acids are metabolised as they cannot
              be metabolised in the cytosol.

         How can a primary carnitine deficiency be treated?
             •   It can be remedied using carnitine supplements.
           What type of molecule enters the beta-oxidation cycle?
             •  Acyl CoA

           What are the products of one beta oxidation cycle?
             •  1x acetyl CoA (2C)
             •  1x shortened acyl CoA (minus 2C)
             •  1x NADH
             •  1x FADH2
             Describe what occurs when palmitic acid (16C) is beta-oxidised.
             •   It is converted to palmitoyl CoA (16C)
             •   It goes through the beta-oxidation cycle 7 times to produce 8 acetyl-
             CoA molecules (2C), 7 NADH and 7 FADH2
             •   It produces 8 acetyl-CoA molecules because the last cycle produces
               2x acetyl CoA molecules instead of 1x acetyl CoA and 1x shortened
                  acyl CoA
             •  These can be used to produce ATP, and A LOT is produced.
          When and why are ketone bodies formed from acetyl CoA?
             •  Ketone body formation is an alternative fate for acetyl CoA that
                  usually occurs during the fasting state
             •  This is because the TCA Cycle intermediates (such as oxaloacetate)
                 are usually committed to gluconeogenesis (production of energy
             when fasting) and so acetyl CoA won’t be able to enter the TCA
                Cycle under these conditions.
                 ◦  Acetyl CoA needs to combine with oxaloacetate when it enters
                     the TCA Cycle

             Describe briefly what lipogenesis is.
             •   It is the formation of a fatty acid.
             •  Effectively the reversal of beta oxidation (fatty acid
                metabolism/breakdown)

           What are the two molecules involved in lipogenesis?
             •  Malonyl CoA (3C) and acetyl CoA (2C)
             •  These molecules are used as the building blocks of lipogenesis
             •  Acyl carrier proteins are used to enable the continuation of
                  lipogenesis - they act as a base upon which more fatty acids can be
              added
