    2.15 Cancer

           What is a tumour?
             • Any kind of mass-forming lesion (lesion meaning an area which has
                 suffered injury from disease or injury)
             •   It can be neoplastic or non-neoplastic
                 ◦  i.e they may or may not have escaped normal constraints on cell
                         proliferation

           What is a neoplasm?
             • The autonomous growth of tissue that have escaped normal
                 constraints on cell proliferation
                 ◦  i.e any tissue that has grown too big without external stimulation,
                    but with cell cycle defects
             •  Cancers are malignant neoplasms
           What is the difference between a benign and malignant neoplasm?
             •  Neoplasia describes cells that have escaped normal constraints on
                    cell proliferation
             • A benign neoplasm is localised and so is harmless
             • A malignant neoplasm can metastasise and invade other tissue
                   locally/distantly and so is dangerous

         How would you distinguish between a benign and malignant tumour upon
              physical examination?
             • A benign neoplasm can be moved around on examination when
                  palpating its surface
             • A malignant neoplasm cannot be moved around on examination
              when palpating its surface
                 ◦  This is because it has often invaded other tissue and become
                    attached to it (i.e muscles)

           What is metastasis?
             •  This is when a malignancy has spread to distant sites around the
               body via blood vessels
             •   It is good to diagnose a cancer before metastasis
           Which malignant tumour is typically known to not always result in death?
             •  Skin cancers (melanomas)

           What is a hamartoma?
             • A localised, benign overgrowth of one or more mature cell types -
                 they are harmless
             • A discrete mass (tumour) is formed within this tissue that consists of
                 multiple cell types
             •   It is not simply a “benign neoplasm” because a hamartoma is
                  distinguished by having multiple cell types
                 ◦ The cell types are usually already present in the tissue, they just
                    tend to clump together to form this distinct mass





                 Is a hamartoma neoplastic?
             •  Yes.
             • The cells involved have escaped the normal constraints of cell
                   proliferation and this is how they combine together to form their
                    distinct mass.

           What is a heterotopia?
             •  This is where there is a normal group of cells (tissue) being found in
                 parts of the body where they are not normally present
                 ◦  i.e pancreas tissue found in large intestine wall
           What does the prefix “chondro” suggest in cancer classification?
             • The tumour is derived from cartilage
           What does the suffix “-oma” suggest in cancer classification?
             • A benign tumour

           What does the suffix “-sarcoma” suggest in cancer classification?
             • A malignant tumour
             •  In any tumour classification, the prefix refers to the type of tissue
                 affected, and the suffix refers to the malignancy of the tumour.

           What is the name given to a benign tumour of squamous epithelial cells?
             • A squamous papilloma
           What is a the name given to a malignant tumour of squamous epithelial cells?
             • Squamous cell carcinoma
           Which tissues can a squamous cell neoplasm affect?
             •  Skin
             • Oesophagus
             •  Cervix
           What is the name given to a benign tumour of glandular epithelial cells?

           What is a the name given to a malignant tumour of glandular epithelial cells?
             •  Adenocarcinoma
           Which tissues can a glandular cell neoplasm affect?
             •  Breast
             •  Colon

           What is a teratoma?
             • A tumour derived from a germ cell
             • Can be derived from all three germ cell layers (i.e endoderm,
               mesoderm, ectoderm)
             •  They can occur in immature or mature cells.
           Where are the most common sites that you would find teratomas at?
             •  Testes
             •  Ovaries
            Teratomas can be derived from both immature and mature cells. Which is
            worse?
             •  Immature teratomas are worse because immature cells have much
                higher proliferative capacity
                 ◦  This leads to higher risk of malignancy and hence systemic
                 damage

              Recall four examples of tumours with the suffix -oma but still being
             malignant.
             • Lymphoma (lymphocytes)
             • Melanoma (skin)
             • Hepatoma (liver cells)
             •  Teratoma (germ cells)
              Recall four ways that benign and malignant neoplasms differ from each
               other.
             •  Extent of invasion
             •  Malignancy
             •  Differentiation
             •  Growth pattern
           Which of these differences can be used to initially identify whether a growth
                 is benign or malignant in a lab?
             •  Differentiation
             • Growth pattern

           What is the difference between dysplasia and cancer?
             •  Cancer usually involves invasion, dysplasia does not
                 ◦  Invasion is seen in malignant neoplasms, dysplasia is seen in
                   benign neoplasms
             •  Invasion (in cancer) involves cells directly invading adjacent
                connective tissue and other structures (i.e blood vessels)
             •  Dysplasia means the cells show cytological features of cancer (i.e
                    ratio of cytoplasm:nucleus) but do not invade
                 ◦  i.e the intermediate stage between normality and cancer
           What is the difference between metastasis of benign and malignant
            tumours?
             •  Benign tumours are non-invasive, malignant tumours are invasive
                 ◦ NOTE: Not all malignant tumours metastasise (spread through
                     the blood). Some simply invade locally.

           What is the difference between invasion and metastasis?
             •  Invasion describes local encroachments of a neoplasm
                 ◦  i.e breast cancer attaching to pectoralis major muscle
             •  Metastasis describes systemic encroachments of a neoplasm where
                 the neoplasm spreads through the blood to affect other structures

           What is meant by differentiation of tumours?
             •  Differentiation describes how much the cells of the tumour
               resemble the cells they originated from
             •  Well differentiated cells look a lot like the cells they derive from
             •  Poorly differentiated cells do not really look like the cells they derive
               from

           What is the difference between malignant and benign tumour cells
              cytologically?
             •  Malignant tumour cells tend to have a much higher
                 nuclear:cytoplasmic ratio than the cells of the tissue they are derived
                from
             •  Benign tumour cells don’t typically possess this trait.
           What is meant by the growth pattern of tumours?
             • Growth pattern describes how well the architecture of the
                 neoplastic tissue resembles the architecture of the tissue it is
                derived from
                 ◦   It is similar to differentiation, but whilst differentiation refers to
                      cytological appearance, growth pattern is all about tissue
                     architecture

           What is the difference in growth patterns between tumour cells and their
             derived cell types?
             •  Poorly differentiated tumours look a lot less similar to the tissue
                they derived from
             •  Well differentiated tumours look a lot more similar to the tissue they
                derived from

              Recall five ways that neoplasms can spread.
             •  Direct extension (structures being in close proximity to each other)
                 ◦  i.e thyroid cancer patients having coarse voices due to
                     proximity of recurrent laryngeal nerve to thyroid gland
             • Haematogenous (via blood vessels)
             •  Lymphatic (through lymphatic vessels to lymph nodes)
             •  Transcoelomic (via seeding of body cavities such as pleural cavities)
             •  Perineural (via nerves)
             •  Through injections (i.e biospies)
           Which types of blood vessels are typically invaded in metastases and why?
             •  Veins and capillaries
             •  This is because they have a lot less smooth muscle and so their walls
                are much thinner

           What is the function of the thoracic duct?
             •  To drain fluid from the lymph nodes back into the circulation
           Which type of cancer typically metastasises through the lymphatics?
             •  Epithelial cell cancers
           Image showing how lymphatic spread of breast cancer occurs through the
             lymphatic capillaries and axillary nodes.





           What is the ‘TNM’ system used for?
             •  To describe the stage and how much a tumour has spread
