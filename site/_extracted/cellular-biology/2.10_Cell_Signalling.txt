    2.10 Cell Signalling

             Describe how the body responds to hypoglycaemia.
             •  Glucagon is released by alpha cells in the Islet of Langerhans
                 (pancreas)
             •  Glucagon travels from pancreas to blood vessels
             •  This stimulates glycogenolysis (breakdown of glycogen) and
                gluconeogenesis (generation of glucose) in the liver, increasing blood
                glucose levels.

           What is meant by “endocrine signalling”?
             •  This describes how secreted hormones travel from one tissue in the
               body to another distant target cell via the bloodstream
             •  For example, insulin is secreted by the pancreas to act on the liver.
             Describe how the body responds to hyperglycaemia.
             •  Insulin is released by beta cells in the Islet of Langerhans (pancreas)
             •  This prevents further glucagon secretion in the pancreas, preventing
                   further increase of blood glucose levels.

           What is meant by “paracrine signalling”?
             • The action of a hormone on an adjacent target cell.
              Recall two other examples of paracrine signalling.
             •  Nitric oxide produced by endothelial cells in blood vessels, acting on
              smooth muscle cells in blood vessels to cause vasodilation
             •  Osteoclast activating factors produced by adjacent osteoblasts (bone
                  remodelling).

             Describe an example of signalling between membrane attached proteins.
             •  Blood-borne virus detected by antigen-presenting cell (APC)
             • APC digests pathogen and expresses MHC Class II molecules on its
                 surface
             •  This leads to T Cell receptor (TCR) interacting with this MHC and
                binding to the antigen

           What is meant by autocrine signalling?
             •  Signalling molecules being released by one cell and binding onto the
             same cell, acting on it.

             Describe how the process of neurotransmission between the presynaptic
           and postsynaptic axon terminals occurs.
             • The action potential is propagated by the depolarisation (Na+ influx)
               and repolarisation (K+ efflux) of the presynaptic membrane
             • The action potential leads to Ca2+ influx into the presynaptic axon
                  terminal and exocytosis
             • The neurotransmitter binds to the postsynaptic receptors
             • The postsynaptic receptors are activated and signal is transmitted.
           Where on cells are postsynaptic receptor molecules located?
             • On cell membranes
              Recall the four types of postsynaptic neurone receptor.
             •  Ionotropic receptor
             •  G-protein coupled receptor
             •  Enzyme-linked receptor
             •  Intracellular receptor
           What is meant by the term “ligand” in cell signalling?
             • A hormone, protein, molecule
             •  Just anything that can bind to a receptor
             Describe how ionotropic receptors work.
             •  Ligand binds onto the receptor
             •  This causes an ion channel protein to open a pore in the cell
              membrane
             • The pore causes ions to travel in and out of cells depending on the
                concentration gradient.

           What is an alternative name for a G-protein coupled receptor and why is
              that name given to it?
             • 7-TM (transmembrane) receptor
             •  This is because it crosses the membrane seven times.

             Describe how G-protein coupled receptors work.
             •  Ligand binds onto the receptor which activates it and causes it to
              change conformation
             •  This allows the previously unassociated heterotrimeric G-protein to
                bind to the receptor
                 ◦ NOTE: multiple G-proteins bind to the receptor as a result of
                  one G protein binding
             • GDP molecule is exchanged for a GTP molecule and the G-protein is
                  activated
             • The G-protein then separates into its alpha and the beta-gamma
                subunits
             • The alpha subunit, attached to the GTP molecule, then moves to the
                 target protein
                 ◦ GTP is dephosphorylated to GDP using internal GTPase, this
                   energy is used to move the alpha-GTP complex to the target
                     protein
             •  Effects are then induced depending on the identity of the target
                 protein
             • The alpha subunit then rebinds to the beta and gamma subunits and
                reforms the G-protein
             • The process stops when the ligand is no longer bound to the cell
                surface

           What are the two most prominent types of G-protein?
             • Gs and Gi.
             •  s for stimulate (adenylyl cyclase activity), i for inhibit (adenylyl cyclase
                     activity).

           What is the third type of G-protein?
             •  Gq.
           What is adenylyl cyclase?
             •  This is one of the target proteins that G proteins can act on during
                 the cascade of reactions

           Which G proteins act on adenylyl cyclase and what effects do they have?
             • Gs - stimulates adenylyl cyclase expression
             •  Gi - inhibits adenylyl cyclase
           What does adenylyl cyclase do?
             •   It stimulates conversion of ATP to cAMP (cyclic adenosine
              monophosphate)
             • cAMP stimulates protein kinase A production
             •  This protein kinase A (PKA) has various intracellular effects within
                    cells.
             •  i.e increased protein kinase A can be used to release adrenaline
              Recall two examples of G-protein coupled receptors.
             •  Beta1-adrenergic receptor
             •  M2-muscarinic receptor
           What is the beta1-adrenergic receptor used for?
             •  Adrenaline binding
                 ◦  This can induce effects such as increasing heart rate
           What is the M2-muscarinic receptor used for?
             •  Acetylcholine binding
                 ◦  This can induce effects such as decreasing heart rate
           What does the Gq protein do?
             •  Stimulates phospholipase C
             •  Converts PIP to IP3 and DAG
             •  IP3 causes Ca2+ release which is useful for exocytosis
             • DAG activates PKC (protein kinase C)

         How does an enzyme-linked receptor work?
             •  At least two ligands bind to the receptor which causes clustering of
                receptors
             • The clustering activates the enzymes that are linked to the receptors
             • The active enzymes then phosphorylate the receptor
             •  Phosphorylation leads to the binding of signalling proteins to the
                 cytoplasmic domain of the receptor
             •  These signalling proteins recruit other signalling proteins and signal
                     is generated throughout the cell.
