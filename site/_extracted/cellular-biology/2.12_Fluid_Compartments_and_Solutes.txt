             •  i.e a cumulative concentration of all the solutes.
         Why is there no osmosis between interstitial fluid and cells?
             • The osmolarity inside and outside of cells is equal.
             •  This means there is no net movement of water in and out of cells.

         Why is osmolarity on its own an unreliable way to judge whether a cell
             bursts or swells?
             •  Because osmolarity can guide the direction which water will move
                 but permeability of the cell membrane to solutes is also very important
                 ◦  i.e a cell membrane could be impermeable to an intracellular
                        solute, meaning that water will move inside in the equilibrium
                       state to balance osmolarity
                 ◦  in contrast, if the cell membrane was permeable to the solute, it
                   would move outwards on its own, and not as much water would
                  need to move into the cell to balance osmolarity
             • Hence the osmolarity of two identical cells can be equal, but
                    different amounts of water go in/out of it depending on its
                 permeability to solutes

          When can a cell rupture?
             • When the difference in osmolarity between inside and outside of the
                     cell is so high that so much water moves in that can cause rupturing.
                 ◦  i.e very high intracellular osmolarity
           What is tonicity?
             •  Tonicity describes the osmolarity of a solution’s impermeant solutes
                 present in the solution
                 ◦  This hence helps us to understand which direction water needs to
                 move in, to balance this osmolarity out

           What is a hypertonic solution?
             • One which has a greater osmolarity of impermeant solutes than the
                  inside of the cells around it
                 ◦  This leads to water having to move out of the cell to balance the
                       osmolarities, so the cell shrinks

           What is a hypotonic solution?
             • One which has a lower osmolarity than the inside of the cells around it
                 ◦  This leads to water having to move into the cell to balance the
                       osmolarities, so the cell swells





           What is an isotonic solution?
             • One which has an identical osmolarity of impermeant solutes to the
                  inside of the cells around it
                 ◦  Water does not need to move in any particular direction to
                    balance out the osmolarities, because all the solutes can move
                   themselves
             •  Basically, water only moves when the solutes cannot move across their
                 concentration gradients





           What mechanism do cells have which allows them to not burst?
             • Na+/K+ ATPase
             •  This constantly pumps Na+ out of the cell, increasing extracellular
                  osmolarity and allowing water to not accumulate within the cell, but
            H2O to follow Na+

           What is an alternative name for the sodium-potassium pump?
             •  Na+/K+ ATPase.
         Why does the sodium-potassium pump not function in organs outside the
           body?
             •   It cannot function below 15ºC
             •  There is no ATP being produced in ischaemic conditions so it cannot
                  function

           What is University of Wisconsin (UW) solution used for?
             •  To reduce cell-swelling in tissues of organs that are preserved in it.
           What three properties of UW solution help it to reduce cell swelling?
             •  Lack of Na+/Cl- which prevents swelling
                 ◦  Otherwise, these ions would move down the conc. gradient into
                     the tissues, and hence drag water into the tissues, causing
                       swelling
             •  Presence of extracellular impermeable solutes
                 ◦  This increases extracellular osmolarity, meaning water is drawn
                     out of tissues
             •  Presence of starch to contribute to and increase colloidal osmotic
                pressure
             •  Antioxidants to protect tissue from damage via reactive oxygen
                  species.

           What property of the endothelial layer allow them to transfer blood plasma
              to the interstitial space?
             •  Leaks or pores (fenestrae) in between the endothelial cells.

           What is colloidal osmotic pressure (COP)?
             •  Also known as oncotic pressure, COP is a type of osmotic pressure
               induced by the plasma proteins, notably albumin in a blood vessel's
               plasma
             •  This pressure is caused by the plasma proteins within blood vessels
                being unable to move out of the blood vessels, so they build up and
               cause pressure in the form of COP
                 ◦   It acts to pull water into blood vessels, because the
                   accumulation of plasma proteins causes high intravascular
                      osmolarity. Water moves into vessels to balance the high
                     osmolarity found within the vessel





           What is hydrostatic pressure?
             • The “pushing” force exerted by any fluid as a result of it moving
                through its concordant vessel

           Can plasma proteins fit through the endothelial wall?
             •  No.

         How do the colloidal osmotic pressure and hydrostatic pressure balance to
            determine the movement of molecules in and out of the endothelium?
             •  Colloidal osmotic pressure can be induced by high amounts of
               plasma proteins accumulating in the lumen
             •  Hydrostatic pressure is induced by the movement of the blood
                through the blood vessel
             • The balance of colloidal/hydrostatic pressure can cause soluble ions
              and even plasma proteins to be pushed through the endothelial wall
              between the pores.
                 ◦  i.e Higher hydrostatic pressure compared to colloidal osmotic
                     pressure can lead to fluid being more readily pushed out to
                    balance things out
             • The aim is to have an equilibrium where colloidal osmotic pressure is
                equal to hydrostatic pressure
             •  Hydrostatic pressure > COP = fluid moving out of blood vessel
             • COP > Hydrostatic pressure = fluid moving into blood vessel

           What happens to the blood vessels in oedema (tissue swelling)?
             •  Excess fluid leaks from the lumen of blood vessels into the
                     interstitial space which causes swelling
             •  This can be caused by extra leaky capillaries, due to the imbalance of
                    colloidal osmotic pressure and hydrostatic pressure
                 ◦  Hydrostatic pressure > COP





           What is the function of the lymphatic capillaries?
             •  To collect interstitial fluid that has leaked from the blood vessels as
               a result of hydrostatic pressure exceeding oncotic pressure
             •   It returns this fluid back to the blood capillaries via the lymphatic
                ducts of the subclavian region, or the lymph nodes
