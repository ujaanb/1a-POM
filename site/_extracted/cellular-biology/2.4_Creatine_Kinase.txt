    2.4 Creatine Kinase

           What is the main role of creatine kinase?
             •  To catalyse the phosphorylation of ADP to ATP via the breakdown of
                  creatine phosphate.

             Describe the structure of a creatine kinase isoenzyme.
             •   It is a dimeric structure which consists of two subunits from M or B.
           What are the three main creatine kinase isoenzyme forms?
             • MB, MM, BB
           Where are they found?
             • BM - cardiac muscle
             • BB - brain
             • MM - skeletal muscle
           What is a special consideration regarding one of the dimeric isoenzymes?
             • MB is only found in heart muscle cells.
           What is a coupled enzyme assay?
             •   It is a test where you measure the activity of an enzyme
             •  You do this by coupling the enzyme to another one, allowing a series of
                  reactions to proceed and measuring a product in one of these
                 reactions to indirectly determine the initial enzyme’s activity





         How does measuring levels of different isoforms of creatine kinase help us?
             •   It can help us to understand where exactly muscle activity is taking
                 place in the body (i.e in brain, muscle or heart muscle).

           What is the isoelectric point?
             • The pH at which the charge on a molecule is neutral.

           What is a myocardial infarct, and what causes it?
             • The death of heart muscle cells, caused by a lack of oxygen, often
               caused by atherosclerosis

         How does a lack of oxygen lead to cell death?
             • A lack of oxygen means that oxidative phosphorylation cannot take
                place and so ATP is not produced in the final step of aerobic
                   respiration
             •  This leads to malfunction of vital cellular components such as protein
             pumps which are crucial to maintaining ion concentrations across
                     cell membranes (i.e sodium-potassium pump)
             •  This poor regulation can lead to cell death if ions are imbalanced and
                    this leads to cells bursting too in a process called lysis
                 ◦  Cell shrinkage can also lead to cell death
           What can be measured as an indicator for cell death?
             •  Levels of certain intracellular proteins (i.e creatine kinase, lactate
                dehydrogenase)
             •  Abnormally high levels of these proteins outside cells could indicate
                 high levels of cell lysis

           What assumption does the statement “the volume of cardiac muscle cell
            (myocyte) death can be measured by the amount of BM Creatine Kinase
            Isomer present in the serum” lie on?
             •  That each myocyte releases an equal amount of creatine kinase
              when it dies (independent of size)

           What laboratory technique can be used to identify different creatine kinase
             isoforms and why?
             •  Gel electrophoresis
             •  This is because they separate subjects on the basis of charge or
                molecular weight
             •  All three isoforms of creatine kinase have different isoelectric points
               and so will be separated on the basis of charge.

           What three markers can be used as an indication of myocardial infarction?
             • Serum glutamate oxaloacetate transaminase (SGOT)
             •  lactate dehydrogenase (LDH)
             •  Cardiac troponin
             •  Obviously MB Creatine Kinase.

             Describe how an immunoassay can be used in measuring serum levels of
              creatine kinase (CK).
             • A blood/serum sample can be obtained from the patient
             •  This sample can be mixed with labelled CK that changes colour
             when bound to the antibodies and it also has native antibodies
             • The level of binding by the labelled CK can be measured using the
                 colour saturation produced by this CK binding to antibodies
             •  Then, using a standard curve we can assess serum levels of CK by
                 converting saturation to serum CK levels. The lower the colour
                  saturation, the higher the levels of native CK are.
                 ◦  This is because native CK is unlabelled, hence if there is a
                    higher level of native CK, there will be less labelled CK able to
                    bind to antibodies and change colour due to increased
                    competition for the antibodies
