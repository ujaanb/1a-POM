    2.14 Enzyme Kinetics

           What is the role of enzymes in our bodies?
             •  To act as catalysts, ensuring that the many biochemical reactions
                  taking place in our body do so at a rate that can support life.

           What is Michaelis constant (Km)?
             • The concentration of substrate which allows an enzyme to function at
                    half its maximal rate
             •   It can be used to compare relative enzyme activity
           What is Vmax?
             • Vmax of enzyme is the maximum rate of reaction
                 ◦ Maximum rate of substrate processing by enzymes





           What is the purpose of a reaction buffer?
             • The buffer maintains the pH of the reaction mixture and supplies
               potassium (K+) or ammonium (NH4+) ions for optimal enzyme
                   activity

         Why are we are measuring p-nitroaniline in the spectrophotometer?
             • We are measuring p-nitroaniline in the spectrophotometer because it
               produces a yellow colour, and hence absorbance
                 ◦  This helps us to monitor product formation
           What is V0?
             •   Initial rate of reaction
           What is Km?
             • Km is known as Michaelis Constant and is defined as the
                concentration of substrate at which a particular enzyme at a
                  particular concentration works at half its maximal velocity.
             •   It helps us to compare the relative strength of enzyme-substrate
               complexes

           What does a high/low Km show?
             • A high Km shows typically weak binding between enzymes and
                substrates and hence low enzymatic activity
             • A low Km shows typically strong binding between enzymes and
                substrates and hence high enzymatic activity

           What is a Lineweaver-Burk plot?
             • A double reciprocal plot used to determine Km and Vmax

             Describe how a Lineweaver-Burk plot helps us to determine Km and Vmax.
             • The x-intercept = -1/Km
             • The y-intercept = 1/Vmax
             •  x-axis = 1/substrate concentration
             •  y-axis = 1/initial rate of reaction

         How can the turnover number (Kcat) of an enzyme be calculated?
             •  Turnover number (Kcat) = Vmax / enzyme concentration
                 ◦  This is because Vmax measures the maximum rate at which
                   product formation occurs (and hence maximum rate at which
                 enzyme acts on substrate) and enzyme concentration tells us
                how many enzymes are involved this product formation

           What effect does a competitive antagonist (one that binds in or near the
            enzyme’s active site) have on Km and Vmax?
             •   It increases Km but has no effect on Vmax
             •  This is because the enzyme’s function has not been affected, but the
                  substrate concentration required to bind to a given concentration of
              enzyme has increased as the enzyme’s affinity for the substrate has
               decreased due to increased competition for the active site (hence
                 the name competitive inhibitor)

           What effect does a non-competitive antagonist have on Km and Vmax?
             •  Non-competitive inhibitors have no effect on Km but act to lower
             Vmax
             •  This is because they induce conformational changes on the enzyme
                which causes a reduction in the Vmax, but do not actually change
                the affinity of substrate molecules binding to enzymes, so the same
               amount of substrate is required to induce similar effects
