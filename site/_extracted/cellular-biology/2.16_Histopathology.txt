    2.16 Histopathology

           What is the difference between a histopathologist and a cytopathologist?
             • A histopathologist specialises in tissues
             • A cytopathologist focuses on cells.
           What are the four main types of tissue samples that a histopathologist
             analyses?
             •  Biopsies
             •  Frozen section
             •  Resection specimen
             •  Post mortems
                 ◦  These samples are often taken in real-time and upon real-time
                    examination they can inform the surgical process

           What is a biopsy?
             • A biopsy, put simply, is a small section of tissue that has been taken
               from a patient.

          When is a biopsy used?
             •  They can be used to determine whether tissue is inflamed, normal,
               cancerous etc.
                 ◦  i.e what exactly is wrong with this tissue?
           What is the name of the solution biopsies are commonly placed in?
             •  Formalin solution.
           What is the role of this solution?
             •  Formalin is used to cross-link proteins and fix the biopsy tissue
         How long does it take to process a biopsy?
             •  Biopsies are processed in 2-3 days
           What is the purpose of paraffin wax in biopsy preparation?
             •  To allow very thin sections to be extracted using a microtome
           What is a resection specimen?
             • A resection specimen is a part of tissue that has been removed via a
                  surgical procedure and can be processed in the same way as a biopsy
                 ◦  Similar to biospy, but the tissue is taken as part of a surgical
                   procedure

          When is a resection specimen used?
             •  They can be used to determine the stage at which the disease is at
                        (i.e has the cancer spread to lymph nodes?)

         How long does it take to process a resection specimen?
             •  Resection specimen are processed in 5-7 days
           What is a frozen section?
             •  Frozen sections are samples taken during surgical procedures that
                 are examined during real time
             • The freshly taken tissue is frozen and stained but no preservatives
                      (i.e formalin) are added

           What is the name of the device used to freeze a frozen section?
             • A cryostat
          When is a frozen section used?
             •  Frozen sections are used to determine the different types of tissue
                present
                 ◦  i.e Is it cancerous? Is there another pathological process
                    ongoing? etc.

         How long does it take to process a frozen section?
             •  Frozen sections are processed in 30 minutes

             • A cytopathology technique that uses a fine needle to penetrate
                  tissue and obtain a sample that can be viewed under a microscope

         How are antibody conjugates useful?
             •  Antibody conjugates can be useful tools in telling us how much
                 antigen is present in a patient’s serum, to deliver chemicals to specific
                 areas in the body etc.
             • By binding different molecules to antibodies, we can accomplish
                    different things.

             Describe four antibody conjugates.
             • Enzymes - the colourless enzyme substrate is added, and the level
                 of coloured product informs us how much antibody is present in the
               serum
             •  Fluorescent probes - allows the rapid measurement of molecule
                  levels within samples.
             •  Magnetic beads - used to purify cell types (attract specific cells and
              remove them)
             • Drugs - delivered to specific cell types by binding onto antibody for
                   that cell type. Can be paired with cytotoxic agents to kill bad cells.
                 ◦  i.e emtansine which kills HER2 (protein seen in breast cancer)

           What are the two ways that antibodies can be used to detect other
            molecules (i.e proteins, lipids, carbohydrates)?
             •  Direct detection - antibody is labelled and detects an antigen
                   directly
                 ◦ The labelled antibody is known as the primary antibody
             •  Indirect detection - One antibody detects an antigen. Another
                antibody is labelled and detects this bound antibody
                 ◦ The labelled antibody is known as the secondary antibody





              Recall two uses of manufactured antibodies in this way clinically.
             •  Blood group serology - determination of blood groups based on
                 antigens present in blood
             • Immunoassays - detection of hormones, circulating
                 antibodies/antigens
             •  Immunodiagnosis
                 ◦  In infectious diseases, seeing which antigens are present helps us
                    diagnose disease (i.e HIV)
                 ◦  Antibody levels - high antibody levels can be a sign of myeloma
                 ◦  IgE - in response to allergy

             Describe how ELISA can be used to determine the concentration of
              antigens/antibodies in a sample.
             •  Antigens are present in a sample
             •  Antibodies are added to the sample, and bind to these antigens
             • Enzymes are linked to the antibodies
                 ◦ Unbound antibodies are washed away
             •  Colourless substrates are added to the sample, so the enzyme can
               break it down and make a coloured product
             • The absorbance of the solution is measured, and using the Beer-
               Lambert Law, concentration of the antibodies and hence antigens
                are measured.
