    2.13 Inflammation

           What is inflammation?
             •  Inflammation is a non-specific response to cellular injury
                 ◦  Its purpose is to remove the cause and consequence of cellular
                       injury

           What are the four cardinal signs of acute inflammation?
             •  Redness (rubor)
             •  Heat (calor)
             •  Swelling (tumor)
             •  Pain (dolor)
           What can be a fifth cardinal sign of acute inflammation?
             •  Loss of function (a result of unresolved inflammation)
              Explain the molecular, cellular and physiological processes underlying each
              of the cardinal features of inflammation.
             • Redness - increased blood flow due to vasodilation means more red
                blood cells are present at site of inflammation
             •  Swelling - Fluid builds up at the site of inflammation due to increased
                  vascular permeability that causes fluid leakage into interstitial fluid
             •  Heat - body fluid that is at body temperature (~37C) is exposed to site
                  of inflammation, which is usually not exposed to site in the steady state
             •  Pain - the inflammatory mediators released by mast cells also drive
                 pain stimulation on neurons
                 ◦  i.e histamines and cytokines
             •  Loss of function - happens in certain area. i.e inflammation can cause
               a barrier in the lungs which can lead to inefficient gas exchange.

           What is acute inflammation?
             • A rapid, non-specific response to cellular injury.

              Recall two cell types found in the interstitium of normal vascularised tissue

                 ◦  There is decreased blood flow caused by vasodilation.

                      slower, explaining the decrease in flow
             •  Structural changes in the microvasculature (vasodilation, increased
                 permeability)
             •  Recruitment/accumulation of immune cells and proteins.

             Describe what happens in neutrophil extravasation.
             •  Blood is flowing in a particular direction in the capillary. This blood flow
                 obviously carries the neutrophil in the same direction as it
                   (neutrophil is in blood)
             • A macrophage/mast cell at the site of infection in the interstitium
                produces cytokines after it is activated which are used to upregulate
                  selectins (adhesion molecules)
                 ◦  Selectins are proteins/adhesion molecules on the endothelial
                    surface that leukocytes bind onto as they roll along in the
                    bloodstream. ROLLING ADHESION
             •  Cytokines then cause production of chemokines either by signalling
                 to macrophages or endothelial cells
             • Chemokines are presented by proteoglycans on the endothelial
                surface and they bind to the neutrophil
             • Chemokines binding to the neutrophil increases the LFA-1 integrin’s
                    affinity to a high affinity conformation
                 ◦  Integrins are also proteins/adhesion molecules on the endothelial
                     surface
             • The integrin ligand (ICAM-1) binds the neutrophil very tightly, and
             now it is moving towards the interstitium. TIGHT ADHESION
             • The neutrophil then undergoes transmigration via endothelial cell-
                   to-cell junctions into the site of infection at the interstitium
                 ◦  This process is mediated by PECAM-1 and involves cytoskeletal
                   rearrangement

         How do multiple neutrophils accumulate at the site of infection after one
              arrives?
             • One or two arrive at the site of infection, before they secrete
                chemoattractants which allow other neutrophils to reach the site
                  too.

           What do neutrophils do at the site of inflammation?
             • Once the neutrophils have rolled via adhesion to the site of infection,
                 they are ready to phagocytose the pathogen at hand

             Describe what happens in neutrophilic phagocytosis.
             • The pathogenic particles are engulfed into phagocytic vesicles to
                form what is known as phagosomes
             • Phagosomes fuse with the lysosome to form a phagolysosome
               (enzymes in the lysosome break down the pathogen)
             •  Reactive oxygen species (ROS) can also disable/neutralise the
                pathogen.
                 ◦  i.e Phagocyte NADPH oxidase
           What is the role of reactive oxygen species (ROS) found in leukocytes?
             •  Reactive oxygen species (ROS) can also disable/neutralise pathogens.
           What additional thing do neutrophils do after phagocytosis, to allow them to
              further phagocytose more pathogens?
             • They release their nucleus and the DNA causes further agglutination
                  of the other pathogens present at the site of infection.
             •  This makes them more easily phagocytosed by other neutrophils.

             Describe two other distinctions between acute and chronic inflammation.
             •  There is often persistent toxic stimuli that are seen commonly in
                 chronic inflammation which causes a vicious cycle where there is no
                clearance of inflammatory agent and resolution to steady state
                 ◦  This lack of resolution can be caused by tuberculosis, Hepatitis
                  B/C or persistent toxic stimuli
             •  There can also be bystander tissue damage in chronic inflammation
             •  There can also be concurrent repair processes such as fibrosis and
                angiogenesis

           What is meant by bystander tissue damage in chronic inflammation?
             •  This is when the chemicals that are released by the cells in the
             immune response actually cause damage to the host cell tissue.

         Why does fibrosis and angiogenesis occur in chronic inflammation?
             • The vicious cycle causes repeated damage to blood vessels which
                 leads to generation of new ones (angiogenesis)
             •  Tissue scarring (fibrosis) is caused by prolonged action of
              macrophages to try and repair the damage

         How can T cells cause adverse effects in chronic inflammation?

                 ◦  This leads to bystander tissue damage
