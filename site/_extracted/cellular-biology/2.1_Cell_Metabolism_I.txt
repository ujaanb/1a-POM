    2.1 Cell Metabolism I

            Basics/Warm-Up

              What is metabolism?
                 •  Metabolism describes the key processes that occur within our
                    bodies that keep us alive

              What is a catabolic reaction?
                 • A reaction that involves the breakdown of a larger molecule into
                      smaller ones
                 •   It typically releases energy which is stored within ATP
              What is an anabolic reaction?
                 • A reaction that involves the formation of a larger molecule from
                      smaller ones.
                 •   It typically requires the input of energy from ATP
              What is the cytosol?
                 • The liquid part of the cytoplasm in a cell.
              Where does the term glycolysis originate from?
                 •  Glyco (sugar) lysis (breaking)
                 •  Glycolysis is hence the breakdown of sugar (glucose) into two
                    molecules of pyruvate.

           What is ligation?
             • The joining of two nucleic acid fragments through the action of an
              enzyme.

           What is a kinase?
             • A kinase is an enzyme that always catalyses the addition of
               phosphate groups to other molecules

           What happens in the first stage of glycolysis?
             •  Glucose —> Glucose-6-phosphate
             • ATP —> ADP + Pi
             •  Catalysed by hexokinase
           What happens in the second stage of glycolysis?
             •  Glucose-6-phosphate changes to fructose-6-phosphate in an
                  isomerisation reaction
             •  Catalysed by phosphoglucose isomerase.
           What is the purpose of this second stage of glycolysis?
             •   It happens because fructose can split into equal halves when cleaved
             •  Glucose, on the other hand, cannot
           What happens in the third stage of glycolysis?
             •  Fructose-6-phosphate —> Fructose 1,6-bisphosphate
             • ATP —> ADP + Pi
             •  Catalysed by phosphofructokinase
           What happens in the fourth stage of glycolysis?
             •  Fructose-1-6-bisphosphate —> Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate
               (GALP) + Dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP)
             •  Catalysed by aldolase
           What happens in the fifth stage of glycolysis?
             •  Dihydroxyacetone phosphate —> Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate
             •  Catalysed by triose phosphate isomerase (TPI)

           What happens in the sixth stage of glycolysis?
             •  Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate —> 1,3-bisphosphoglycerate
             • NAD+ —> NADH + Pi
             •  Inorganic phosphate is also added to glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate, to
                 prepare it for substrate-level phosphorylation of two molecules of ATP,
                 not one.
             •  Catalysed by glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase
             •  This enzyme needs NAD+ to function, hence glycolysis is reliant on the
                presence of NAD+ to go ahead, otherwise this step will not proceed
               and pyruvate won’t be produced.

           What happens in the seventh stage of glycolysis?
             •  1,3-bisphosphoglycerate —> 3-phosphoglycerate
             • ADP + Pi —> ATP
             •  Catalysed by phosphoglycerate kinase
           What happens in the eighth stage of glycolysis?
             •  3-phosphoglycerate —> 2-phosphoglycerate
             •  Catalysed by phosphoglycerate mutase
           What happens in the ninth stage of glycolysis?
             •  2-phosphoglycerate —> Phosphoenolpyruvate + H2O
             •  Catalysed by enolase
           What happens in the tenth stage of glycolysis?
             •  Phosphoenolpyruvate —> Pyruvate
             • ADP + Pi —> ATP
             •  Catalysed by pyruvate kinase
           What are the net products of glycolysis?
             • 2x pyruvate
             • 2x NADH
             • 2x ATP, 4x produced by 2x pyruvate, but 2 were used initially to form
                 glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate.

             Describe briefly what the pentose-phosphate pathway is.

           What are the three fates of pyruvate?
             •  Alcoholic fermentation - yeast
             •  Lactate production - human anaerobic
             •  Acetyl CoA production - human aerobic
           What reactions occur in alcoholic fermentation?
             •  Ethanol is ultimately produced from pyruvate in a two-stage reaction





           What role do alcoholic fermentation and lactate production play in cell
            metabolism?
             •  Regenerating NAD+
             • The lactate and ethanol produced by these reactions are “waste”
                products, the main aim of the reactions is to regenerate NAD+ from
           NADH to keep ATP production going
             •  This is mainly important in anaerobic conditions as NADH is not
                 useful to us in states of hypoxia, as it is used in oxidative
                  phosphorylation, so regeneration of NAD+ allows for ATP production
                  via glycolysis
                 ◦ NAD+ can be used in the 6th step of glycolysis (glyceraldehyde-
                   3-phosphate —> 1,3-glyceraldehyde bisphosphate) which can
                    subsequently help us to produce ATP in the remaining reactions
                      of glycolysis

         How does creatine phosphate function in metabolism?
             •  Creatine phosphate has one phosphate group removed from it to
                  return to its original creatine state and phosphorylate the ADP to form
             ATP
             •   It is used as ATP is usually in high demand during exercise + muscle
                  contractions and so it acts as a reservoir for these demanding
                  conditions

           What is the acetyl CoA produced by pyruvate used for?
             • The TCA Cycle.
           What is the role of the enzyme lactate dehydrogenase?
             •  Catalyses the reduction of pyruvate to lactate and vice-versa
             •  This reaction regenerates NAD+ which allows glycolysis to continue
                  (step 6)
                 ◦ NADH is not useful to us in states of hypoxia, as it is used in
                       oxidative phosphorylation, so regeneration of NAD+ allows for
                 ATP production in glycolysis
