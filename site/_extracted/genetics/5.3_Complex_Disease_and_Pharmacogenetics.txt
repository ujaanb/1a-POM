           What is a Mendelian trait?
             • A trait that is controlled by a single gene. Put simply, it is not a
               complex trait.
             •  i.e eye colour
           What is a complex trait?
             • A trait that is controlled by multiple genes + the effect of the
               environment.
             •  i.e height, weight, intelligence, blood pressure.
         Why is sickle cell disease thought to be more prevalent amongst those of
             Afro-Carribean descent?
             • The sickle cell phenotype was thought to have given protection
                  against malaria.

           What does the term heritability describe?
             •   It describes the level of contribution that genetics has to any one
                given phenotype.
                 ◦  i.e 0% genetic to 100% genetic
                 ◦  Most phenotypes fall somewhere in between this scale.
           What is the difference between monozygotic and dizygotic twins?
             •  Monozygotic twins arise from the fertilisation of one egg by one
                sperm, and this zygote splitting into two
                 ◦  These are identical twins as they have the same genomes.
             •  Dizygotic twins arise from the fertilisation of two eggs by two sperms,
                   resulting in the formation of two zygotes
                 ◦   It is effectively the same as having two siblings who are born at
                    the same time instead of a couple of years apart.
                 ◦  These twins are described as fraternal twins.

         How are twin studies used to estimate heritability?
             •  Phenotypes are observed in both monozygotic and dizygotic twins
             • The difference in phenotypic expression between monozygotic
                twins (who have the same genome) as well as dizygotic twins is
                observed and then we can estimate heritability
                 ◦  i.e if a trait is identical in monozygotic but not in dizygotic twins,
                    then we can estimate that heritability of this trait is pretty high

           Which big underlying assumption may undermine the validity of twin study
              results?
             •  That the environmental differences between the two sets of twins
                are identical
             •  There are so many environmental differences between the two sets of
                 twins that it is almost impossible to account for this in the studies.

           What is the most common type of mutation in the human genome?
             •  Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms (SNPs)
                 ◦  Each SNP represents a difference in a single DNA building block,
                       called a nucleotide

         How do association studies work?
             •  They work by analysing the concordance between a particular SNP
                 (mutation) and risk of a disease
             • A group of controls (people without the disease) and sufferers are
               assembled
             • We then look for the level of correlation between the type of SNPs
                 present in the population and how frequently these SNPs cause
                disease phenotypes.

             Describe how the genetic causes of aggression were studied using
              association studies.
             •  There was an X-linked mutation noticed in the gene coding for
              monoamine oxidase (MAOA)
             •  This mutation led to monoamines such as adrenaline, noradrenaline,
                  serotonin, dopamine not being metabolised
             • The presence of the mutation was associated with aggressive
                 behaviour in carriers of the mutation

         Why are association studies limited?
             •  Because there is such a great focus on specific mutations having
                   specific effects, we may miss out something else that could be a
                 causative factor
             •  There is also significant bias to causative factors that we know of (i.e
                  there may be a causative factor that science hasn’t discovered yet,
                   that is actually responsible for phenotypic changes)
             •  Correlation does not equal causation

           What are genome-wide association studies (GWAS)?
             •   It is a hypothesis-free study used to identify as many SNPs as possible
                   within a wider section of the genome
             •  i.e there is no hypothesis (X causes Y), but we are simply trying to find
                         all the different SNPs in the genome which may suggest causation





              Recall two benefits of GWAS over association studies.
             •  They aren’t limited to what we know - we may discover more
                  biological mechanisms
             • We can identify individuals at risk of certain conditions due to the
                presence of particular SNPs.

              Recall two limitations of GWAS.
             •   It doesn’t identify causal relationships between SNPs and phenotypes.
             •   It doesn’t account for environmental changes to the genome
                 (epigenetics)
             •   It can be hard to predict heritability.
             • The threshold set may also mean we miss out on identifying very rare
              SNPs

           What can whole genome sequencing (WGS) give us over GWAS?
             • WGS allows us to analyse the entire genome which helps us to
                   identify every SNP

           What does the term pharmacogenetics refer to?
             • The study of variability in responses to drugs as a result of genetic
                 differences

           What does the term pharmacokinetics refer to?
             •   It describes the how the body acts on a particular drug





             Describe how mutations in proteins involved in drug absorption can lead to
            adverse effects.
             •   It can lead to unusually high or low levels of the drug accumulating
                   in the blood which means that doses that are usually of the right level
                     in normal people, become all of a sudden too high or low in these
                    individuals.
                 ◦ The effects can be mitigated by administering higher or lower
                   doses of said drug.
                 ◦  i.e mutations in absorption proteins in the duodenum affecting
                   drug absorption
             • SNPs can lead to changes in enzyme activity which can alter the
                  activation/inactivation of different drugs after consumption

           What is a prodrug?
             • A drug that doesn’t actually have a direct effect on the user itself,
                 but is instead metabolised to another substance which can have the
                desired effect.
             •  i.e codeine is a prodrug that is metabolised to morphine.
         Why is codeine used instead of morphine directly?
             •  Codeine has less depressive and sedative side effects
