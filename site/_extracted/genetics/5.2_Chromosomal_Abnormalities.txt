           What happens in meiosis I?
             •  Each normal adult cell should have 46 chromosomes. In interphase,
                    this is replicated to 92 chromosomes
             • Homologous chromosomes (i.e chromosomes of the same number)
                    line up and are ready for cell division
                 ◦  Note at this stage, there are four copies of each chromosome
                             (i.e one paternal + one maternal has become two paternal + two
                     maternal after interphase)
             •  Crossing over occurs between homologous chromosomes from the
               mother and father which leads to all chromosomes becoming
                  genetically distinct
             • The cells split in half, forming two daughter cells each with 46
              chromosomes
                 ◦  Each daughter cell would have 23 paternal and 23 maternal
                 chromosomes (though due to crossing over there is now paternal
                  and maternal DNA on each chromosome)

           What happens in meiosis II?
             • The cells split in half again now, and four daughter cells result, each
                with 23 chromosomes (haploid)
                 ◦ Each daughter cell would have a mixture of paternal and
                    maternal chromosomes to allow genetic variation (via random
                      assortment), but would have 23 chromosomes, ready to fuse
                    with another haploid gamete to form a diploid zygote
             • Chromosome from meiosis one further splits into two, forming two
                daughter cells each with one chromatid.

             Describe what causes chromosome aneuploidy.
             •  This is when the chromosomes from a parent cell do not split equally
                    into daughter cells (in meiosis I or II), which results in an abnormal
              number of chromosomes within a daughter cell
             •  This process is called non-disjunction, when the chromosomes aren’t
                separated in the correct and normal manner.

           What can X-inactivation of chromosomes lead to?
             •   It can lead to only one of the X chromosomes remaining active and the
                   rest (excess) being inactivated
             •   It occurs in those with sex chromosome aneuploidy
           What is a pseudo-autosomal region (PAR)?
             •   It is a region that can be present on an X or Y chromosome that can
               remain active even after inactivation

         Why is it thought that females are more susceptible to autoimmune diseases
             than males?
             • A lot of the genes from the pseudo-autosomal regions that escape X
              chromosome inactivation are involved in the immune system, so
                females have more of these, hence leading to increased susceptibility
                  to autoimmune diseases.

           What is trisomy-21?
             • A karyotype that involves three chromosomes on the 21st pair instead
                  of two

           What maternal factor can influence the likelihood of a baby having trisomy-
            21?
             • The risk of maternal non-disjuncture increases with age,
                    significantly increasing the probability of their offspring developing
                  trisomy-21.





         Why is this the case?
             •   It is likely due to the degradation of the factors that hold the
              homologous chromosomes in the middle of the spindle bundle, that
                occurs with age.
                 ◦ The chromosomes aren’t held in the middle of the cell, and this
                     leads to an increased likelihood of cell division in meiosis resulting
                          in an abnormal number of chromosomes in resulting
                  chromosomes
                 ◦  Basically, oogenesis is faulty as women age.
             •  Also, the development of a primary oocyte → secondary oocyte does
                 not occur until a woman’s egg is fertilised and it is arrested in prophase
                 ◦  This means the egg has more time to accumulate damage, and
                     abnormalities are increasingly likely

           What is Robertsonian translocation?
             •  Robertsonian translocations are a specific class of translocations in
               which two acrocentric chromosomes fuse at their centric ends
             •   It can occur on chromosomes 13,14,15 and 21 and can cause
               problems in offspring.
             •   It involves part or the entirety of these chromosomes being moved
               onto another chromosome
