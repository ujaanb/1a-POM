    5.7 Genetic Testing

           What is a nuchal scan?
             •   It is one of the two main types of ultrasound scan used for assessing
                  abnormalities in infants

          When do pregnant women undergo nuchal scans?
             •  10-14 weeks.
              Recall three purposes of nuchal scans.
             •  To date the pregnancy accurately
             •  To diagnose multiple pregnancy (i.e twins, triplets)
             •  To diagnose major fetal anomalies
             •  To diagnose early miscarriages
             •  To assess the chance of Down’s syndrome and any other
              chromosomal abnormalities

             Describe how a nuchal scan works.
             •   It uses Nuchal Translucency (NT) which measures the thickness of
                    fluid at the back of a baby’s neck
             •   If the thickness is >3mm, this can indicate chromosomal
                 abnormalities





          When is an anomaly scan undertaken?
             • 20 weeks.

           What is the purpose of an anomaly scan?
             •  To look for structural anomalies specifically in the heart, brain, spinal
                 cord, face, kidney and abdomen
             •  To measure the lengths of the bones
           What is prenatal testing?
             •  Testing that is undertaken during a pregnancy (before birth) on babies
           What is the purpose of prenatal testing?
             •  Following results of the two ultrasound scans, if the combined risk
                 score is over 1/150, then prenatal testing is used to confirm the
                 abnormalities present

          When can prenatal testing be used?
             • Combined risk score of baby is over 1/150
             •  Family history of genetic condition
             •  Parents are carriers of risk genes
             •  Previous pregnancy has been affected by a condition
             •  Mainly to check the genotype of the baby for a particular trait they are
                   at risk of

           What is non-invasive prenatal diagnosing (NIPD)?
             •  Analysis of the DNA fragments present in the maternal plasma during
               pregnancy
             •  Different things can be learned from these DNA fragments
             • Most of the DNA is from the mother, but 10-20% comes from the
                placenta which is representative of the fetus
                 ◦  the fetal DNA is in the form of cell-free DNA and will contain both
                     the maternal and paternal DNA





          When is non-invasive prenatal diagnosing (NIPD) undertaken?
             •  Around 9 weeks.
         How does NIPD fetal sexing work?
             •  To look for the SRY gene which is only present on Y chromosomes
             •  Hence, if the fetal DNA has the SRY gene, we know that it is a male
             Describe how NIPD is used in genetic testing.
             •  Because NIPD involves blood from the mother mainly, we are looking
                    for genes from the father
             •  Autosomal dominant single gene disorders inherited from fathers are
                searched for
             •  Recessive genes from the father can also be looked for if the mother
               and father have different versions of this gene
             •  X-linked conditions in males can be used to determine which X-
              chromosome they inherited from mother.

             Describe how NIPT is used in detecting aneuploidy.
             •   It is used as a screening technique, not diagnosis
             •   If one is found to have a high risk of aneuploidy, invasive prenatal
                   testing is used to confirm it.

              Recall two limitations of NIPD/NIPT.
             •   If there are twins, can’t tell whose DNA possesses which genes.
             • Women with a high BMI have lower proportions of cell free fetal DNA,
               and so it is hard to use NIPD for them
             •  Invasive testing may be required to confirm abnormal result
              Recall two benefits of NIPD/NIPT.
             • No increased risk of miscarriage
             •  Less expertise required to perform a blood test than an invasive test
           What is purpose of the main invasive tests (CVS/amniocentesis)?
             •  Invasive testing is offered when there is a known risk of disorder
                 within the fetus
             •  This could be due to family history or NIPD/NIPT results
          When is chorionic villus sampling undertaken?
             •  12-14 weeks
         How is chorionic villus sampling undertaken?
             • A sample of chorionic villi (part of developing placenta) is taken from
                 the woman and analysed
                 ◦  This region has the same DNA as fetus

              Recall two risks of chorionic villus sampling.
             • 0.5% risk of miscarriage
             •  Infection
             • Rhesus (Rh group) sensitisation
                 ◦  maternal exposure to fetal blood cells
          When is amniocentesis undertaken?
             •  15 weeks.
             •   It takes sample of amniotic fluid which contains fetal cells
              Recall two risks of amniocentesis.
             • 0.5% risk of miscarriage
             •  Infection
             •  Rhesus sensitisation (foreign Rh group exposure to woman)
           What can be done with the DNA samples obtained from these invasive tests?
             •  Monogenic disorders can be tested for
             •  Karyotype analysis
             • QF-PCR
                 ◦  tests for trisomy 13, 18, 21
           What is a comparative genomic hybridization (CGH) array and when is it
            used?
             • A comparative genomic hybridization (CGH) array is a test used to
                determine the abnormalities present in the genome of a fetus
             •   It is used when abnormalities are suspected from the 20-week scan.
             •   It looks for small/large imbalances in chromosomes (picks up
                  microdeletions and microduplications)

           What is a prenatal exome R21 test?
             • A test used to examine where exactly the fetus has had significant
                anomalies (i.e kidney, heart, brain, skeletal)
             • The exome is the coding region of the genome
             •   It happens at 20+ weeks of pregnancy
             •   It is not always conclusive
