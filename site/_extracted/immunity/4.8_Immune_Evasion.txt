   4.8 Immune Evasion

           What is the complement system?
             • A group of about 20 plasma proteins crucial to the innate immune
                response used to opsonise or lyse bacteria.
             •  Their goal is always to either opsonise bacteria via C3b or to use
             membrane attack complexes to kill the bacteria.

           What are the three pathways involved in the complement system?
             •  Classical pathway
             •  Alternative pathway
             •  Lectin pathway

             Describe the events that occur in the classical pathway.
             •  Antigen reacts with antibody which activates the C1 component
             • C1 breaks down C4 into C4a and C4b
                 ◦ C1 combines with C4b to form C14b
             • C14b breaks down C2 into C2a and C2b
                 ◦ C14b combines with C2a to form C3 convertase (C14b2a)
             • C3 convertase converts C3 into C3a and C3b
                 ◦ C3 convertase combines with C3b to form C5 convertase
                  (C14b2a3b)
             • C5 convertase converts C5 into C5a and C5b
             • C5b is used to combine with C6,C7,C8 and C9 to form the Membrane
                Attack Complex (MAC or C5b6789)





           Which two antibodies can initiate the classical pathway?
             •  IgM and IgG

             Describe the events that occur in the lectin pathway.
             •  Chemicals called lectins detect glycans on the surfaces of microbes
             •  This leads to the same cascade (C3 and C5 convertase formation)
                which ends up in the same response.

           What is the main difference between the classical and lectin pathways?
             •  Lectin pathway involves the activation of C4b and C2a via binding of
                  lectins to sugars
             •  Classical pathway involves the activation of C4b and C2a via binding
                 of antibodies to antigens

           What is a bacterial endotoxin?
             • A lipopolysaccharide found in the outer membrane of gram-negative
                 bacteria, used to trigger the alternative pathway of the complement
                 system.

             Describe the events that occur in the alternative pathway.
             • C3 is spontaneously hydrolysed to C3b
                 ◦  This occurs continuously, leading to regeneration of C3b.
             •  Factor Bb binds to Factor C3b and this forms a C3 convertase
              (C3bBb)
             • The C5 convertase is then generated which allows the MAC to be
                 generated.

           What role does C3b play in the immune response?
             •   It activates neutrophils and macrophages which allow them to
               phagocytose easier
             •  This is opsonisation so C3b can be referred to as an opsonin
             Describe the role of the Membrane Attack Complex (MAC) in the
           complement system.
             •  This ruptures the bacterial cell wall which causes fluid influx into the
                   bacterial cell and eventual lysis.

             Describe how the MAC is formed from a C5b molecule.
             • C5 convertase binds to the bacterial cell wall
             • C5 convertase produces C5b which also binds to the bacterial cell wall
             •  This recruits C6, C7, C8 and C9 to make the MAC

         How does C3b interact with phagocytic cells to allow opsonisation of
            pathogens?
             • C3b binds onto pathogenic surfaces (antigens)
             • The phagocytic cells can recognise the C3b using its complement
                receptor and this sends a signal to begin phagocytosis

         How do antibodies interact with phagocytic cells to allow opsonisation of
            pathogens?
             •  Antibodies (IgG/IgM) bind onto the antigen
             • The phagocytic cell uses its Fc receptor to recognise the antibody and
                     initiate a phagocytic response.

           What is MRSA?
             •  Methicillin-resistant staphylococcus aureus.
           What are the four methods used by bacteria to evade the immune system?
             •  Degrade C3
             •  Inhibit C1 binding
             •  Inhibit C3 and C5 convertases
             •  Inhibit C5
         How does S. aureus act directly on complement factor C3?
             •   It releases proteases (Aur) that degrade C3 which prevents C3a and
             C3b formation
             • C3b is an important opsonin so the effects of this evasion are
                 increased risk of infection.

         How does S. aureus prevent the formation of C3 convertase?
             •  Sbi proteins are released by bacteria which bind to antibodies
             •  This means that C1 cannot recognise the bacteria and so there is no
                opsonisation
             • C1 not binding to the bacteria means there is a lack of C4 and C2
                 conversion which is required to form C3 convertase (C4b2a)





         How does S. aureus inhibit C3 and C5 convertases?
             •   It produces SCIN which binds to C3bBb (alternative pathway form of
             C3 convertase)
             •  This reduces its enzymatic capacity meaning C3 is not converted
                which also prevents formation of C5 convertase.

         How does S. aureus inhibit C5?
             •   It releases SSL7 which binds to C5 and prevent it from converting

         How does the capsule polysaccharide work in bacterial immune evasion?

             •  This prevents the antigens from being recognised by the antibodies as

         How do bacteria interact with Fc regions in immune evasion?
             • The bacteria can produce proteins (Protein A) which bind onto the
               Fc region of the antibodies that bind to their antigens
                 ◦  Normally, antibodies would use their Fab regions to bind
                    pathogens, and Fc receptors in opsonisation to phagocytic cells
             •  There is obviously less opsonisation as a result of this
             • NOTE: Antibody-mediated opsonization: In this process, Fab (fragment
                 antigen binding) region of the antibody binds to the antigen, and the
               Fc region of the antibody binds to the Fc receptor on the phagocytic
                   cells and helps phagocytic cells, which include neutrophils,
                   eosinophils, macrophages, and natural killer cells





         How does staph. aureus cleave antibodies?
             •   It breaks down plasminogen into plasmin which can be used to digest
                 antibodies
             • Some bacteria directly release the proteases which digest antibodies.
