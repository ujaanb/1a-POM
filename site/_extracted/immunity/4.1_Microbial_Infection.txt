    4.1 Microbial Infection

           What are the five main microbial agents?
             •  Viruses
             •  Bacteria
             •  Fungi
             •  Protozoa
             •  Helminths
           What type of organisms are each of these agents?
             •  Viruses - not cells in their own right
             •  Bacteria - prokaryotes (no distinct nucleus)
             •  Fungi - (single-celled) eukaryotes
             •  Protozoa - (single-celled) eukaryotes
             •  Helminths - (multicellular) eukaryotes
         Why are viruses described as ‘obligate parasites?’
             •  Because they have to infect hosts and use their material in order to
                reproduce and survive.
             •  This means they are not found anywhere else in the environment other
                 than in hosts.

           What are the two mechanisms used by viruses to divide?
             •  Cytolysis or budding out of cells
           What is the difference between cytolysis and budding?
             •  Cytolysis is a method of viral particle release after replication which
                causes the death of the infected host cell
             •  Cytopathy is a method of viral particle release after replication which
              does not cause the death of a host cell, but releases particles via
                budding.

           What advantage does budding have over cytolysis?
             • The virus can continue to divide and replicate whilst the host does not
                 recognise it as a pathogen.

           What is a retrovirus?
             • A virus that has an RNA genome.
             •  This means it requires reverse transcriptase.

           What is one of the most important steps to take in eradicating an infectious
             disease?
             •  Implementing an efficient vaccine programme.
           What is special about the genetic makeup of prokaryotes?
             •  They contain one copy of each chromosome, instead of two like us
             •  This means that a mutation to a gene is increasingly likely to have
                 serious phenotypic effects.

           What is different between the cytoskeleton of prokaryotes and eukaryotes?
             •  Prokaryotes have a poor cytoskeleton (most of it comes from the
                 peptidoglycan cell wall)
             •  They don’t have the actin cytoskeleton
         How do prokaryotes divide?
             • By binary fission.
           What is the difference between gram-negative and gram-positive bacteria?
             •  Gram-positive bacteria have thicker cell walls with more
                peptidoglycan than gram negative bacteria
             •  This can be seen because the gram-positive bacterial cell walls retain
                the purple crystal violet stain when it is applied but gram-negative
              do not
             •  Gram-negative bacteria have peptidoglycan which is sandwiched
               between two membranes, but there is only one of these membranes
                   in gram-positive bacteria.

           What role does the capsule play in bacteria?
             •   It can allow them to escape phagocytosis by hosts as well as other
                  destructive immune processes as well as protecting them from
                  desiccation (drying up)

           What role does the pili play in bacteria?
             •   It can be used to attach to certain surfaces
         How can bacteria move other than using their flagella?
             •  Using the actin cytoskeleton
              Recall two hospital acquired bacterial infections.
             •  Clostridium difficile (C. diff)
             •  Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA)
         How do bacterial genomic mutations spread faster than human genomic
             mutations, despite the fact that both organisms have the same point
             mutation rates (mutations per number of genes)?
             • The rate at which bacteria reproduce is astronomically faster than
              humans - they have a shorter generation time
             • A new bacterial generation can take as short as 20 minutes to develop,
                 but in humans, the average generation takes about 26 years to form.
             •  This means that mutations that occur in bacteria are spread to
                  offspring much more frequently, compared to in humans where the
                spread can take a number of years.
             •  Also, the haploid nature of bacterial cells means that a mutation
                spreads far more easily to offspring and can have phenotypic effects
              more easily, compared to in humans where we have copies of each
               gene from mum and dad

           What is a cutaneous infection?
             • One that has its effect through the deep tissue in the skin
             •  For example, it can cause swelling or deep inflammation of skin tissues
           What is a visceral infection?
             • One that has its effect through the organs in the body
         How do protozoa divide?
             • By binary fission or trophozoite formation
             •  Trophozoites are growing stages which result in bursting and the
                  release of protozoa into the environment.
