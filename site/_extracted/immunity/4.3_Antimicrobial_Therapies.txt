           What is prontosil?
             •   It the first example of a sulphonamide antibiotic
              Recall two other properties of prontosil.
             •  Bacteriostatic
             •  Synthetic
           What is prontosil used for?
             •  Urinary Tract Infections (UTIs)
             •  Respiratory Tract Infections (RTIs)
             •  Bacteraemia (bacteria in the blood)
           What are sulphonamide antibiotics?
             • A class of broad-spectrum antibiotics used to treat various types of
                   bacterial infections.

           What is a drawback of prontosil?
             •   It is only effective against gram-positive bacteria.
           What property of gram-negative bacteria makes them more difficult to treat
             with antibiotics?
             • The presence of an outer and inner membrane in gram-negative
                  bacteria makes things difficult
             •  This is because the outer membrane has different properties to the
                  inner membrane so requires different treatment to break down.

           What is a beta-lactam?
             • The most commonly prescribed antibiotic
             Describe the mechanism through which beta-lactams function.
             •  They bind to structures on the bacterial cell wall called penicillin
                binding proteins
             •  This interferes with the synthesis of the peptidoglycan component of
               a bacterial cell wall

             Give two examples of beta-lactams.
             •  Penicillin
             •  Methicillin
           What are penicillin-binding proteins?
             •  These are proteins involved in the synthesis of peptidoglycan (an
                 important component of bacterial cell walls)
             •  Beta-lactams bind to these, to inhibit peptidoglycan synthesis and
               hence weaken bacterial cell walls

           What is an antibiotic?
             • An antimicrobial agent produced by a microorganism that kills or
                   inhibits other microorganisms
             •  Most antibiotics used today are produced by fungi or bacteria.
             Describe the difference between bactericidal and bacteriostatic antibiotics.
             •  Bactericidal antibiotics kill bacteria
             •  Bacteriostatic antibiotics inhibit bacterial growth
           What is an antiseptic?
             • An antimicrobial substance used to prevent the growth of microbial
                agents.

           What is a patient-induced cause of antibiotic resistance?
             •  Underdosing
             •  Using an inadequate quantity of antibiotic which results in the
                  survival of bacteria.

           What is the minimal inhibitory concentration (MIC)?
             • The lowest concentration of an antibiotic that is required to inhibit
                  bacterial growth.
             •  You can look at it as the maximum concentration of antibiotic that a
                bacterium can grow in

         Why is antibiotic resistance more prevalent in hospitals than in the
           community?
             •  Antibiotics are used much more frequently in hospitals, and so the
                 bacteria is subject to a much heavier selection pressure in this
                environment
             •  This means it is much more likely for the prevalence of an
               advantageous mutation to increase in popularity in hospitals, than in
                 the community as they are being selected for.

           What is meant by horizontal gene transfer in bacteria?
             • The exchange of DNA between bacteria in the same generation.
             •   It is a mechanism through which bacteria can resist antibiotics.
           What can antibiotic resistance lead to from a clinical point of view?
             •  Increased mortality (death)
             •  Increased morbidity (illness)

             •  Protein and phospholipid.

             •  Staphylococcus aureus - wound/skin infections, pneumonia,

             •  Streptococcus pneumoniae - Pneumonia, septicaemia

           What are aminoglycosides?
             •  They are bactericidal antibiotics

             •  They target protein synthesis by causing the wrong amino acids and
               hence the wrong membrane proteins to be produced
             •  This leads to the death of bacterial cells as damage has been done to
                 the cell membrane

           What is rifampmicin?
             •  They are bactericidal antibiotics
             Describe the mechanism through which rifampmicin functions.
             •   It targets the RpoB subunit of RNA polymerase
             •  This leads to protein synthesis not proceeding smoothly and hence
                 the death of the cell

              Recall a side effect of rifampmicin.
             •   It makes secretions go red/orange
             •  This leads to problems encouraging patients to comply with the full
                course

           What is vancomycin?
             •   It is a bactericidal antibiotic
             Describe the mechanism through which vancomycin functions.
             •  Targets cell wall biosynthesis
           What is linezolid?
             •   It is a bacteriostatic antibiotic that works against gram-positive
                   bacteria.

             Describe the mechanism through which linezolid functions.
             •   It inhibits the initiation of protein synthesis by binding to the 50S
                   (smaller) ribosomal subunit

           What is daptomycin?
             •   It is a bacteriostatic antibiotic that works against gram-positive
                  bacteria

             Describe the mechanism through which daptomycin functions.
             •  Targets bacterial cell membranes
           What is an important consideration to make when prescribing linezolid and
            daptomycin?
             •  They only work on gram-positive bacteria.
           What is meant when antibiotics are described as selectively toxic?
             •   It means that they are selectively more toxic (~ 100 times more) to
                   bacterial cells than to human cells
             •  This is because bacterial cells are significantly distinct to human
                   cells

           What are the four mechanisms through which antibiotic resistance can
            occur?
             •  Altered target site (antibiotic can no longer engage its target)
             •  Inactivation of antibiotic
             •  Altered metabolism
             •  Decreased drug accumulation
           What are the two ways bacteria can obtain altered target sites?
             •  Acquisition of a gene that encodes for a different target
             •  Acquisition of a gene that encodes for a target-modifying enzyme
                which makes antibiotic ineffective against target

         How does an altered target site lead to antibiotic resistance?
             •   It can lead to antibiotics not being able to bind onto the relevant
                 target sites anymore as their structures have changed or the target
                   site has been relocated

             Describe how an altered target site in MRSA leads to antibiotic resistance.
             •  i.e MRSA can encode for an alternative penicillin-binding protein
                (PBP2a).
             •  Beta-lactams can still bind onto the original PBP, but it has no effect
                as the PBP2a can still help the MRSA carry out protein synthesis.
             • The PBP2a also has a low affinity for beta-lactams.
         How does inactivation of an antibiotic lead to antibiotic resistance?
             • The bacteria produces an enzyme which leads to the degradation or
                  alteration of the antibiotic
             •  This renders it ineffective
             • An example of this is beta-lactamase
         How does altered metabolism lead to antibiotic resistance?
             •  Because antibiotics target central metabolic processes in bacteria (i.e
                  protein synthesis), a method of bacterial evasion is to find other ways
                  of obtaining energy/materials from the environment
             •  Bacteria can also reduce reliance on substances produced from the
                 targeted pathways, so that any effect that antibiotics have are
                rendered null.

         How does decreased drug accumulation lead to antibiotic resistance?
             •  This works by pumps on the surfaces of bacterial cell membranes
                reducing the intake of antibiotic from the environment, and
                 increasing the efflux of antibiotic to the environment
             •  This means the drug does not reach the required concentration to be
                   effective against the bacterial cell.

           What is a macrolide and how does it work?
             •   It is an antibiotic used to stop polypeptide chain formation by
                binding to the 50S ribosomal subunit

           What is a quinolone and how does it work?
             • An entirely synthetic bactericidal antibiotic which inhibits DNA
                   replication.

          Name the three sources of antibiotic resistance genes.
             •  Plasmids - these can code for multiple resistance genes
             •  Transposons - Integrate DNA from chromosomes to plasmid and vice-
                 versa
             • Naked DNA - DNA released by other bacteria is taken up from the
                environment

             Describe three mechanisms of antibiotic resistance gene uptake.
             •  Transformation (uptake of extracellular DNA)
             •  Transduction (phage-mediated DNA transfer)
             •  Conjugation (pilus-mediated DNA transfer)

           What is a bacteriophage?
             • A virus that affects bacteria
             •   It is involved in the horizontal spread of DNA throughout a bacterial
                  population.

              Recall three ways that antibiotic treatment may fail.
             •  Biofilm
             •  Intracellular location
             •  Slow growth
             •  Spores
             •  Persisters
          Name three ways that non-genetic antibiotic resistance can arise within a
              population.
             •  Inappropriate drug choice for bacteria
             •  Poor penetration of antibiotic into target site
             •  Inappropriate dosage
             •  Inappropriate administration (i.e oral or intravenous)
             •  Presence of other bacteria which are able to kill/break down antibiotic -
                   collateral resistance

          Name three risk factors for hospital-acquired infections.
             •  Large number of ill people
             • Crowded wards
             •  Broken skin - easy entry
             •  Transmission by staff - poor hygiene
         How can we address antibiotic resistance to prevent it arising?
             •  Removal of selection pressures which are easy to evade (i.e broad
               spectrum antibiotics)

         How can we react to antibiotic resistance in humans?
             •  Modifying antibiotics to make them more effective
             •  Combinations of an antibiotic and inhibitor of bacterial mechanism
                        (i.e PBPA2)
             • The problem is, that these are responses to resistance after it has
                 occurred.

           What are the three broad classes of condition that fungi cause in humans?

             •  Mycotoxicoses - ingestion of fungi and their toxic products

           What are three targets for antifungal agents?
             •  Cell membrane - particularly ergosterol
             • DNA synthesis - arrested by agents
