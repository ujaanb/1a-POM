   4.9 Anaphylaxis

          When are histamines and leukotrienes released?
             • When IgE on the surface of mast cells binds to antigens.
           What effects can histamines normally have on hosts?
             •  Peripheral vasodilation of the arteries
             •  This can lead to the appearance of a reddish rash in what is known as
                   urticaria

           What is the name of the histamine receptor?
             • GPCRs
          When is IgE released?
             •  In response to allergy and parasitic infections
           What effect does adrenaline have on blood vessels?
             •   It causes vasoconstriction.
           What effect do histamines have on blood vessels?
             •  They have the opposite effect, meaning they cause vasodilation and
              smooth muscle contraction
             •  This can cause difficulty breathing in sufferers caused by insufficient
                blood supply to the heart which is seen in complications such as
                asthma.

           What effect do histamines have on small intestine and lungs?
             •  Vasoconstriction
             •  Airway constriction due to smooth muscle contraction in the airways
           What test can be used to test if someone has an allergy to something?
             • A pin-prick test which will usually inject small amounts of allergen into
                 the subject to see if they produce a type of reaction known as a wheal-
                 and-flare reaction.

           What two medical complications can the inhalation of allergens cause?
             • Hay fever (allergic rhinitis)
             • Asthma
         Why is this the case?
             • When an allergen is inhaled, it will act principally on the mucosal mast
                   cells of the respiratory system

           What happens physiologically in hay fever?
             •  In hay fever, there is swelling (oedema) in the epithelia lining the nose
                which leads to general irritation and continual running of the nose.

           What happens physiologically in asthma?
             • The allergen reaches the bronchioles through inhalation and causes
               an inflammatory response in this area
             • The action of inflammatory mediators leads to the contraction of the
               smooth muscle in the airways
             •  This can leads to breathing difficulties, seen in those with asthma.
           Where does an allergen that is ingested act in the body?
             •   If the allergen is ingested, then it will act at mucosal mast cells in the
                    intestinal tract.

           What can the ingestion of allergens lead to?
             • The stimulation of the intestinal smooth muscle leads to the vomiting
              and diarrhoea typical of a food allergy.
             •   If the allergic substance can be absorbed and pass into the blood
               stream it may also cause skin symptoms (rashes or itching).

           What does the ‘shock’ part of the phrase ‘anaphylactic shock’ specifically
               refer to?
             •   It refers to the massive drop in blood pressure that occurs when there
                      is vasodilation of the blood vessels, which is obviously caused by the
                 inflammatory mediators such as histamine.
             •  This can lead to organs not receiving the oxygen they need to function,
               and hence there is a risk of death

           What effect does salbutamol have on the body?
             •   It is a bronchodilator which can be given to people suffering from
                  anaphylaxis, as there are breathing problems during asthma

           What can hypotension (low blood pressure) lead to?
             •  Fainting (insufficient blood flow to the brain)
             •  Nausea (urge to vomit)
             •  Vomiting

           What is biphasic anaphylaxis?
             • A type of allergic reaction where the symptoms disappear but then
                reappear after a short time.
             •  Patients with moderate respiratory or cardiovascular compromise
                should be observed for to up to 8-10 hours before discharge.
