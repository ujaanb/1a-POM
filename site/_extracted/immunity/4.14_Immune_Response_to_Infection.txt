              Recall three microbe associated molecular patterns (MAMPs).
             •  Lipopolysaccharides from gram-negative bacteria
             •  Peptidoglycan from all bacteria
             •  Beta-glucan cell wall on fungi
             •  Surface glycoproteins and nucleic acids from viruses
              Recall three pattern/danger associated molecular patterns (PAMPs/DAMPs).
             • ATP released from dead cells
             •  Alarmins - chemicals released as a result of cell death
             •  Specific cytokines/interleukins
         Why are natural killer cells described to be part of the innate immune
             response, even though they are lymphocytes?
             •  Because they respond very quickly and in an antigen-independent
              manner

           What is an advantage of the adaptive immune system over the innate?
             • A lot more microbe-associated molecular patterns (antigens) can be
                detected by the adaptive system
             •  About 1000 molecular patterns are detected by the innate in
                comparison to ~10^7 by the adaptive

                 Is blood sterile (lacks viruses and bacterial presence)?
             •  Yes. Blood is completely sterile and you would be in the ICU if it wasn’t.
               In the immune response, which are the first types of cells to reach the site of
              infection?
             •  Neutrophils
             •  They are followed by macrophages after neutrophilic release of
                  cytokines.

             Describe how interferon-γ can lead to the activation of macrophages.
             • The macrophage binds to a pathogen and releases cytokines, which
                  activates lymphocytes
             • Lymphocytes produce interferon-γ
             •  Interferon-γ is important as it can activate the macrophage, allowing
                            it to cause enhanced phagocytosis, increased cytokine production,
                 expression of cell surface molecules, and antigen presentation (hence
             T cell activation)
                 ◦   It also allows the macrophage to express certain antimicrobial
                  genes for molecules such as reactive oxygen species (i.e bleach
                        for killing cells)
             •  Interferon-γ is hence vital to controlling infection and the transition
                from the innate to adaptive immune response

           Which type of interferons are used only against viruses?
             • Type I (IFNα/β)
             • Type II (IFNγ) and Type III (IFNλ) also work against viruses but have
                 other functions too (Type II = antibacterial, Th1 skewing | Type III =
                      antiviral, mucosal immunity)

         Why do interferons have their name?
             •  Because they were discovered to interfere with viral replication.
           What is pyroptosis?
             • A type of cell death which involves the influx of water into cells via their
                pores which causes them to burst
             •  This leads to inflammation as well as fevers (increased body
                temperature)

             Describe how Natural Killer (NK) cells are involved in the immune response.
             •  Natural Killer cells detect infected cells and this leads to them
                   triggering apoptosis in the infected target cell.

           Which two cytokines work antagonistically in bacterial and fungal infections?
             •  Interferon-γ (IFNγ) is used for bacterial infections
             •  IL-17 is used for fungal infections
              Recall which cytokines are involved in Th1, Th2 and Th17 immunity.
             •  Th1 - IFN-γ
             • Th2 - IL-4, IL-5, IL-13
             •  Th17 - IL-17, IL-22
              Recall which type of leukocyte are targeted by these T helper cells and
            which pathogens each of these T cells are produced against.
             •  Th1 - Macrophage - intracellular pathogens (i.e viruses)
             • Th2 - Eosinophils - Helminths, parasites
             •  Th17 - Neutrophils - extracellular bacteria and fungi
