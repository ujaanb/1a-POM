   4.6 Lymphocytes

           What is the difference between innate and adaptive immune cells?
             •  Innate immune cells are involved in engulfing and killing pathogens
                    this way (i.e macrophages, neutrophils)
             •  Adaptive immune cells are involved in targeting specific regions on
                pathogens (i.e T cells, B cells)

           What are the main functions of T cells?
             •  To orchestrate an immune response (T helper) and kill infected cells
                 (T killer)

           What are the main functions of B cells?
             •  To make antibodies
         Why is adaptive immunity important?
             •  To clear initial infections and protect us from repeated exposure to
                these pathogens via memory

         Why are memory responses typically faster than primary responses?
             • The process for a naive cell to become an effector cell is one that
                  requires a lot of signalling
             •  But the process for a memory cell to respond to a familiar pathogen is
                  quite quick and doesn’t require much signalling





         How long can immune memory last for?
             • A very, very long time - can be lifelong
             •  There are cases which suggest to us that it could last for up to 60
                  years.

           What are the two types of T cell?
             • CD4
             • CD8
           What do CD4 T cells do?
             •  They produce cytokines to help shape the immune response.
           What do CD8 T cells do?
             •  They kill infected cells
           What part of a T or B cell allows it to interact with antigens?
             • The T or B cell receptor.
         How is a B or T cell activated?
             • The antigen binds onto the receptor
             •  This causes the proteins under the receptor region to bind onto
                other proteins in a signalling cascade





           What is an epitope?
             • The region on an antigen where a B or T cell receptor binds to
         How do T cells bind to antigens?
             • T cells recognise a linear sequence of ~10-15 amino acids on the
                 epitope on an MHC (major histocompatibility complex)
             •  They recognise the primary structure in the epitope
         How do B cells bind to antigens?
             •  They recognise the structure/shape of the epitope rather than the
               sequence
             •  They recognise the tertiary/quaternary structure of the epitope

           What is a cognate antigen?
             • The antigen that a specific T cell recognises
             Describe what clonal expansion is.
             • When a vaccine is given for example, there are small amounts of T cell
               and B cell produced in response to the spike proteins
             •  Clonal expansion describes how the number of these T and B cells
                 increase as they are re-exposed to their cognate antigen as they have
                  to produce an immune response.





           What is immunoglobulin gene rearrangement and why is it important?
             •   It is the process where the genes are rearranged during protein
                 production in order to increase the B and T cell receptor diversity
             •  This is because there are not enough genes to combat the large
              number of pathogens that exist globally and so this method of
               rearrangement helps to increase the number of receptors possible by
                  rearranging genes

           What happens to T cell production as we age?
             •   It decreases.
             • Once we hit the age of ~ 25-26, T cell production in the thymus
                stops so we stop making new T cells and our T cell repertoire doesn’t
                 increase in size.

             Describe three differences between MHC Class I and Class II.*
             • MHC Class I is found in all nucleated cells, but MHC Class II is only
               found in professional antigen-presenting cells
             • MHC Class I is used against intracellular pathogens, MHC Class II is
               used against extracellular pathogens
             • MHC Class I has a single variable alpha chain and a common beta-
                 microglobulin. MHC Class II has an alpha and a beta chain





           What term is used to describe MHC gene expression?
             •  Polygenic.
             •   It has genes that code for it at different loci.
           Which T cells does MHC Class I interact with?
             • CD8
           Which T cells does MHC Class II interact with?
             • CD4
           Which cells are used to produce cytokines?
             • T helper cells
         How are the different classes of T helper cells useful?
             •  They are used to provide different types of responses (i.e Th1, Th2,
                Th17 response) to pathogens

          When are cytokines produced?
             • When a T helper cell is responding to a pathogen
           What is the role of cytokines?
             •  To act as inflammatory mediators

         How do T killer cells (CD8) recognise infected cells?
             •  Through the recognition of the MHC Class I and antigenic proteins
             Describe the mechanism used by CD8 cells to kill infected cells.
             •  They have a core protein called perforin (hydrophilic) which insert a
                hole into host cell membrane that allows hydrophilic molecules to go
                through
                 ◦  Previously hydrophilic molecules couldn’t get through due to the
                    hydrophobic nature of the lipid membrane
             •  This allows the CTL (cytotoxic T-lymphocyte/T cell) to insert
              granzyme into the cell which triggers a cascade of reactions leading
                 to apoptosis

           What is the name of the other pathway used by CD8 cells to kill infected
               cells?
             •  Fas and Fas-ligand pathway
         Why are B cells said to be part of the humoral immune response?
             •  Because they secrete antibodies
             • The humoral immune response describes the role of antibodies in
                  providing immunity

             Describe the structure of an antibody.
             •  Constant region (bottom) and a variable region (top)
             •  FOur chains, two light (short) and two heavy (long)

           What is the role of the variable region on antibodies?
             •  To allow the antibody to bind to different types of antigen.
           What are the three methods used by antibodies to protect against
              infections?
             •  Neutralisation
             •  Opsonisation
             • Complement activation





             Describe how the process of neutralisation works.
             •  Neutralisation is when an antibody prevents a virus from binding
               onto a host cell receptor
             •   It does this by binding onto a region of the virus called the receptor
                binding domain and so the virus is no longer able to bind onto host
                   cells and use their machinery for proliferation.

             Describe how the process of opsonisation works.
             •  This describes how covering a pathogen with antibodies increases
                the likelihood of an innate effector cell (those that phagocytose)
               phagocytosing the pathogen

           What are the roles/characteristics of IgE?
             •  Fighting against allergy.
           What are the roles/characteristics of IgG?
             •  Important in the serum
             •  Highest neutralisation and opsonisation activity.
             •   It has four major subclasses in humans.

           What is the difference between the way B and T cells bind to antigens?
             • B cells bind to soluble antigens roaming freely in the blood
             • T cells bind to antigens on the surface of MHC complexes on APCs
           What three things need to happen for a B cell to be activated?

             • Have surface contact with a T cell (co-stimulatory contact)
             • Become activated by cytokines (released by CD4 T cells)

           What are thymus-independent antigens?
             •  These are antigens, (often polysaccharides) that can activate B cells
                without the help of T cells
             •  Signal from the pathogen (PAMP) is enough to activate the B cell.
           Which antibodies are activated by thymus-independent antigens?
             • IgM
             Describe how B cells are activated by T cells.
             • A B cell that has antibody on its surface is able to recognise a
                   specific antigen
             • A dendritic cell is also able to recognise this specific antigen
             •  Both of these cells bind onto the pathogen in question, and present
                the antigen on their MHC II complexes
                 ◦  This allows the same T helper cell to recognise both the dendritic
                          cell and the B cell it needs to communicate with
             • The T helper cell binds onto the antigen on the dendritic cell
                 surface, and tells the B cell to proliferate and differentiate into plasma
                   cells which create and secrete more and more antibody against the
                antigen in question
                 ◦ The T helper cells also signals to T killer cells, so they can kill
                     infected cells
