Overview

What is the complement system?

  • A group of about 20 plasma proteins crucial to the innate immune response used to opsonise or lyse bacteria.
  • Their goal is always to either opsonise bacteria via C3b or to use membrane attack complexes to kill the bacteria.

What are the three pathways involved in the complement system?

  • Classical pathway
  • Alternative pathway
  • Lectin pathway

Describe the events that occur in the classical pathway. Which two antibodies can initiate the classical pathway?

  • Antigen reacts with antibody which activates the C1 component
  • C1 breaks down C4 into C4a and C4b
  • C1 combines with C4b to form C14b
  • C14b breaks down C2 into C2a and C2b
  • C14b combines with C2a to form C3 convertase (C14b2a)
  • C3 convertase converts C3 into C3a and C3b
  • C3 convertase combines with C3b to form C5 convertase (C14b2a3b)
  • C5 convertase converts C5 into C5a and C5b
  • C5b is used to combine with C6,C7,C8 and C9 to form the Membrane Attack Complex (MAC or C5b6789)
  • IgM and IgG

Describe the events that occur in the lectin pathway.

  • Chemicals called lectins detect glycans on the surfaces of microbes
  • This leads to the same cascade (C3 and C5 convertase formation) which ends up in the same response.

What is the main difference between the classical and lectin pathways?

  • Lectin pathway involves the activation of C4b and C2a via binding of lectins to sugars
  • Classical pathway involves the activation of C4b and C2a via binding of antibodies to antigens

What is a bacterial endotoxin?

A lipopolysaccharide found in the outer membrane of gram-negative bacteria, used to trigger the alternative pathway of the complement system.

Describe the events that occur in the alternative pathway.

  • C3 is spontaneously hydrolysed to C3b
  • This occurs continuously, leading to regeneration of C3b.
  • Factor Bb binds to Factor C3b and this forms a C3 convertase (C3bBb)
  • The C5 convertase is then generated which allows the MAC to be generated.

What role does C3b play in the immune response?

  • It activates neutrophils and macrophages which allow them to phagocytose easier
  • This is opsonisation so C3b can be referred to as an opsonin

Describe the role of the Membrane Attack Complex (MAC) in the complement system.

This ruptures the bacterial cell wall which causes fluid influx into the bacterial cell and eventual lysis.

Describe how the MAC is formed from a C5b molecule.

  • C5 convertase binds to the bacterial cell wall
  • C5 convertase produces C5b which also binds to the bacterial cell wall
  • This recruits C6, C7, C8 and C9 to make the MAC Image showing the key steps of the complement cascade.
  • Step 2 represents the action of C3b which causes opsonisation of macrophages and neutrophils, leading to phagocytosis

What role does C5a play in wider immunity?

It conducts chemotaxis of neutrophils and macrophages to the site of infection.

What role do factors C3a, C4a and C5a all play in immunity?

They are involved in the activation of mast cells and basophils which leads to the release of inflammatory mediators such as histamine and serotonin.

What is a pyogenic disease?

A “pus-producing” disease that is characterised by tissue destruction mediated by cytotoxins and hydrolytic enzymes.

Recall the four types of phagocytic cell. Which two types of molecules can act as opsonins in the immune response?

  • Neutrophils
  • Monocytes
  • Macrophages
  • Dendritic cells
  • C3b
  • Antibodies

How does C3b interact with phagocytic cells to allow opsonisation of pathogens?

  • C3b binds onto pathogenic surfaces (antigens)
  • The phagocytic cells can recognise the C3b using its complement receptor and this sends a signal to begin phagocytosis

How do antibodies interact with phagocytic cells to allow opsonisation of pathogens?

  • Antibodies (IgG/IgM) bind onto the antigen
  • The phagocytic cell uses its Fc receptor to recognise the antibody and initiate a phagocytic response. Diagram showing the six immune evasion strategies commonly used by pathogens.

What type of bacterium is staphylococcus aureus?

Gram-positive.

What type of infections does staphylococcus aureus cause?

Skin and soft tissue infections (i.e bacteraemia and sepsis, endocarditis, osteomyelitis)

What is MRSA?

Methicillin-resistant staphylococcus aureus.

What are the four methods used by bacteria to evade the immune system?

  • Degrade C3
  • Inhibit C1 binding
  • Inhibit C3 and C5 convertases
  • Inhibit C5

How does S. aureus act directly on complement factor C3?

  • It releases proteases (Aur) that degrade C3 which prevents C3a and C3b formation
  • C3b is an important opsonin so the effects of this evasion are increased risk of infection.

How does S. aureus prevent the formation of C3 convertase?

  • Sbi proteins are released by bacteria which bind to antibodies
  • This means that C1 cannot recognise the bacteria and so there is no opsonisation
  • C1 not binding to the bacteria means there is a lack of C4 and C2 conversion which is required to form C3 convertase (C4b2a)

How does S. aureus inhibit C3 and C5 convertases?

  • It produces SCIN which binds to C3bBb (alternative pathway form of C3 convertase)
  • This reduces its enzymatic capacity meaning C3 is not converted which also prevents formation of C5 convertase.

How does S. aureus inhibit C5?

It releases SSL7 which binds to C5 and prevent it from converting

C5a→C5b and producing the MAC

How does the capsule polysaccharide work in bacterial immune evasion?

  • The thick layer (capsule) forms around the bacteria
  • This prevents the antigens from being recognised by the antibodies as they are hidden

How do bacteria interact with Fc regions in immune evasion?

  • The bacteria can produce proteins (Protein A) which bind onto the Fc region of the antibodies that bind to their antigens
  • Normally, antibodies would use their Fab regions to bind pathogens, and Fc receptors in opsonisation to phagocytic cells
  • There is obviously less opsonisation as a result of this
  • NOTE: Antibody-mediated opsonization: In this process, Fab (fragment antigen binding) region of the antibody binds to the antigen, and the Fc region of the antibody binds to the Fc receptor on the phagocytic cells and helps phagocytic cells, which include neutrophils, eosinophils, macrophages, and natural killer cells

How does staph. aureus cleave antibodies?

  • It breaks down plasminogen into plasmin which can be used to digest antibodies
  • Some bacteria directly release the proteases which digest antibodies.

How does staph. aureus interact with Fc receptors on phagocytes?

It can release molecules such as FLIPr that bind onto the Fc receptor and prevent the opsonisation of bacteria. Diagram summarising how staphylococcus aureus can evade the immune response.
