Overview

What is an adjuvant?

  • A substance that activates/enhances the body’s immune response against an antigen
  • It allows the body to provide a stronger immune response against a pathogen

What is a hapten?

A small molecule which on its own is not antigenic, but when associated with a large carrier such as a protein can form an antigen.

What is an immunogen?

An antigen that can independently elicit an immune response

What is an immune complex?

An antibody bound to an antigen

What is immunogenicity?

The ability of a substance to elicit an immune response

What is a damage associated molecular pattern (DAMP)?

  • Molecules that are released as a result of non-apoptotic cell death
  • They are recognised by and can activate the innate immune system (first line of defence) in order for it to fight against the causative agent of the damage

What is a pathogen associated molecular pattern (PAMP)?

  • Common molecular patterns found in pathogens and microbes, but not host cells
  • These patterns allow the innate immune system to recognise that there are pathogens about

What are PRRs (Pattern recognition receptors)?

These are proteins on hosts that recognise DAMPs and PAMPs

Recall three diseases that result from Type I (immediate or anaphylactic) hypersensitivity reactions.

  • Asthma
  • Allergic rhinitis (hay fever)
  • Atopic dermatitis (eczema)

What can a skin prick test be used for in the context of allergy?

  • Skin prick tests involve exposing the skin to small amounts of allergen
  • This can often be used to diagnose allergy as inflammation will occur in the case of an allergy

What is a Type I hypersensitivity reaction?

  • This is an allergic reaction that occurs when a susceptible host is re- exposed to what is known as an allergen
  • i.e they have already been exposed to the allergen before, and have antibodies in their arsenal that can work against this allergen
  • It happens because allergic individuals produce IgE antibodies against common antigens, which shouldn’t have antibodies produced against them in a normal situation
  • IgE antibodies are normally only produced against potent antigens such as parasitic ones
  • It can lead to a “wheal-and-flare” reaction (i.e inflamed tissue)

How is the immune system initially sensitised to a particular allergen?

The initial sensitisation of the immune system to aberrantly recognise a harmless antigen as harmful is complex with factors in genetics, age and the environment involved

Describe how IgE is initially produced in Type I hypersensitivity reactions. Which cells do IgE bind to and how do they do this?

  • The immune system is sensitised to a particular allergen, meaning it aberrantly recognises it as dangerous. This results in the production of:
  • Th2 CD4 cells and T follicular helper (Tfh) CD4 cells
  • These T cells produce cytokines IL-4 and IL-13 and the action of these cytokines on B cells causes them to switch to producing IgE specific to the allergen
  • The IgE then binds to mast cells using its Fc receptor, leading to degranulation of the mast cells
  • Basophils and mast cells
  • These granulocytic cells express a high affinity IgE receptor, Fc epsilon receptor I (FcεRI)

What are the two types of T helper cell produced in a Type I hypersensitivity reaction?

  • Th2 CD4 T cells
  • T follicular helper (Tfh) CD4 T cells

What two molecules are produced by these cells?

  • IL-4
  • IL-13

What effect do these molecules have in hypersensitivity reactions?

IL-4 and IL-13 are cytokines that are used to promote the production of IgE when they act on B cells.

How does IgE interact with antigens in a Type I hypersensitivity reaction, and what effect does this have on the cells around it?

  • IgE then binds onto mast cells and basophils due to their high affinity for the Fc epsilon receptor I (FcεRI) found on IgE
  • When IgE is bound to antigens, these IgE are cross-linked and degranulated and this leads to release of inflammatory mediators such as histamine and serotonin
  • The speed and magnitude of this response is heightened during the inflammatory response compared to normal infection

Why are there three phases in Type I hypersensitivity reactions?

There is such a wide range and variety of different molecules released that the release of these molecules can be classed into three phases

What happens in the early phase of a Type I hypersensitivity reaction?

Bioactive small molecules (i.e histamine, cytokines, leukotrienes and prostaglandins) are produced directly by mast cells

What happens in the later phase of a Type I hypersensitivity reaction?

Neutrophils are recruited to the site of reaction

What happens in the late phase of a Type I hypersensitivity reaction?

It happens 3-4 days after exposure and it’s where high frequencies of eosinophils are recruited and Th2 cells are present.

What is the role of leukotrienes and prostaglandins in a Type I hypersensitivity reaction? Which two types of antibodies are involved in Type 2 hypersensitivity reactions?

  • They are used to cause smooth muscle to contract
  • They are highly active molecules.
  • IgG and IgM

What is an alternative name for Type 2 hypersensitivity reactions?

Antibody-mediated cytotoxic hypersensitivity

What happens in Type 2 hypersensitivity reactions?

  • The IgM or IgG antibodies bound to self cell antigens, and sometimes go on to kill these cells
  • These reactions are otherwise known as antibody-mediated cytotoxic hypersensitivity reactions

What are the two ways that Type 2 hypersensitivity reactions come about?

  • Exposure to a foreign antigen which is recognised by antibodies
  • Aberrant (unusual) response of the antibodies against self-antigens

What are the three ways that these reactions can cause disease?

  • Anti-receptor activity – blocking or activating a receptor’s function
  • Antibody dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity (abbreviated to ADCC)
  • Classical activation of the complement cascade
  • This leads to inflammation, opsonisation and recruitment of immune cells

Recall two examples of Type 2 hypersensitivity reactions.

  • Immune thrombocytopenia (antibodies act against self platelet antigens)
  • Graves disease
  • Blood transfusion reactions

What happens in Grave’s disease?

Patients develop thyroid stimulating antibodies that bind the thyrotropin receptor resulting in secretion of thyroid hormones.

Where are antibody-antigen complexes bound to in Type 2 hypersensitivity reactions?

  • Fc receptors (they bind to constant region of antibody) on the surface of Natural Killer (NK) cells and granulocytes
  • This leads to the direct lysis of the target cell, but also release of inflammatory mediators, chemokines and cytokines
  • Hence, Type 2 hypersensitivity reactions can also lead to loss of function or imbalance in organ function

What is antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity (ADCC)?

  • In ADCC, antibody-antigen complexes on the surface of cells are bound by Fc receptors expressed by cells such as granulocytes and NK cells
  • This leads to directed lysis of the target cell, but also the release of inflammatory mediators, chemokines and cytokines.

What is an alternative name for Type 3 hypersensitivity reactions?

Immune complex driven disease

What happens in Type 3 hypersensitivity reactions?

  • Immune complexes (non cell-bound antibody-antigen complexes) form and normally they are cleared up by the immune response
  • In Type III HRs, the immune complexes aren’t cleared up and this leads to their deposition in blood vessel walls and tissues, promoting inflammation and tissue damage due to excess neutrophil extravasation to the tissues
  • i.e if antibodies are produced against DNA, since almost all cells have DNA, it will be near impossible to clear up these immune complexes
  • This can lead to fever, rashes, joint pain or protein in the urine: vasculitis if deposited in blood vessels, glomerulonephritis if in the kidneys or arthritis if in the joints.

Describe the distinction between Type 2 and Type 3 hypersensitivity reactions.

  • Type II reactions involve recognition of antigens on the surface of cells by IgGs and IgMs and the lysis or dysfunction of those cells
  • Type III reactions, again using antibodies, recognise antigen that is freely available in the body, not attached to cells and usually present over sustained periods.

Recall three autoimmune diseases caused by Type 3 hypersensitivity reactions.

  • Rheumatoid arthritis
  • Multiple sclerosis
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE)

What happens in systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) reactions?

IgG binds to DNA or nucleoproteins which form deposits

How is anti-serum used to treat patients with snake bites and how can Type 3 hypersensitivity interfere with this?

Antibodies for the snake venom particles are injected into the body
