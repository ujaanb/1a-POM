4.1 Microbial Infection

Overview

What are the five main microbial agents?

  • Viruses
  • Bacteria
  • Fungi
  • Protozoa
  • Helminths

What type of organisms are each of these agents?

  • Viruses - not cells in their own right
  • Bacteria - prokaryotes (no distinct nucleus)
  • Fungi - (single-celled) eukaryotes
  • Protozoa - (single-celled) eukaryotes
  • Helminths - (multicellular) eukaryotes

Why are viruses described as ‘obligate parasites?’

  • Because they have to infect hosts and use their material in order to reproduce and survive.
  • This means they are not found anywhere else in the environment other than in hosts.

What are the two mechanisms used by viruses to divide?

Cytolysis or budding out of cells

What is the difference between cytolysis and budding?

  • Cytolysis is a method of viral particle release after replication which causes the death of the infected host cell
  • Cytopathy is a method of viral particle release after replication which does not cause the death of a host cell, but releases particles via budding.

What advantage does budding have over cytolysis?

The virus can continue to divide and replicate whilst the host does not recognise it as a pathogen.

What is a retrovirus?

  • A virus that has an RNA genome.
  • This means it requires reverse transcriptase.

What is one of the most important steps to take in eradicating an infectious disease?

Implementing an efficient vaccine programme.

What is special about the genetic makeup of prokaryotes?

  • They contain one copy of each chromosome, instead of two like us
  • This means that a mutation to a gene is increasingly likely to have serious phenotypic effects.

What is different between the cytoskeleton of prokaryotes and eukaryotes?

  • Prokaryotes have a poor cytoskeleton (most of it comes from the peptidoglycan cell wall)
  • They don’t have the actin cytoskeleton

How do prokaryotes divide?

By binary fission.

What is the difference between gram-negative and gram-positive bacteria?

  • Gram-positive bacteria have thicker cell walls with more peptidoglycan than gram negative bacteria
  • This can be seen because the gram-positive bacterial cell walls retain the purple crystal violet stain when it is applied but gram-negative do not
  • Gram-negative bacteria have peptidoglycan which is sandwiched between two membranes, but there is only one of these membranes in gram-positive bacteria.

What role does the capsule play in bacteria?

It can allow them to escape phagocytosis by hosts as well as other destructive immune processes as well as protecting them from desiccation (drying up)

What role does the pili play in bacteria?

It can be used to attach to certain surfaces

How can bacteria move other than using their flagella?

Using the actin cytoskeleton

Recall two hospital acquired bacterial infections.

  • Clostridium difficile (C. diff)
  • Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA)

How do bacterial genomic mutations spread faster than human genomic mutations, despite the fact that both organisms have the same point mutation rates (mutations per number of genes)?

  • The rate at which bacteria reproduce is astronomically faster than humans - they have a shorter generation time
  • A new bacterial generation can take as short as 20 minutes to develop, but in humans, the average generation takes about 26 years to form.
  • This means that mutations that occur in bacteria are spread to offspring much more frequently, compared to in humans where the spread can take a number of years.
  • Also, the haploid nature of bacterial cells means that a mutation spreads far more easily to offspring and can have phenotypic effects more easily, compared to in humans where we have copies of each gene from mum and dad

What is a cutaneous infection?

  • One that has its effect through the deep tissue in the skin
  • For example, it can cause swelling or deep inflammation of skin tissues

What is a visceral infection?

One that has its effect through the organs in the body

How do protozoa divide?

  • By binary fission or trophozoite formation
  • Trophozoites are growing stages which result in bursting and the release of protozoa into the environment.

What is a trophozoite?

A trophozoite is the activated, feeding stage in the life cycle of certain protozoa such as malaria-causing Plasmodium falciparum

What are the two forms that fungi can exist in?

Yeasts or filaments.

Give two examples of viral infections.

  • HIV
  • Smallpox
  • Ebola
  • HTLV-1

Give three examples of bacterial infections.

  • Mycobacterium tuberculosis (TB)
  • Helicobacter pylori - peptic & gastric ulcers
  • Pathogenic E. Coli - (urinary tract infections, food poisoning, meningitis etc.)
  • Clostridium difficile
  • Methicillin resistant staphylococcus aureus

Give an example of a fungal infection.

Athlete’s foot

Give two examples of protozoa.

  • Malaria
  • Leishmania

Give three examples of helminths.

  • Roundworms
  • Flatworms
  • Tapeworms
  • Hookworms

What characteristic of helminths can make them easier to avoid?

They are visible to the naked eye.

What does the term ‘viral tropism’ refer to?

Viral tropism is the range of hosts that viruses can bind onto

What is a bacillus?

It is a gram-positive, rod shaped bacteria.

What is a nosocomial infection?

An infection acquired in a hospital.
