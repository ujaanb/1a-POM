Infection

Recall three microbe associated molecular patterns (MAMPs).

  • Lipopolysaccharides from gram-negative bacteria
  • Peptidoglycan from all bacteria
  • Beta-glucan cell wall on fungi
  • Surface glycoproteins and nucleic acids from viruses

Recall three pattern/danger associated molecular patterns (PAMPs/DAMPs).

  • ATP released from dead cells
  • Alarmins - chemicals released as a result of cell death
  • Specific cytokines/interleukins

Why are natural killer cells described to be part of the innate immune response, even though they are lymphocytes?

Because they respond very quickly and in an antigen-independent manner

What is an advantage of the adaptive immune system over the innate?

  • A lot more microbe-associated molecular patterns (antigens) can be detected by the adaptive system
  • About 1000 molecular patterns are detected by the innate in comparison to ~10^7 by the adaptive

Is blood sterile (lacks viruses and bacterial presence)?

  • Yes. Blood is completely sterile and you would be in the ICU if it wasn’t. In the immune response, which are the first types of cells to reach the site of infection?
  • Neutrophils
  • They are followed by macrophages after neutrophilic release of cytokines.

What happens to neutrophils and macrophages when they interact with pathogens?

  • They become activated
  • This is because they are normally naive as they come from bone marrow, but their gene expression changes after interaction with pathogens.

How can the immune system distinguish between dead and live pathogens?

Dead pathogens have much less RNA than live pathogens Image showing the different molecules produced in response to different pathogens.

Describe how interferon-γ can lead to the activation of macrophages. Which type of interferons are used only against viruses?

  • The macrophage binds to a pathogen and releases cytokines, which activates lymphocytes
  • Lymphocytes produce interferon-γ
  • Interferon-γ is important as it can activate the macrophage, allowing it to cause enhanced phagocytosis, increased cytokine production, expression of cell surface molecules, and antigen presentation (hence T cell activation)
  • It also allows the macrophage to express certain antimicrobial genes for molecules such as reactive oxygen species (i.e bleach for killing cells)
  • Interferon-γ is hence vital to controlling infection and the transition from the innate to adaptive immune response
  • Type I (IFNα/β)
  • Type II (IFNγ) and Type III (IFNλ) also work against viruses but have other functions too (Type II = antibacterial, Th1 skewing | Type III = antiviral, mucosal immunity)

Why do interferons have their name?

Because they were discovered to interfere with viral replication.

What is pyroptosis?

  • A type of cell death which involves the influx of water into cells via their pores which causes them to burst
  • This leads to inflammation as well as fevers (increased body temperature)

Describe how Natural Killer (NK) cells are involved in the immune response. Which two cytokines work antagonistically in bacterial and fungal infections?

  • Natural Killer cells detect infected cells and this leads to them triggering apoptosis in the infected target cell. Image showing humoral and cellular innate immune responses.
  • As seen here, the complement system can be used to opsonise bacteria (using C3b) which leads to enhanced phagocytosis, or it can produce reactive oxygen species, which lead to their lysis
  • Interferon-γ (IFNγ) is used for bacterial infections
  • IL-17 is used for fungal infections

Recall which cytokines are involved in Th1, Th2 and Th17 immunity.

  • Th1 - IFN-γ
  • Th2 - IL-4, IL-5, IL-13
  • Th17 - IL-17, IL-22

Recall which type of leukocyte are targeted by these T helper cells and which pathogens each of these T cells are produced against.

  • Th1 - Macrophage - intracellular pathogens (i.e viruses)
  • Th2 - Eosinophils - Helminths, parasites
  • Th17 - Neutrophils - extracellular bacteria and fungi Table showing how different phagocytes release different cytokines which lead to different lymphocytes being produced. Image showing the sequence and timing of events in the cellular immune response.
  • As we can see here, the immune response progresses from complement and phagocytes within a few hours, to B and T lymphocytes after about two days, to effector T cells and plasma cells (and hence antibodies) after about five days
