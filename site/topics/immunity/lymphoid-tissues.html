Overview

Why does our body need lymphoid tissues?

  • Lymphoid tissue is required because T and B cells cannot express a large enough number of TCRs and BCRs (T/B Cell Receptors) to deal with all the possible antigens that are threatening to the immune system
  • The number of BCRs/TCRs that can be expressed (repertoire) is much larger (10^15 - 10^20) than the number of these cells present in our blood (~ 7.5 x 10^9)
  • The lymphoid tissues are introduced to provide the immune system with an alternative way to combat these antigens and cope with the strenuous demands
  • They increase the likelihood of interactions between B/T cells and antigens (secondary)

What comprises the lymphatic system?

  • Lymphatic vessels
  • Secondary lymphoid tissue

Where does lymphopoiesis occur?

In the primary lymphoid tissue (organs).

What are the three main types of lymphocyte?

  • B cells
  • T cells
  • Natural killer cells.

What is the role of primary lymphoid organs?

Lymphopoiesis (lymphocyte production)

Recall three primary lymphoid organs.

  • Thymus
  • Bone marrow
  • Foetal liver Image showing B and T cell responses to pathogens.

What is the primary source of haematopoiesis in humans?

Bone marrow

How is haematopoiesis affected during infection?

  • White blood cell (leukocyte) production increases.
  • T cells and B cells which are lymphocytes are used in the immune response so it makes sense to have more present in response to antigens.

Where do B cells undergo their final differentiation?

  • In the bone marrow.
  • This is why they are called B cells.

What is a repertoire in immunological terms?

  • The range of genetically distinct B and T cell receptors present in a given host
  • The more BCRs or TCRs present in a given host, the more antigens and hence threats can be dealt with

Where are antigens found?

On the surface of cells, viruses, bacteria etc.

Where do T cells undergo their final differentiation?

  • In the thymus
  • This is why they are called T cells.

Describe how positive selection in B/T cell differentiation works.

  • It is a process that is used to determine whether a B/T cell can detect antigens using its BCR/TCR (its primary function)
  • If it cannot, it has developed abnormally and these cells undergo apoptosis.

Describe how negative selection in B/T cell differentiation works.

  • It is a process which is used to determine whether a B/T cell has BCRs/TCRs that react against self (host cell) antigens
  • We don’t want those B/T cells that react against self antigens, and so they undergo apoptosis

What happens to T cell production as we age, and why?

  • The rate of T cell production decreases and this is due to a process called thymus involution
  • This involves the thymus shrinking, and being reduced to fat and so the production of T cells is reduced because the thymus is involved in

T cell differentiation

What are secondary lymphoid organs?

Organs which act as the site where lymphocytes can interact with antigens and other lymphocytes.

Approximately what percentage of the total number of T cells are contained in the blood?

  • 2%
  • The rest is found mainly in the secondary lymphoid organs

Recall four secondary lymphoid organs.

  • Spleen
  • Lymph nodes
  • Appendix
  • Tonsils

What are afferent and efferent lymphatic vessels?

  • An afferent vessel is where lymph fluid flows into the lymph node
  • An efferent vessel is where lymph fluid flows out of the lymph node

What does the lymph fluid contain?

  • Lymphocytes
  • Antigens
  • Although the lymph nodes have arterial and venous connections, these two elements flow to the lymph nodes via the lymphatic vessels.

What causes the separation between the B cells and T cells within lymph nodes?

  • Chemokines
  • They are produced by the stroma within the lymph nodes

What additional role does the spleen play that the lymph nodes don’t play?

  • The spleen filters blood antigens when it comes through the arteries
  • The lymph nodes filter skin or respiratory tract antigens

What is the first line of defence against infection?

Mucosal/epithelial barriers

What are Peyer’s patches?

These are specialised secondary lymphoid tissues that are found below the epithelium in the ileum of the small intestine

Describe a property of Peyer’s patches.

  • They have a high frequency of B cells
  • They have a lot of germinal centres

What is a germinal centre?

A site where B cells undergo mutation and selection to produce high- affinity antibodies

What is Waldeyer’s ring?

The ring of tonsils that surround the pharyngeal region

Why is it a better idea for B and T cells to be located within secondary lymphoid tissue, rather than freely circulating around the blood?

  • A lot of fluid that surrounds tissues is drained through the secondary lymphoid organs for filtering and is returned to the blood
  • 2-3 L of fluid returned to blood via superior vena cava per day
  • If there are T and B cells located within these tissues, then there is a far greater chance of them encountering antigens that flow through these systems and dealing with them
  • Lymphocytes also recirculate around the blood once every 24 hours and this is another way of increasing the chance of an antigen- lymphocyte interaction.
  • Overall, the purpose of the secondary lymphoid organs is to increase the speed at which the lymphocytes travel across the body, so that they can deal with their respective antigens swiftly.
  • This is needed because our T Cell repertoire (or number of different antigens) vastly outweighs the number of T cells we actually have. Image showing how chemokines, integrins and selectins are involved in the extravasation of T cells into the lymph node.
  • CCR7 binds to its chemokine CCL21
  • LFA-1 binds to its chemokine ICAM

How does inflammation affect T cell and B cell distribution?

  • Inflammation at a particular site will cause signals to be released at this site which will instruct the specific T cells and B cells at the site of inflammation to remain where they are, to increase the chance of them encountering their respective antigens on an antigen presenting cell.
  • Normally, T cells and B cells are circulating through the lymphatic system, but inflammation sends signals for them to migrate to the site of inflammation

What is an antigen-presenting cell (APC)?

An APC is a cell type which presents antigens captured from the environment, so that a T cell can recognise it and initiate an adaptive immune response.
