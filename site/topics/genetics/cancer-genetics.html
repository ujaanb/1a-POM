Overview

What is cancer?

  • Cancer is a condition where cells in a specific part of the body grow and reproduce uncontrollably.
  • The cancerous cells can invade and destroy surrounding healthy tissue, including organs.

What is tumour heterogeneity?

Tumour heterogeneity describes how differences can exist both within tumours and between different types of tumour.

What is the difference between intratumour and intertumour heterogeneity?

  • Intratumour heterogeneity describes how different groups of cells called “sub-clones” can come together and constitute a single tumour
  • Intertumour heterogeneity describes how no two people have the same cancer

What is the difference between a driver and a passenger mutation?

  • Driver mutations are those that drive cancer initiation and progression
  • Passenger mutations are those that do not drive cancer initiation and progression

What is a cancer risk gene?

  • It is a gene that is at high risk of suffering from a driver mutation.
  • These genes are normally protective against cancer, but once they mutate, we become at risk of cancer
  • There are approximately 743 cancer risk genes

What is a proto-oncogene?

  • A normal, benign gene that codes for a protein that promotes cell growth and proliferation
  • When there is a driver mutation in a proto-oncogene, it becomes an oncogene

What causes a proto-oncogene to become an oncogene?

  • A driver mutation.
  • i.e a driver mutation can occur in the KRAS gene which causes it to continually proliferate and lead to cancer.

What is a tumour suppressor gene?

  • A type of normal gene that produces a protein that helps to limit cell growth and proliferation
  • p53 is an example of a tumour suppressor gene, as it makes the tumour protein which suppresses proliferation
  • p53 → p21 protein → binds to CDK-cyclin complex cell proliferation is stopped

What is the Knudson hypothesis?

  • The hypothesis that states that both alleles for tumour suppressor genes need to be inactivated to cause phenotypic changes.
  • It is also known as the two-hit hypothesis

What is a germline mutation?

  • Mutations that occur in the germ cells (egg or sperm)
  • They are passed on from parents to offspring

What is a somatic mutation?

  • Mutation in any cells that aren’t in the germ cells (not egg or sperm)
  • They are not passed on from parents to offspring

Why is it difficult to treat cancers?

The difficulty arises from a problem with selectively targeting cancer cells, without targeting normal host cells.

How can this be worked around (relatively)?

  • Chemicals can be engineered to kill fast dividing cells
  • The problem is, some healthy cells (i.e hair follicles, GI tract cells) are also fast dividing
  • This is why chemotherapy can lead to side effects such as vomiting, hair loss etc.

Recall three treatments for cancer.

  • Surgery
  • Chemotherapy
  • Radiotherapy
  • Targeted therapy
  • Immunotherapy

How is Whole Genome Sequencing used in genetic screening?

  • It can be used in pre-malignant monitoring to detect when a person is at risk of cancer (i.e due to family history)
  • It can be used to diagnose and classify cancer in those who already have it
  • It can be used in pharmacogenomics to determine the best possible treatment for patients who already have cancer

What is the Cancer Gene Census (CGC)?

This is an ongoing effort to catalogue all genes that have been causally linked to cancer NOTE: Different types of cancer genes require different number of driver mutations to lead to cancers.
