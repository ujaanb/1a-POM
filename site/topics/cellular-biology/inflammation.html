Overview

What is inflammation?

  • Inflammation is a non-specific response to cellular injury
  • Its purpose is to remove the cause and consequence of cellular injury

What are the four cardinal signs of acute inflammation?

  • Redness (rubor)
  • Heat (calor)
  • Swelling (tumor)
  • Pain (dolor)

What can be a fifth cardinal sign of acute inflammation?

Loss of function (a result of unresolved inflammation)

Explain the molecular, cellular and physiological processes underlying each of the cardinal features of inflammation.

  • Redness - increased blood flow due to vasodilation means more red blood cells are present at site of inflammation
  • Swelling - Fluid builds up at the site of inflammation due to increased vascular permeability that causes fluid leakage into interstitial fluid
  • Heat - body fluid that is at body temperature (~37C) is exposed to site of inflammation, which is usually not exposed to site in the steady state
  • Pain - the inflammatory mediators released by mast cells also drive pain stimulation on neurons
  • i.e histamines and cytokines
  • Loss of function - happens in certain area. i.e inflammation can cause a barrier in the lungs which can lead to inefficient gas exchange.

How are mast cells derived from haemopoietic stem cells (HSCs)?

Recall two cell types found in the interstitium of normal vascularised tissue in the steady state.

  • Mast cells
  • Macrophages

How are these two cell types able to detect damage in the more superficial layers?

  • Damage associated molecular patterns (DAMPs)
  • From damage caused to cells in the superficial layers of tissue
  • Pathogen associated molecular patterns (PAMPs)
  • From foreign material (i.e bacteria) entering the body

What physiological changes occur in acute inflammation?

  • Change in local blood flow (decreased)
  • There is decreased blood flow caused by vasodilation. Vasodilation causes blood to move through the blood vessels slower, explaining the decrease in flow
  • Structural changes in the microvasculature (vasodilation, increased permeability)
  • Recruitment/accumulation of immune cells and proteins.

Describe what happens to a blood vessel at the site of epidermal damage.

  • There is first death of the epidermal cells caused by a wound, for example
  • This causes inflammatory mediators such as histamine and nitric oxide to be released which both cause vasodilation
  • They also lead to increased vascular permeability which leads to plasma contents migrating to the site of damage.
  • i.e plasma exudation

Recall two vasodilators released at the site of inflammation.

  • Histamine
  • Nitric oxide

How are these vasodilators released at the site of inflammation?

  • Histamine and nitric oxide are released from mast cells and macrophages respectively in response to detection of foreign material (DAMPs and PAMPs)
  • i.e bacteria

What is the purpose of vasodilation and increased vascular permeability at the site of inflammation?

This allows plasma contents to leak into the interstitium and site of damage from the blood

Recall three components of the plasma (blood) that leak into the interstitium at the site of damage.

  • Antibodies
  • Complement (C5a, C3a, C4a)
  • Platelets
  • Leukocytes (white blood cells)

Recall five inflammatory mediators released at the site of inflammation.

  • Chemokines

  • Histamine

What is exudate?

  • Fluid, proteins and cells that have seeped out of a blood vessel
  • It describes that liquid component that is released after increased vascular permeability in response to infection

What important role can the exudate play in fighting infection?

It can form and act as a barrier between the blood vessel and the site of infection, preventing the infection turning from a local one to a systemic one that travels through the bloodstream.

What is the role of chemokines in inflammation?

To recruit other cells to the site of infection, specifically leukocytes.

Which cell type is often the first type to be recruited by chemokines to the site of infection?

Most of the time, neutrophils are the first type of leukocyte to be recruited to the site of inflammation

Which type of cell releases chemokines in response to infection?

Which is usually the first type of leukocyte that is recruited to the site of infection?

Neutrophils

Which chemokine is responsible for the chemotaxis of neutrophils to the site of infection?

CXCL8 (IL-8)

What mechanism do chemokines usually use to transport leukocytes to the site of infection?

Chemokines bind onto the receptors of the cell they are transporting, and induce the movement of this cell.

What is extravasation?

  • The leakage of a particular substance (from the blood) into the tissue around it
  • i.e blood, lymph, or other fluid, such as an anticancer drug, from a blood vessel or tube into the tissue around it.

What is the purpose of neutrophil extravasation?

To move the neutrophil from the blood vessel into the interstitium or tissue at the site of damage

Describe what happens in neutrophil extravasation.

  • Blood is flowing in a particular direction in the capillary. This blood flow obviously carries the neutrophil in the same direction as it (neutrophil is in blood)
  • A macrophage/mast cell at the site of infection in the interstitium produces cytokines after it is activated which are used to upregulate selectins (adhesion molecules)
  • Selectins are proteins/adhesion molecules on the endothelial surface that leukocytes bind onto as they roll along in the bloodstream. ROLLING ADHESION
  • Cytokines then cause production of chemokines either by signalling to macrophages or endothelial cells
  • Chemokines are presented by proteoglycans on the endothelial surface and they bind to the neutrophil
  • Chemokines binding to the neutrophil increases the LFA-1 integrin’s affinity to a high affinity conformation
  • Integrins are also proteins/adhesion molecules on the endothelial surface
  • The integrin ligand (ICAM-1) binds the neutrophil very tightly, and now it is moving towards the interstitium. TIGHT ADHESION
  • The neutrophil then undergoes transmigration via endothelial cell- to-cell junctions into the site of infection at the interstitium
  • This process is mediated by PECAM-1 and involves cytoskeletal rearrangement
  • NOTE: Selectins and integrins are adhesion molecules that are used to help neutrophils migrate to the site of infection. Cytokines and chemokines upregulate their expression respectively.

Which molecule is used to upregulate selectins?

Cytokines

Which molecule is used to upregulate integrins?

Chemokines

How do multiple neutrophils accumulate at the site of infection after one arrives?

One or two arrive at the site of infection, before they secrete chemoattractants which allow other neutrophils to reach the site too.

What do neutrophils do at the site of inflammation?

Once the neutrophils have rolled via adhesion to the site of infection, they are ready to phagocytose the pathogen at hand

Describe what happens in neutrophilic phagocytosis.

  • The pathogenic particles are engulfed into phagocytic vesicles to form what is known as phagosomes
  • Phagosomes fuse with the lysosome to form a phagolysosome (enzymes in the lysosome break down the pathogen)
  • Reactive oxygen species (ROS) can also disable/neutralise the pathogen.
  • i.e Phagocyte NADPH oxidase

What is the role of reactive oxygen species (ROS) found in leukocytes?

Reactive oxygen species (ROS) can also disable/neutralise pathogens.

What additional thing do neutrophils do after phagocytosis, to allow them to further phagocytose more pathogens?

  • They release their nucleus and the DNA causes further agglutination of the other pathogens present at the site of infection.
  • This makes them more easily phagocytosed by other neutrophils. (BOTTOM LEFT)

What is the purpose of resolution of acute inflammation?

To restore the tissue to its steady state after it has been damaged.

What happens in the resolution of acute inflammation?

  • The macrophages are used to clear the debris of the apoptotic cells (neutrophils) that have a short half-life
  • Macrophages also produce anti-inflammatory mediators triggered by IL-10 which help to restore the inflamed tissue to its steady state.

Recall the anti-inflammatory mediator produced by macrophages during the resolution of acute inflammation.

IL-10

What is the main difference between acute and chronic inflammation, in terms of immune cells? *EQ*

  • At the site of infection, there is a much wider variety of infiltratory cell types seen in chronic inflammation compared to acute inflammation
  • In chronic inflammation, it is more common to see cell types such as inflammatory macrophages, T cells and plasma (B) cells.

Describe two other distinctions between acute and chronic inflammation.

  • There is often persistent toxic stimuli that are seen commonly in chronic inflammation which causes a vicious cycle where there is no clearance of inflammatory agent and resolution to steady state
  • This lack of resolution can be caused by tuberculosis, Hepatitis

B/C or persistent toxic stimuli

What is meant by bystander tissue damage in chronic inflammation?

This is when the chemicals that are released by the cells in the immune response actually cause damage to the host cell tissue.

Why does fibrosis and angiogenesis occur in chronic inflammation?

  • The vicious cycle causes repeated damage to blood vessels which leads to generation of new ones (angiogenesis)
  • Tissue scarring (fibrosis) is caused by prolonged action of macrophages to try and repair the damage

Describe three “good” things that macrophages do at the site of inflammation.

  • Phagocytosis
  • Cytotoxic - release chemicals that can kill damaged cells
  • Release anti-inflammatory mediators (i.e IL-10, TNF-alpha)
  • Repair wounds

Describe three “bad” things that macrophages do at the site of inflammation.

  • Cytotoxic - can be damaging to host cells.
  • Inflammatory - can cause inflammation of host tissue
  • Pro-fibrotic - can cause fibrosis of tissue via over-repair

How can T cells cause adverse effects in chronic inflammation?

  • They can have cytotoxic and inflammatory effects on “self” cells.
  • This leads to bystander tissue damage
