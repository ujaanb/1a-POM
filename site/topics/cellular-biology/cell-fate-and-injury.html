2.11 Cell Fate and Injury

Overview

What are the two mechanisms through which normal cells can become injured/die?

  • A stimulus which directly causes cell death (injurious stimulus)
  • An inability for a group of cells to adapt as too much demand has been placed on them.

What is the difference between lethal and sublethal cell injury?

  • Lethal cell injury directly leads to death
  • Sublethal cell injury does not directly lead to death and the injury may be reversible, or progress to cell death eventually

What is hypertrophy?

The enlargement of cells.

What is infarction?

Cell death due to ischaemia (lack of oxygen supply)

Recall five stimulatory causes of cell injury.

  • Oxygen deprivation
  • Genetic defects (i.e sickle cell)
  • Aging (i.e acquisition of increasingly volatile cells over time)
  • Nutritional imbalance (i.e lack of key ions/vitamins or simply calories)
  • Infectious agents
  • Immunological reactions
  • Chemical agents (weed killers, drug chemicals)
  • Physical agents (i.e trauma, gunshot wounds, radiation)

Which four intracellular processes are particularly vulnerable to cell injury?

  • Cell membrane integrity
  • ATP synthesis
  • Protein synthesis
  • Genetic apparatus integrity (i.e DNA)
  • These processes are so integrally related. For example, a lack of ATP synthesis means the Na+/K+ ATPase cannot function correctly and cell membrane integrity is affected

Why can we not assume that a normal looking cell (cell that looks alive) is functional?

  • Cells undergoing the process of death usually lose their function before actually dying and undergoing the morphological changes seen.
  • This means a cell can be dead before looking dead, which is an important thing to note.

What is atrophy?

  • The shrinkage in the size of a cell.
  • It can lead to the related organ also becoming smaller. In which pathology can brain atrophy be seen?
  • Advanced brain dementia.

What is the difference between physiological and pathological?

  • Physiological means processes that occur in normal, healthy people
  • Pathological means processes that occur in people with diseases

What is hypertrophy?

  • Increase in the size of cells which can lead to the increase in organ size
  • It can be physiological (seen in normal people)
  • i.e uterus expands during pregnancy
  • Or pathological (part of a disease process)

What causes hypertrophy?

  • Increased functional demand
  • Specific hormonal stimulation

Recall an example of physiological hypertrophy.

  • Increased demand placed on muscle cells (i.e in athletes)
  • The uterus during pregnancy

Recall an example of pathological hypertrophy.

  • People with hypertension/valve disorders
  • This leads to left ventricular hypertrophy which is a result of the left ventricle pumping blood that is of such high pressure

What is hyperplasia?

  • An increase in the number of cells in an organ which can lead to an increase in the organ’s size.
  • It can be hormonal (i.e endometrial gland proliferation during pregnancy driven by oestrogen) or compensatory (i.e in response to a stimulus)
  • Pathological hyperplasias are seen in cancer.

What causes physiological hyperplasia?

  • Hormone production
  • Compensatory mechanisms
  • i.e cell proliferation in response to damage

What causes pathological hyperplasia?

  • Excessive hormone production
  • Excessive growth factor production

Recall an example of physiological hyperplasia.

  • The uterus during menstrual cycle after the lining has broken down
  • Hepatocyte regeneration in injured liver

Recall an example of pathological hyperplasia.

Carcinomas (cancers of epithelial tissue)

What is metaplasia?

A reversible change where one adult cell type is replaced by another

Recall an example of physiological metaplasia.

In pregnancy when the inner columnar epithelial cells of the cervix

are replaced by squamous epithelial cells during pregnancy. This is due to the pH of the vagina

This change is reversed after pregnancy.

Recall an example of pathological metaplasia.

  • A pathological metaplasia is seen in Barrett’s oesophagus where the squamous cells of the oesophagus are replaced by columnar ones from the stomach.
  • In some cases, it is reversible

What is dyplasia?

  • The state where cells show the genetic and cytological features of malignancy, but don’t invade the surrounding tissue.
  • The intermediate step between cells transforming from normal cells to cancerous cells.
  • In the image, cells on the right show signs of cancer (i.e big nuclei, increased mitosis) but they are still constrained within their cell membranes and aren’t invading other tissues

What is a distinct characteristic of cells that are cancerous (or pre- cancerous)?

  • They have larger nuclei, so can often look darker on light microscopes (C)
  • B: Metaplasia . C: Dysplasia

What is alcoholic fatty change?

  • When the consumption of alcohol leads to the formation of fatty areas in the liver
  • After a period of non-consumption, the changes will reverse.
  • Image showing fatty changes occurring to hepatocytes

What is ballooning degeneration?

  • Cells become much larger (hence the name ballooning) as a result of damage to the cytoskeleton of the cells
  • This damage can be induced by alcohol intake

What is the cell cytoskeleton?

  • This describes the structures within a cell that allow it to maintain its structure and internal organisation
  • This is similar to a human skeleton and the role it plays in humans

What is necrosis?

Cell death that is associated with inflammation.

What are the four types of necrosis?

  • Coagulative
  • Liquefactive
  • Caseous
  • Fat

What is coagulative necrosis?

  • A type of cell death that occurs when blood flow to cells stops or slows (ischaemia)
  • The dead cells often leads to hardening of the tissue (coagulation)

How is coagulative necrosis identified microscopically?

  • Cells will often lose their nuclei
  • Myocardial infarct seen below

What is liquefactive necrosis?

  • Where the tissue becomes liquefied as a result of cell death
  • It is common in the brain

How is liquefactive necrosis identified microscopically?

Liquid (white) can be seen to cover the majority of the tissue

What is caseous necrosis?

A “cheesy” type of necrosis where the tissue becomes “oozy and structureless”

How is caseous necrosis identified microscopically?

Granular necrotic material is associated with the caseous areas

What is fat necrosis?

  • The type of necrosis that causes the abnormal breakdown of fatty tissue which leads to liquefication of this fatty tissue
  • It is usually caused by the inappropriate activation of lipases in wrong tissue
  • i.e pancreatitis where lipases act in pancreas instead of duodenum

How is fat necrosis identified microscopically?

  • Soapy calcium deposits (blue)
  • This is caused by lipases breaking triglycerides → glycerol + FFAs
  • FFAs combine with calcium to form unabsorbable Ca-fatty acid soaps
  • FUN FACT: Ca2+ can hence cause weight loss in humans as FFAs won’t be absorbed.

What separates apoptosis from necrosis?

  • The confluence of inflammation with cell death in necrosis.
  • This is caused by the leakage of cellular contents caused by cell membrane degradation seen in necrosis that is not seen in apoptosis

Describe the process of apoptosis.

  • The cell breaks down but no leakage - an apoptotic body is formed
  • Macrophages/phagocytes then come along to phagocytose these cells.
