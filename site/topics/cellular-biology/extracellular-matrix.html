Overview

What is the extracellular matrix (ECM)?

  • A complex network of carbohydrates and proteins that fill up the spaces between cells.
  • It’s made up of fibrillar and non-fibrillar components

What does the term “fibrillar” mean?

Fibre-making.

Recall three roles of the ECM.

  • Provides physical support for cells
  • Influences the growth and adhesion of cells as well as their differentiation status
  • Development, tissue function and organogenesis

Recall three types of tissue that fall under the umbrella term connective tissue.

  • Bone
  • Cartilage
  • Fat
  • Blood
  • Lymphatic tissue.
  • Tendon
  • Skin

Which collagen types are fibrillar?

Types I, II and III

Which collagen types are non-fibrillar?

Type IV

What is collagen IV used for?

To constitute the basement membrane

What constitutes the connective tissue?

  • Extracellular matrix
  • Component cells (i.e macrophages, epithelial cells etc.)
  • Fluid

What are the three main extracellular matrix components?

  • Collagens
  • Multi-adhesive glycoproteins
  • Proteoglycans

Recall three types of collagens.

  • Type I
  • Type II
  • Type III
  • Type IV

Recall three types of multi-adhesive glycoprotein.

  • Fibronectin
  • Fibrinogen
  • Laminins

Recall three types of proteoglycan.

  • Aggrecan
  • Versican
  • Decorin
  • Perlecan

What are multi-adhesive glycoproteins and proteoglycans both examples of?

Glycoproteins

What is a proteoglycan?

A heavily-glycosylated glycoprotein (i.e one with lots of carbohydrates - specifically GAG chains)

Image showing key components of ECM

Why do connective tissues need to have different properties depending on their area of usage?

This is to allow the particular tissue to be well-adapted to performing its function

What causes connective tissues to have varying properties (i.e resilient, flexible)?

The properties of the type of collagen in the ECM.

What is tendon-skin ECM like?

Resilient, flexible

What is bone ECM like?

Hard and dense

What is cartilage ECM like?

  • Resilient and shock-absorbing.
  • Cartilage is the tissue that protects our bones from mechanical stress damage.

Recall three disorders arising from gene mutations that affect matrix proteins.

  • Osteogenesis imperfecta
  • Marfan’s syndrome
  • Alport’s syndrome
  • Epidermolisis Bullosa
  • Congenital Muscular Dystrophy Mutation in which gene causes osteogenesis imperfecta?
  • Type I Collagen Mutation in which gene causes Marfan’s syndrome?
  • Fibrillin 1 Mutation in which gene causes Alport’s syndrome?
  • Type IV Collagen Mutation in which gene causes Epidermolysis Bullosa?
  • Laminin 5 Mutation in which gene causes Congenital Muscular Dystrophy?
  • Laminin 2

What are glycosaminoglycans?

Glycosaminoglycans (GAGs), also known as mucopolysaccharides,

are negatively-charged polysaccharide compounds often forming the sugar part of proteoglycans

Examples include heparin/heparan sulfate, chondroitin sulfate/dermatan sulfate, keratan sulfate, and hyaluronic acid.

Recall a disorder that arises from a gene mutation affecting ECM catabolism (mucopolysaccharidoses).

  • Hurler’s syndrome (L-alpha-iduronidase)
  • This leads to excess GAG build-up in the ECM

Recall a disorder arising from excessive ECM deposition (increased presence).

  • Liver fibrosis
  • Kidney fibrosis
  • Lung fibrosis

Recall a disorder that arises from excessive loss of ECM.

Osteoarthritis.

What type of protein are collagens (structurally)?

Fibrous

How are the collagen fibrils in the skin arranged?

At right angles to each other

What property does this give to the tissue?

It allows it to have tensile strength.

How are the collagen fibrils in the bone and cornea arranged?

In a uniform parallel arrangement

Describe the basic structure of a collagen molecule.

  • It is made up of three alpha-chains which form a triple helix
  • The alpha chains can either be different (Type I) or all the same (Type II, Type III)

Which three chains comprise a Type I Collagen fibre?

[a1(I)]2 [a2(I)]

Which three chains comprise a Type II Collagen fibre?

[a1(II)]3

Which three chains comprise a Type III Collagen fibre?

[a1(III)]3

What is a heterotrimeric collagen?

  • A collagen molecule made up of one or more types of alpha chains
  • Type I is an example.

What is a homotrimeric collagen?

  • A collagen molecule made up of one type of alpha chain
  • Type II and Type III are examples.

Describe the structure of an alpha chain in collagen.

  • It is made up of repeating glycine-x-y units
  • Typically the x and y are proline and hydroxyproline

Why is glycine repeated every three monomers in an alpha chain?

  • It is the simplest amino acid (H chain as the R group)
  • This means it is also the smallest and can pack tightly inwards in the inside of the triple helix
  • The collagen needs to be compact so this is a helpful property.

Describe the levels of assembly of collagen fibres from smallest level to collagen fibre.

  • Three alpha chains (of same or different type, depending on the type of collagen being made) are assembled together and condensed to form a collagen fibril
  • Collagen fibrils are cross-linked to form collagen fibres which add tensile strength to the structure

What is a post-transcriptional modification made to collagen fibres that will be secreted by cells?

  • N-termini and C-termini (non-collagenous domains/propeptides) are removed
  • This is the case for Type I,II and III collagens, those that remain in the extracellular matrix

Describe the steps involved in collagen fibre biosynthesis.

  • The collagen pro-alpha-chain is synthesised within the ribosome (protein synthesis)
  • The alpha-chain undergoes hydroxylation of selected proline and lysine residues
  • The alpha-chain undergoes glycosylation of selected hydroxylysine residues
  • The pro-alpha chain combines together with other pro-alpha-chains to form a triple helix (procollagen)
  • The procollagen migrates to the cytoplasm and packaged into a vesicle
  • The triple helix (procollagen) is secreted via exocytosis
  • The procollagen undergoes cleavage of the N and C (termini) propeptides (non-collagenous)
  • The triple helices condense together to form collagen fibrils
  • The collagen fibrils combine together to form collagen fibres

What happens to our collagen fibres as we age?

The extent of cross-linking reduces and hence the strength of the collagen and tissues decreases

Which molecules are involved in the cross-linking of collagen fibres?

Proline, hydroxyproline, lysine and hydroxylysine.

What is the importance of the hydroxylation of proline and lysine in collagen assembly?

To allow them to form hydrogen bonds with each other in cross- linking

What is a hydroxylase?

An enzyme that is used to hydroxylate a molecule

Recall the two cofactors required by proline and lysine hydroxylases to hydroxylate proline and lysine residues of procollagen.

Vitamin C and Fe2+ are both cofactors which are heavily involved in the action of hydroxylases

What can a lack of vitamin C hence result in?

  • A lack of vitamin C will hence lead to a lack of hydroxylation of proline and lysine which means cross-linking between collagen fibres cannot occur
  • This leads to weakened tissue stability as seen in scurvy

What are Ehlers-Danlos syndromes?

  • These are a group of syndromes that are caused by mutations which negatively affect collagen production, structure or processing
  • It can lead to symptoms such as stretchy skin and loose joints

Recall two fibril-associated collagens and what their roles are.

  • Type IX and Type XII
  • Their roles are to regulate the organisation of other collagen fibrils (i.e Type II)
  • They are very flexible

What is special about the assembly of collagen type IV?

The non-collagenous domains (N and C termini) of the propeptides

are not cleaved off and instead are used to strengthen the collagen as they interact and form a network on basement membranes

See lecture PDF.

How do fibrotic diseases arise?

Excessive production of connective tissue

What is the basement membrane (basal lamina)?

It is a specialised layer of extracellular matrix that is found on the surface of most epithelial layers.

What is the basal lamina made of?

  • Type IV collagen
  • Laminin
  • Other molecules

What are integrins?

These are cell surface receptors/proteins used to interact with extracellular matrix molecules such as fibronectin

How can the glomerular basement membrane be affected in diabetic nephropathies?

  • It plays a key role in the filtration unit of the kidney (the glomerulus is the filtration unit of the kidney)
  • Diabetic nephropathies can arise when this membrane thickens due to excess ECM deposition (Type IV Collagen) and renal filtration is restricted.

What happens in Alport syndrome?

Mutations in collagen IV result in abnormally split glomerular basement membrane and this is is associated with a loss of kidney filtration function.

Describe the structure of a laminin molecule.

One alpha, one beta and one gamma subunit intertwined to form a trimeric protein.

How many laminin variants does the human genome code for and why?

  • 16.
  • This is because it codes for 5 alpha, 3 gamma and 3 beta subunits.

What is the N-terminus (short end) of laminin used for?

  • Self-interaction with other NH2 groups
  • Interaction with integrins and nidogens
  • This contributes to the strength of the basal lamina

What is the C-terminus of laminin used for?

  • Interacts with cellular receptors
  • Regulates cell adhesion and other key cellular processes

What causes muscular dystrophy?

Laminin-alpha2 deficiency caused by a mutation in the LAMA2 gene

What does a lack of laminin-alpha2 lead to and why?

  • Muscular dystrophy
  • Laminin-alpha2 is one of the three units used to form the main heterotrimer laminin-211
  • Laminin-211 usually interacts with integrins and dystroglycans, and muscles connect to laminin, using it as an anchor
  • Integrins and dystroglycans are key to skeletal muscle contraction and development
  • A failure of laminin-211 (caused by a lack of laminin-alpha2) to interact with the alpha7beta1 integrin and the alpha-dystroglycan means there is a subsequent lack of interaction between muscle cells and integrins/dystroglycans
  • Overtime this causes progressive muscle weakness and degeneration.

What is the role of elastic fibres?

To provide elasticity for tissues

Describe the structure of an elastic fibre.

  • Elastin core with microfibrils surrounding
  • The microfibrils are rich in a protein called fibrillin

Describe the structure of a typical elastin molecule.

  • It is usually arranged in a random coil with alternating hydrophobic and hydrophilic domains.
  • The coils are cross-linked and can be stretched when needed to, conforming back to their regular shapes when required

What are the hydrophilic domains in elastin rich in?

  • Lysine.
  • This helps the different regions of elastin to be cross-linked

How do these regions contribute to the structure of elastin?

They allow the elastin molecules to be cross-linked with each other

What is Marfan’s syndrome?

  • It is a disorder which involves a mutation in the fibrillin-1 component of elastin molecules
  • Fibrillin is an important component on the microfibrils of elastic fibres, hence this mutation can drastically reduce the elasticity of a tissue
  • This leads to the formation of diverse manifestations and disarray within elastic fibres and can lead to increased susceptibility to aortic ruptures
  • It affects the cardiovascular, ocular and skeletal systems

What is fibronectin?

A multi-adhesive glycoprotein that interacts with cell surface receptors and other molecules.

Describe the structure of a fibronectin molecule.

  • Dimeric protein joined using disulphide bonds
  • Heparin binding site
  • Integrin binding site
  • Collagen binding site

Recall three functions of fibronectin.

  • Blood clotting
  • Regulating cell adhesion and tissue repair
  • Linking the extracellular matrix to the actin cytoskeleton

What process gives rise to different types of fibronectin?

Alternative splicing.

How does fibronectin link the extracellular matrix to the actin cytoskeleton?

  • The collagen (ECM) binds onto the fibronectin at the binding site
  • The integrin also binds onto the fibronectin at the binding site
