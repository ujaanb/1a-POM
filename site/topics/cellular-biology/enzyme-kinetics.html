Overview

What is the role of enzymes in our bodies?

To act as catalysts, ensuring that the many biochemical reactions taking place in our body do so at a rate that can support life.

What is Michaelis constant (Km)?

  • The concentration of substrate which allows an enzyme to function at half its maximal rate
  • It can be used to compare relative enzyme activity

What is Vmax?

  • Vmax of enzyme is the maximum rate of reaction
  • Maximum rate of substrate processing by enzymes

What is the purpose of a reaction buffer?

The buffer maintains the pH of the reaction mixture and supplies potassium (K+) or ammonium (NH4+) ions for optimal enzyme activity

Describe the steps involved to set up this practical used to determine Km and Vmax of chymotrypsin.

  • Add relevant volume of reaction buffer to cuvette using pipette
  • Add relevant volume of GPNA stock solution (substrate) to cuvette using pipette
  • Mix together with thumb placed on top of the cuvette opening
  • Place the cuvette in the spectrophotometer, calibrating so the absorbance reads zero
  • Add relevant volume of enzyme to cuvette using pipette
  • Place cuvette in spectrophotometer immediately, starting a timer and measuring absorbance at time intervals
  • Vary the concentrations of the GPNA stock solution in order to assess the enzyme activity at different substrate concentrations
  • Keep the enzyme concentration constant at all GPNA concentrations
  • NOTE: As you decrease the volume of GPNA, you must increase the volume of buffer solution being added to the cuvette

Serine protease found in the digestive system of mammals.

What reaction are we measuring in this practical?

  • Breakdown of GPNA into N-glutaryl-L-phenyl alanine and p- nitroaniline
  • We are measuring p-nitroaniline in the spectrophotometer

Why are we are measuring p-nitroaniline in the spectrophotometer?

  • We are measuring p-nitroaniline in the spectrophotometer because it produces a yellow colour, and hence absorbance
  • This helps us to monitor product formation

What is V0?

Initial rate of reaction

What is Km?

  • Km is known as Michaelis Constant and is defined as the concentration of substrate at which a particular enzyme at a particular concentration works at half its maximal velocity.
  • It helps us to compare the relative strength of enzyme-substrate complexes

What does a high/low Km show?

  • A high Km shows typically weak binding between enzymes and substrates and hence low enzymatic activity
  • A low Km shows typically strong binding between enzymes and substrates and hence high enzymatic activity

What is a Lineweaver-Burk plot?

A double reciprocal plot used to determine Km and Vmax

Describe how a Lineweaver-Burk plot helps us to determine Km and Vmax.

  • The x-intercept = -1/Km
  • The y-intercept = 1/Vmax
  • x-axis = 1/substrate concentration
  • y-axis = 1/initial rate of reaction Image showing how the Lineweaver-Burk plot can help us to calculate Km and Vmax.
  • The first figure is a regular rate of reaction against substrate concentration graph.
  • The second figure shows a Lineweaver-Burk plot.
  • 1 / V = 1 / initial rate of reaction (V for velocity)
  • 1 / [S] = 1 / substrate concentration

What is the turnover number (Kcat) of an enzyme?

  • The number of bonds that a single enzyme can cleave in a second
  • i.e the number of molecules that a single enzyme can process in a given unit of time

How can the turnover number (Kcat) of an enzyme be calculated?

  • Turnover number (Kcat) = Vmax / enzyme concentration
  • This is because Vmax measures the maximum rate at which product formation occurs (and hence maximum rate at which enzyme acts on substrate) and enzyme concentration tells us

how many enzymes are involved this product formation

What effect does a competitive antagonist (one that binds in or near the enzyme’s active site) have on Km and Vmax?

  • It increases Km but has no effect on Vmax
  • This is because the enzyme’s function has not been affected, but the substrate concentration required to bind to a given concentration of enzyme has increased as the enzyme’s affinity for the substrate has decreased due to increased competition for the active site (hence the name competitive inhibitor)

What effect does a non-competitive antagonist have on Km and Vmax?

  • Non-competitive inhibitors have no effect on Km but act to lower Vmax
  • This is because they induce conformational changes on the enzyme

which causes a reduction in the Vmax, but do not actually change the affinity of substrate molecules binding to enzymes, so the same amount of substrate is required to induce similar effects Graph showing the effect that competitive antagonists have on Lineweaver- Burk plots.
