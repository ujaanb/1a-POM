Overview

Describe how pyruvate is converted to acetyl CoA.

  • NAD+ —> NADH
  • HS-CoA is just coenzyme A with Sulfur and Hydrogen
  • The pyruvate dehydrogenase complex catalyses this reaction

What happens when acetyl CoA (2C) enters the mitochondrial matrix for the TCA Cycle?

It reacts with oxaloacetate (4C) to produce citrate (6C)

Describe briefly what happens in the TCA Cycle.

  • Acetyl CoA (2C) enters from the Link Reaction and reacts with oxaloacetate (4C) to form citrate (6C)
  • Redox and decarboxylation reactions occur which produce NADH, CO2, GTP and FADH2 molecules using the citrate and convert it back to oxaloacetate (4C)
  • This oxaloacetate (4C) has now been reproduced and can be used in another round of the TCA Cycle.

Describe the molecules that are produced via one TCA Cycle.

  • 2x CO2 produced from citrate (6C) —> oxaloacetate (4C)
  • 3x NADH from different reactions (2 arise alongside CO2 removals)
  • 1x FADH2 from 4C to 4C reaction
  • 1x GTP from 4C to 4C reaction

How much ATP (equivalents) can hence be produced via one TCA Cycle?

12x ATP.

How does the TCA Cycle help to produce energy, if no ATP is even produced?

  • The reduced coenzymes (3x NADH, 1x FADH2) that are produced in the TCA Cycle help to produce the ATP in oxidative phosphorylation
  • They supply the H+ and e- used in the electron transport chain

What stops the TCA Cycle from occurring under anaerobic conditions?

  • The TCA Cycle requires oxidised coenzymes (NAD+/FAD) for it to proceed, so it can reduce them to NADH/FADH2 during the cycle
  • Since these oxidised coenzymes can only be produced via their reoxidation during oxidative phosphorylation (where oxygen accepts their electrons), a lack of oxygen would mean the coenzymes continue to exist in their reduced state
  • Hence the TCA Cycle requires oxygen to go ahead.

Where are the enzymes used in the TCA Cycle stored?

In the mitochondrial matrix as they are soluble proteins.

What enzyme is the exception to this rule and where is it found?

  • Succinate dehydrogenase.
  • This enzyme is used to form complex II of the electron transport chain and so is found on the inner mitochondrial membrane

How do amino acids enter the TCA Cycle?

  • Proteins are down into amino acids
  • The carbon skeletons of these amino acids can form acetyl CoA or other intermediates present in the TCA Cycle
  • The intermediates formed can then either be used to make the same products from the TCA Cycle or in gluconeogenesis (reverse of TCA Cycle)

What does the term glucogenic imply?

  • The amino acids that form TCA Cycle intermediates can produce either glucose or ketone bodies
  • Those that produce glucose are described as glucogenic

What does the term ketogenic imply?

  • The amino acids that form TCA Cycle intermediates can produce either glucose or ketone bodies (i.e acetyl CoA)
  • Those that produce ketone bodies are described as ketogenic

What is a transamination reaction?

A reaction that involves the transfer of an amine group from one molecule to another.

How is a transamination reaction useful for amino acid metabolism?

  • Transferring amine groups from amino acids to keto acids allows amino acids to be metabolised without harm to the cell
  • This is because metabolising amine groups can be toxic to humans
  • Overall, it helps us to metabolise amino acids efficiently.

What are aminotransferases?

Enzymes that are involved in the catalysis of transamination reactions.

Which two molecules are involved in transamination reactions for amino acids?

Amino acids and ketoacids

How is urea produced in protein metabolism?

  • When amino acids are metabolised, only the carbon skeleton is productive (gives us energy)
  • The amine group is hence not metabolised into the TCA Cycle, but is instead transferred to the ketoacid via transamination, while the carbon skeleton of the amino acid produces an intermediate
  • The “other molecule” hence has an amine group on it but gets rid of it and as a result, urea is produced via the urea cycle

What is the difference between a ketoacid and an amino acid?

  • A ketoacid has a ketone functional group
  • An amino acid has an amine functional group

What is the role of the glycerol-phosphate shuttle?

  • Its role is to transfer NADH (electrons) from the cytosol into the mitochondrial matrix so that it can be reoxidised in oxidative phosphorylation
  • It does this for skeletal muscle and the brain
  • NOTE: this is done for NADH only and not FADH2 because only glycolysis products have this issue (of going from cytoplasm to mitochondrial matrix)

Describe the function of the glycerol-phosphate shuttle.

  • NADH adds its electrons to Dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP), converting it to glycerol-3-phosphate and NADH becomes oxidised to NAD+
  • This reaction is catalysed by cytoplasmic glycerol-3-phosphate dehydrogenase
  • Glycerol-3-phosphate adds these electrons back to FAD, reducing it to FADH2 and converting itself back to Dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP)
  • This reaction is catalysed by mitochondrial glycerol-3- phosphate dehydrogenase
  • FADH2 then passes its electrons onto coenzyme Q (ubiquinone - via complex II in the electron transport chain) where they can be used in oxidative phosphorylation

Describe the path that the electrons from NADH take through the glycerol- phosphate shuttle.

  • Electrons are transferred from NADH to DHAP by cytosolic glycerol-3- phosphate dehydrogenase in order to convert the DHAP to glycerol- 3-phosphate
  • The electrons are then are transferred to FAD by mitochondrial glycerol-3-phosphate dehydrogenase when glycerol-3-phosphate is converted back to DHAP
  • The FADH2 can then transfer these electrons to coenzyme Q (ubiquitone) via complex II in the electron transport chain.

What is the role of the malate-aspartate shuttle?

  • Its role is to transfer NADH (electrons) from the cytosol into the mitochondrial matrix so that it can be reoxidised in oxidative phosphorylation
  • It does this for the liver, kidney and heart

How can oxaloacetate be converted to malate for the malate-aspartate shuttle?

Reduction via the addition of two hydrogen atoms (H2)

Describe the function of the malate-aspartate shuttle.

  • Aspartate reacts with alpha-ketoglutarate in a transamination reaction to form oxaloacetate and glutamate via aspartate transaminase
  • Oxaloacetate is reduced to malate via malate dehydrogenase
  • During this reaction, the NADH is oxidised to form NAD+ and the electrons are transferred to the malate
  • Malate takes the electrons through the malate-alphaketoglutarate antiporter whilst alphaketoglutarate moves in the opposite direction
  • Malate is then oxidised back to oxaloacetate via malate dehydrogenase and NAD+ is reduced again to form NADH
  • Oxaloacetate is then reacted with glutamate to form aspartate and alphaketoglutarate via aspartate transferase
  • The aspartate travels through the aspartate-glutamate antiporter (while glutamate goes the other way) and another cycle can begin.

What are antiporters and what are the names of the two present in the malate-aspartate shuttle?

  • They allow two molecules to travel in opposite directions through them
  • Glutamate-aspartate
  • Malate-alphaketoglutarate
  • This is necessary because the movement of these molecules are effectively what facilitates the transfer of the NADH electrons from the cytosol to the mitochondrial matrix

Which enzyme in the malate-aspartate shuttle is responsible for the oxidation of NADH in the cytosol and its subsequent reduction in the mitochondria?

Malate dehydrogenase (MDH)

What is reoxidation of cofactors (what happens)?

This is when cofactors lose the electrons they have gained previously

(from glycolysis/Krebs Cycle)

How many ATP molecules are formed by the reoxidation of NADH?

3

How many ATP molecules are formed by the reoxidation of FADH2?

2

How else can the intermediates of glycolysis and the TCA Cycle help individuals?

  • They can be used to provide building blocks for biosynthetic reactions.
  • Formation of cholesterol from acetyl CoA
  • Formation of nucleotides from glucose-6-phosphate via the pentose phosphate pathway
  • Formation of amino acids from ketoacids (reverse of original transamination)

Describe the effects that TCA Cycle enzyme defects can have on individuals.

  • Malfunction of enzymes involved in the TCA Cycle can cause certain intermediates to not be produced, and hence the cycle won’t be run through correctly
  • The cell will hence choose to switch to glycolysis as the main form of ATP production

What is the name of the shuttle used to transport fatty acids from the cytoplasm to the mitochondria of cells for metabolism?

Carnitine shuttle

Describe the structure of a fatty acid.

It is a straight-chain hydrocarbon which has a -COOH group on the end of the molecule.

Diagram showing the beta-oxidation cycle

Describe how fatty acids are prepared for the beta-oxidation cycle.

  • Initially, fatty acids are converted to acyl CoA species by acyl CoA synthetase enzymes
  • NOTE: Acyl CoA species (i.e acetyl CoA) refer to these acyl groups on fatty acids attached to Coenzyme A
  • They do this through the action of acyl CoA synthetase and use energy from 2 high-energy ATP bonds
  • This can come from either the breakdown of ATP to either AMP in one ATP molecule, or ATP to ADP in two molecules

Which type of molecule enters the beta-oxidation cycle?

  • Acyl CoA species
  • This is after they have been converted from their acyl (fatty acid) forms by acyl CoA synthetase

Describe what occurs in the beta-oxidation cycle for fatty acids.

  • During the beta-oxidation cycle, a series of oxidation + hydrolysis, oxidation and thiolysis (reaction with HS-CoA to shorten the chain) reactions occur to the original acyl CoA molecule
  • During this process 1x FADH2 and 1x NADH are produced
  • The acetyl CoA species can also be used to produce more ATP through the Krebs Cycle
  • Overall, acyl CoA’s (derived from fatty acids) are broken down to acetyl CoA (2Cs) and a shortened acyl CoA (by 2Cs as it has lost acetyl CoA) which can be used in another beta oxidation cycle, whilst producing 1x NADH and 1x FADH2 in the process

Describe the role and function of the carnitine shuttle.

  • Acyl CoA is generated in the first step of fatty acid beta-oxidation
  • Its role is to transport acyl CoA from the cytosol to the mitochondrial matrix, where it can be metabolised
  • It uses enzymes called carnitine acyltransferases to convert carnitine to acyl carnitine outside and acyl carnitine to carnitine inside so that the acyl group can be transported into the matrix by a translocase
  • The acyl group carried by the acyl carnitine is then added to CoA in the matrix to reform the acyl CoA

What type of genetic disorder is primary carnitine deficiency?

  • Autosomal recessive
  • Mutations in a gene known as SLC22A5 which encodes a carnitine transporter result in reduced ability of cells to take up carnitine, needed for the β-oxidation of fatty acids.

How does primary carnitine deficiency affect individuals?

  • The transporter deficiency means that less acyl CoA is transported into the mitochondrial matrix as less acyl carnitine goes through the carnitine shuttle
  • This ultimately means less fatty acids are metabolised as they cannot be metabolised in the cytosol.

How can a primary carnitine deficiency be treated?

It can be remedied using carnitine supplements.

What type of molecule enters the beta-oxidation cycle?

Acyl CoA

What are the products of one beta oxidation cycle?

  • 1x acetyl CoA (2C)
  • 1x shortened acyl CoA (minus 2C)
  • 1x NADH
  • 1x FADH2

Describe what occurs when palmitic acid (16C) is beta-oxidised.

  • It is converted to palmitoyl CoA (16C)
  • It goes through the beta-oxidation cycle 7 times to produce 8 acetyl- CoA molecules (2C), 7 NADH and 7 FADH2
  • It produces 8 acetyl-CoA molecules because the last cycle produces 2x acetyl CoA molecules instead of 1x acetyl CoA and 1x shortened acyl CoA
  • These can be used to produce ATP, and A LOT is produced.

Describe briefly what lipogenesis is.

  • It is the formation of a fatty acid.
  • Effectively the reversal of beta oxidation (fatty acid metabolism/breakdown)

What are the two molecules involved in lipogenesis?

  • Malonyl CoA (3C) and acetyl CoA (2C)
  • These molecules are used as the building blocks of lipogenesis
  • Acyl carrier proteins are used to enable the continuation of lipogenesis - they act as a base upon which more fatty acids can be added
