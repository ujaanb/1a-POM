Overview

What is a tumour?

  • Any kind of mass-forming lesion (lesion meaning an area which has suffered injury from disease or injury)
  • It can be neoplastic or non-neoplastic
  • i.e they may or may not have escaped normal constraints on cell proliferation

What is a neoplasm?

  • The autonomous growth of tissue that have escaped normal constraints on cell proliferation
  • i.e any tissue that has grown too big without external stimulation, but with cell cycle defects
  • Cancers are malignant neoplasms

What is the difference between a benign and malignant neoplasm?

  • Neoplasia describes cells that have escaped normal constraints on cell proliferation
  • A benign neoplasm is localised and so is harmless
  • A malignant neoplasm can metastasise and invade other tissue locally/distantly and so is dangerous

How would you distinguish between a benign and malignant tumour upon physical examination?

  • A benign neoplasm can be moved around on examination when palpating its surface
  • A malignant neoplasm cannot be moved around on examination

when palpating its surface

This is because it has often invaded other tissue and become attached to it (i.e muscles)

What is metastasis?

  • This is when a malignancy has spread to distant sites around the body via blood vessels
  • It is good to diagnose a cancer before metastasis

Which malignant tumour is typically known to not always result in death?

Skin cancers (melanomas)

Which benign tumour is typically known to result in death and why?

  • Brain tumours
  • This is because it stops the flow of CSF between ventricles
  • Thyroid and other endocrine gland benign tumours can also be harmful

What is a hamartoma?

  • A localised, benign overgrowth of one or more mature cell types - they are harmless
  • A discrete mass (tumour) is formed within this tissue that consists of multiple cell types
  • It is not simply a “benign neoplasm” because a hamartoma is distinguished by having multiple cell types
  • The cell types are usually already present in the tissue, they just tend to clump together to form this distinct mass

Is a hamartoma neoplastic?

  • Yes.
  • The cells involved have escaped the normal constraints of cell proliferation and this is how they combine together to form their distinct mass.

What is a heterotopia?

  • This is where there is a normal group of cells (tissue) being found in parts of the body where they are not normally present
  • i.e pancreas tissue found in large intestine wall

What does the prefix “chondro” suggest in cancer classification?

The tumour is derived from cartilage

What does the suffix “-oma” suggest in cancer classification?

A benign tumour

What does the suffix “-sarcoma” suggest in cancer classification?

  • A malignant tumour
  • In any tumour classification, the prefix refers to the type of tissue affected, and the suffix refers to the malignancy of the tumour.

What are the two types of haematological neoplasms?

  • Lymphomas
  • Leukaemias
  • Benign tumours are very rare for haematological neoplasms.

What is the difference between a lymphoma and a leukaemia?

  • A lymphoma is a cancer derived from lymphocytes
  • The lymphocytes would be found anywhere in the bloodstream, not the bone marrow
  • A leukaemia is a cancer derived from the bone marrow
  • However, since lymphocytes that arise from the bone marrow, what

are these cancers called?

Cancers in the lymphocytes still present in the bone marrow are classified as lymphocytic leukaemias

What is a teratoma?

  • A tumour derived from a germ cell
  • Can be derived from all three germ cell layers (i.e endoderm, mesoderm, ectoderm)
  • They can occur in immature or mature cells.

Where are the most common sites that you would find teratomas at?

  • Testes
  • Ovaries Teratomas can be derived from both immature and mature cells. Which is worse?
  • Immature teratomas are worse because immature cells have much higher proliferative capacity
  • This leads to higher risk of malignancy and hence systemic damage

What is the difference between dysplasia and cancer?

  • Cancer usually involves invasion, dysplasia does not
  • Invasion is seen in malignant neoplasms, dysplasia is seen in benign neoplasms
  • Invasion (in cancer) involves cells directly invading adjacent connective tissue and other structures (i.e blood vessels)
  • Dysplasia means the cells show cytological features of cancer (i.e ratio of cytoplasm:nucleus) but do not invade
  • i.e the intermediate stage between normality and cancer

What is the difference between metastasis of benign and malignant tumours?

  • Benign tumours are non-invasive, malignant tumours are invasive
  • NOTE: Not all malignant tumours metastasise (spread through the blood). Some simply invade locally.

What is the difference between invasion and metastasis?

  • Invasion describes local encroachments of a neoplasm
  • i.e breast cancer attaching to pectoralis major muscle
  • Metastasis describes systemic encroachments of a neoplasm where the neoplasm spreads through the blood to affect other structures

What is meant by differentiation of tumours?

  • Differentiation describes how much the cells of the tumour resemble the cells they originated from
  • Well differentiated cells look a lot like the cells they derive from
  • Poorly differentiated cells do not really look like the cells they derive from

What is the difference between malignant and benign tumour cells cytologically?

  • Malignant tumour cells tend to have a much higher nuclear:cytoplasmic ratio than the cells of the tissue they are derived from
  • Benign tumour cells don’t typically possess this trait.

What is meant by the growth pattern of tumours?

  • Growth pattern describes how well the architecture of the neoplastic tissue resembles the architecture of the tissue it is derived from
  • It is similar to differentiation, but whilst differentiation refers to cytological appearance, growth pattern is all about tissue architecture

What is the difference in growth patterns between tumour cells and their derived cell types?

  • Poorly differentiated tumours look a lot less similar to the tissue they derived from
  • Well differentiated tumours look a lot more similar to the tissue they derived from

Recall five ways that neoplasms can spread.

  • Direct extension (structures being in close proximity to each other)
  • i.e thyroid cancer patients having coarse voices due to proximity of recurrent laryngeal nerve to thyroid gland
  • Haematogenous (via blood vessels)
  • Lymphatic (through lymphatic vessels to lymph nodes)
  • Transcoelomic (via seeding of body cavities such as pleural cavities)
  • Perineural (via nerves)
  • Through injections (i.e biospies)

What is the ‘TNM’ system used for?

To describe the stage and how much a tumour has spread

Describe how the TNM system works.

  • T = size of tumour and extent of invasion by the tumour
  • N = number of lymph nodes affected by the tumour
  • M = presence of distant metastases (how bad the metastasis is)
