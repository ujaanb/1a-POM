Overview

What is the difference between hypoxia and anoxia?

  • Hypoxia is diminished oxygen levels
  • Anoxia is total oxygen lack

What is substrate-level phosphorylation?

It is the production of ATP by the transfer of a high-energy phosphate group from one intermediate substrate to ADP

Give an example of substrate-level phosphorylation in glycolysis.

  • Step 7
  • 1,3-bisphosphoglycerate —> 3-phosphoglycerate
  • ADP + Pi —> ATP

Where in the mitochondria does the TCA Cycle occur?

Mitochondrial matrix

Where in the mitochondria does oxidative phosphorylation occur?

  • In the inner mitochondrial membrane.
  • Numerous folds within the cristae increase the surface area where it

can take place.

See lecture PDF.

Why does the reoxidation of FADH2 produce 2 molecules of ATP but NADH produces 3?

The electron transport chain has complexes/membrane proteins which

are used to pump protons into the intermembrane space to generate ATP via the action of ATP synthase

  • FADH2 electrons enter at complex II in the electron transport chain, and bypass complex I, one of the complexes that pump aforementioned protons
  • This means that less ATP is synthesised via the reoxidation of FADH2 as NADH electrons instead enter the chain at complex I as NADH results in more H+ pumped into the intermembrane space

What is ubiquinone?

An alternative name given to coenzyme Q (the key coenzyme required for procession of the electron transport chain)

What is succinate dehydrogenase?

  • It is an enzyme that is found in the inner mitochondrial membrane that makes up complex II of the electron transport chain.
  • NOTE: Each complex is actually an enzyme

How does succinate dehydrogenase perform its function?

  • It passes electrons directly to coenzyme Q (ubiquinone) via the action of cofactor FADH2
  • In the process, it also passes protons to coenzyme Q to form QH2

Diagram showing the electron transport chain

What is a redox couple?

A substrate that can exist in both oxidised and reduced forms (i.e

NAD+/NADH, FAD/FADH2)

What is reduction potential?

The ability of a redox couple to accept or donate electrons.

What does a negative/positive redox potential mean?

  • Negative means the couple is more likely to donate electrons and has a greater reducing power than hydrogen
  • Positive means the couple is more likely to accept electrons and has a greater oxidising power than hydrogen

What happens to electrons as they are pumped across the electron transport chain?

They lose energy

Where does the energy go?

  • To be used to pump protons to the intermembrane space and form a concentration gradient across the inner membrane
  • This allows protons to move across the membrane, and energy from this movement is transferred to the F1 subunit of ATP synthase to make ATP Explanation of energy transfers occurring during oxidative phosphorylation.
  • Electrons move across electron carrier proteins (complexes) - this movement generates energy
  • Energy is transferred to pump protons to the intermembrane space
  • Protons move down the electrochemical/concentration gradient, this generates energy
  • This energy is transferred to the F1 subunit of ATP synthase to be used in ATP production.

Where is ATP synthase located?

On the inside of the inner mitochondrial membrane

What are the two portions that make up ATP synthase called?

F0 (membrane bound) and F1 (projecting into matrix).

What smaller subunits make up these two portions respectively?

  • F0 - a, b and c
  • F1 - alpha, beta and gamma

What happens to ATP synthase when protons move into the mitochondrial matrix?

  • The F1 subunit starts to rotate.
  • This is because the alpha, beta and gamma subunits have varying affinities for ADP, Pi and ATP subunits
  • Conformational energy then flows from the rotation of this catalytic subunit (F1) and is transferred to ADP and Pi, allowing them to make ATP
  • The energy is ultimately converted to chemical energy which remains present in phosphoanhydride bonds of ATP.
  • This is chemiosmosis

How does ATP synthase decide whether to make or consume ATP?

  • It depends on the direction of proton flow across the inner mitochondrial membrane
  • Protons flowing from space to matrix will trigger the making of ATP
  • Protons flowing from matrix to space will trigger the consumption of ATP.

What is the purpose of the oxygen electrode?

  • To measure the concentration of oxygen within a solution
  • This hence allows us to measure oxidative phosphorylation by monitoring the consumption of oxygen

Which metals are present at the cathode and anode?

  • Cathode - platinum
  • Anode - silver

What happens at the platinum cathode?

  • O2 + 4H+ + 4e- → 2 H2O
  • Oxygen is reduced to water

What happens at the silver anode?

  • 4 Ag0 + 4Cl- → AgCl + 4e
  • This is to complete the circuit, so there is current running through it

How does the oxygen electrode allow us to measure the rate of oxidative phosphorylation?

  • It is a device that allows us to measure oxygen levels in a solution
  • By measuring the rate of oxygen consumption in mitochondria, we can measure the rate of oxidative phosphorylation

What does respiratory control describe in oxidative phosphorylation?

  • It describes how ADP and inorganic phosphate control the rate of oxygen consumption in oxidative phosphorylation
  • This allows the body to adjust the rate of oxygen consumption according to how much energy is required by the body

How is oxygen then reduced to water in oxidative phosphorylation?

The e- from the electron transport chain and the H+ that move across the inner mitochondrial membrane are added to O2 to form water

What is a metabolic poison?

  • A molecule that interrupts the movement of e- across the electron transport chain, or interrupts the movement of H+ across the inner membrane to generate ATP
  • Both methods stop ATP from being synthesised which is their purpose.

How do cyanide (CN-) and azide act as metabolic poisons?

  • It binds to the haem group in complex IV of the electron transport chain which prevents electrons from flowing through
  • This ultimately stops ATP from being formed.

How does malonate act as a metabolic poison?

  • Malonate slows down the flow of electrons from complex II (succinate dehydrogenase) to coenzyme Q (ubiquinone)
  • It does this by inhibiting the oxidation of succinate to fumarate (step

6 of TCA Cycle)

How can dinitrophenol (DNP) be used to induce weight loss?

  • DNP works by pumping protons across the inner mitochondrial membrane and ensuring that the energy produced from this movement is not used by ATP synthase to make ATP
  • This then means that a lot of protons pumped are actually wasted (not used to make ATP), and more protons hence need to be pumped than usual to make the same amount of ATP
  • This means more electrons need to be pumped across the electron transport chain, more cofactors need to be reoxidised and hence produced and so more fuel (glucose, fatty acids) needs to be metabolised to produce similar amounts of ATP

Why is DNP no longer prescribed?

  • The difference between the dose which is considered effective and that which is considered fatal is so slight
  • Many patients ended up dying or suffering fatal injury before it was banned in 1937

What is non-shivering thermogenesis?

It is a process which (like DNP) uncouples ATP from proton pumping in response to low body temperatures

How does non-shivering thermogenesis work?

  • An alternative proton channel called UCP-1 is activated in response to low temperatures
  • Protons are pumped through this channel following an electrochemical gradient and instead of the energy from this movement being used to form ATP via ATP synthase, it is dissipated as heat energy

What is rotenone and how does it work?

It is also a metabolic poison which works by preventing electron transfer from complex I to coenzyme Q (ubiquinone)

What is oligomycin and how does it work?

  • It binds to the stalk of ATP synthase and prevents H+ flowing through it
  • This obviously stops ATP synthesis via the enzyme

What is cytochrome C?

Cytochrome C is an electron transport chain protein that transfers electrons between complex III and complex IV of the chain.
