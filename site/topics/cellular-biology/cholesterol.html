Overview

What are the three ways that humans can derive fats?

  • Diet
  • Lipogenesis (biosynthesis)
  • Release and breakdown of triglycerides from adipose tissue

What are bile salts?

Bile salts are derivatives of cholesterol used to aid lipases with their digestion of fats

How are bile salts released into the intestine?

They are released by the liver and they travel via the bile duct

What role do bile salts play when interacting with fats?

  • They emulsify and solubilise fats in the intestine, aiding their digestion and absorption
  • They do this by breaking them down into smaller molecules, making the digestion/absorption job easier as lipases can act on a greater surface area on the fats
  • They aid lipases

What can a lack of bile salts cause?

  • It results in the majority of fats passing through the gut undigested and unabsorbed
  • This, in turn can cause steatorrhea (fatty stool)

What is an amphipathic/amphiphilic molecule?

A molecule that has both hydrophobic and hydrophilic properties

Recall one amphipathic/amphiphilic molecule.

A chylomicron

What is Orlistat (tetrahydrolipstatin)?

A drug which is prescribed to inhibit gastric and pancreatic lipases (enzymes that break down lipids)

How does Orlistat work?

  • It works by inhibiting these lipases which results in a 30% reduction in fat absorption, most of which is excreted by the faecal route
  • It can be effective in treating obesity for up to two years

Recall three side effects of Orlistat.

  • Abdominal pain
  • Urgency to defecate (poo)
  • Increased flatus (bloating)
  • Steatorrhea

How are fats (lipids) transported around the body?

  • Using lipoproteins
  • Different density lipoproteins transport different types of fat

What are chylomicrons?

They are lipoproteins that are used to transport dietary fats

Describe the structure of a chylomicron.

  • Formed of triglycerides, apoproteins and phospholipids
  • Apoproteins embedded between the phospholipids

How do chylomicrons have apoproteins in their structures?

They receive them from HDLs after the release of apoproteins into the bloodstream

Why do HDLs donate apoproteins to chylomicrons?

The apoproteins are really important to the chylomicron’s structure because it is what allows the other cells in the body to recognise that it is a lipoprotein

Where are chylomicrons made?

In the small intestine by enterocytes

How are chylomicrons transported to the bloodstream?

Via the lymphatics

What are lipoprotein lipases used for?

  • They are used to access and break down the contents of lipoproteins such as the triglycerides
  • Specifically, the FFAs which can be used in beta-oxidation

Where are these enzymes located?

On the capillary endothelial cells lining the adipose, heart and skeletal muscle tissues.

What happens to the components of the triglyceride after the lipoprotein lipase has performed its function?

  • Glycerol is returned to the liver for gluconeogenesis
  • Fatty acids undergo beta-oxidation

How does the liver interact with chylomicrons?

  • After chylomicrons have been broken down and have transported the lipids to the relevant locations, the liver absorbs the remnants
  • This is important as we don’t want apoproteins and phospholipids accumulating in the blood, so the liver acts as a salvage organ

What is the role of a chylomicron?

To transport dietary lipids

What is the role of VLDL?

To transport endogenous (self-made) lipids

What is cholesterol?

  • It is a steroid whose function is to increase or decrease cell membrane stiffness, depending on temperature and the nature of the membrane.
  • It is classed as a sterol (type of steroid) because of its hydroxyl group

Where is the majority of the cholesterol in our bodies stored?

  • In our cell membranes (90%)
  • It can increase or decrease membrane stiffness, depending on temperature and nature of membrane

What is dietary cholesterol uptake limited to in humans?

~0.5g/day.

How is the majority of the cholesterol in our bodies obtained then?

The liver carries out de novo synthesis of cholesterol from acetyl CoA.

What are the three steps through which cholesterol is synthesised?

  • 1 - Synthesis of isopentenyl pyrophosphate (5C)
  • 2 - Condensation of six isopentenyl pyrophosphate monomers → squalene
  • 3 - Cyclisation and demethylation of squalene (30C) → cholesterol (27C)

Where do each of these steps take place?

  • 1 - Synthesis of isopentenyl pyrophosphate (6C) - CYTOPLASM
  • 2 - Condensation of six isopentenyl pyrophosphate monomers to form squalene - CYTOPLASM
  • 3 - Cyclisation and demethylation of squalene (30C) to form cholesterol (27C) - ENDOPLASMIC RETICULUM Biosynthetic pathways need reducing powers to proceed. Where does this typically come from?
  • NADPH.

Describe what happens in the first stage of cholesterol biosynthesis.

  • Two acetyl CoA (2C) molecules are condensed together to form acetoacetyl CoA (4C)
  • One CoA molecule is lost in this reaction catalysed by beta- ketothiolase
  • Acetoacetyl CoA (4C) is condensed with acetyl CoA (2C) to form 3- Hydroxy-3methylglutaryl CoA (6C) (HMG-CoA)
  • This reaction is catalysed using HMG-CoA synthase
  • HMG-CoA (6C) is reduced to form mevalonate (6C)
  • This is done using the major biosynthetic reducing power NADPH and the enzyme HMG-CoA reductase
  • Mevalonate (6C) is sequentially phosphorylated by various kinases before being decarboxylated to form 3-isopentenyl pyrophosphate (5C)

What is special about the reduction of HMG-CoA to mevalonate?

  • It can be inhibited by cholesterol, bile salts or mevalonate itself (product inhibition)
  • It is a key regulatory step and it can be described as a rate-limiting step.

What is an alternative fate for HMG-CoA?

Formation of ketone bodies

Describe how negative feedback is involved in the rate limiting step of cholesterol biosynthesis.

  • Cholesterol, mevalonate and bile salts are involved in inhibiting HMG- CoA reductase which is key for reducing HMG-CoA (6C) to mevalonate (6C)
  • This is so not too much cholesterol is synthesised and to prevent hypercholesterolemia.

What type of molecule is 3-isopentenyl pyrophosphate (5C)?

An isoprene.

How are isoprene units related to coenzyme Q (ubiquinone)?

  • Isoprene units are present on coenzyme Q and this gives them the properties that allow it to be confined (held) to the inner mitochondrial membrane for oxidative phosphorylation
  • This is because isoprene units have lipophilic properties

Describe what happens in the second stage of cholesterol biosynthesis.

  • Isopentenyl pyrophosphate (5C) is isomerised to form dimethylallyl pyrophosphate (5C)
  • Dimethylallyl pyrophosphate (5C) undergoes condensation with isopentenyl pyrophosphate (5C) to form Geranyl pyrophosphate (10C)
  • Geranyl pyrophosphate (10C) undergoes further condensation with isopentenyl pyrophosphate (5C) to form farnesyl-pyrophosphate (15C)
  • Two molecules of farnesyl-pyrophosphate (15C) condense to form squalene (30C)

Describe what happens in the third stage of cholesterol biosynthesis.

  • Squalene (30C) is reduced by NADPH in the presence of O2 to form squalene epoxide (30C)
  • This is catalysed by squalene monoxygenase
  • Squalene epoxide (30C) is then isomerased by squalene-epoxide lanosterol cyclase catalyses the formation of lanosterol (30C)
  • Lanosterol (30C) is reduced and demethylated three times to form cholesterol (27C)

Which molecule provides the reducing power for the third and final reaction in cholesterol biosynthesis?

NADPH

What are three important bodily biochemicals that are synthesised from cholesterol?

  • Steroid hormones
  • Vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol)
  • Bile salts

Why is cholesterol described as the basis for steroid hormones?

  • The precursor pregnenolone can be made from cholesterol using the enzyme desmolase
  • Pregnenolone is the starting point from which all five steroid hormones are derived.

How is vitamin D synthesised from cholesterol?

  • UV light (from the Sun) initiates the conversion of 7- dehydrocholesterol → previtamin D3
  • Previtamin D3 eventually results in calcitriol production via Previtamin D3 → Vitamin D3 → 25-OH cholecalciferol → 1,25- OH cholecalciferol (calcitriol)
  • Calcitriol (active Vitamin D) can be used as a key molecule in calcium metabolism

Why are lipoproteins important to us?

They are very useful in transporting hydrophobic molecules (i.e lipids), in aqueous environments (i.e the blood)

Anatomy of a lipoprotein

Why do cholesterols form cholesterol esters?

To allow them to become even more hydrophobic, and pack together even more tightly in the centre of the lipoproteins carrying them.

How do cholesterols form cholesterol esters?

  • Phosphatidylcholine (lecithin) transfers its acyl group to cholesterol via the action of the enzyme lecithin:cholesterol acyl transferase (LCAT)
  • This forms a cholesterol ester that has an acyl group on the end

How are VLDLs produced?

Synthesis in the liver

How are IDLs produced?

  • By triglyceride removal from VLDLs
  • This makes sense as VLDLs transfer triglycerides to the tissues that require them
  • Apoproteins are also transferred between VLDLs and HDLs to produce IDLs that have different receptors so they can be recognised by different cells

How are LDLs produced?

Cholesterol esters are transferred from HDL to IDL to form LDLs

How are HDLs produced?

Synthesis in the liver

How do VLDLs work in action?

  • When triglyceride/cholesterol levels are low, the lipoprotein lipases of the relevant tissues recognise the VLDLs and break them down to release the free fatty acid (FFA) and cholesterol content
  • The cholesterol can be used in various ways (steroids, vitamin D + bile salt production)
  • Likewise, the free fatty acids can be used as fuel (beta- oxidation)
  • The glycerol can be recouped by the liver for gluconeogenesis (it is converted to DHAP using glycerol-3-phosphate dehydrogenase)
  • Hence, the role of VLDL is to increase triglyceride and cholesterol levels in the areas that require them

Describe the mechanism by which HDLs function.

  • They return cholesterol from the peripheral tissues to the liver where it can be used or disposed of (reverse cholesterol transport)
  • By doing this, they help to lower total serum cholesterol

Describe the mechanism by which LDLs function.

  • They transport cholesterol from the liver to the peripheral tissues around the body
  • By doing this, they help to increase total serum cholesterol

What is the main difference in the structure between HDLs and LDLs?

LDLs have a much higher composition of cholesterol, with it making up to 40% of their weight.
