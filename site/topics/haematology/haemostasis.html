Overview

What is haemostasis?

Haemostasis is the “halting/inactivation of blood” following trauma to blood vessels

Recall two reasons why haemostasis is important.

  • To prevent blood loss from normal blood vessels
  • To prevent blood leaking from injured blood vessels

How is equilibrium an important aspect of haemostasis?

  • There needs to be a balance between coagulation factors/platelets and fibrinolytic factors/anticoagulant proteins
  • This is because they both need to be in the right abundance to prevent blood flow in the area of injury, but to allow blood flow in other areas of the body.

Recall three benefits of equilibrium in haemostasis.

  • To allow the stimulation of blood clotting processes following injury (coagulation - coagulation factors, platelets)
  • To limit the response to injury to a single area in the body (thrombosis - platelets)
  • To start the process that leads to the breakdown of the clot that is formed during healing (fibrinolysis - fibrinolytic factors)

Describe the vague process that occurs in a blood vessel in response to endothelial cell injury.

  • Vasoconstriction occurs
  • Unstable platelet plug forms in blood vessel to prevent blood loss
  • Stabilisation of the plug with fibrin occurs - coagulation
  • Dissolution of blood clot occurs alongside vessel repair in fibrolysis Diagram showing the haemostatic response to blood vessel injury.

What is primary haemostasis?

  • This is the process through which platelets aggregate together at the site of injury
  • “Unstable platelet plug”

What is secondary haemostasis?

This is when stabilisation of the plug with fibrin occurs -

COAGULATION

How are platelets derived from haematopoietic stem cells (HSCs)?

HSC —> Myeloid progenitor —> Megakaryocyte —> Thrombocyte (platelet)

Are platelets nucleated?

No.

Do platelets have granules?

Yes

What is the circulation lifespan of a platelet?

Around 10 days.

What component of platelets allows them to bind to collagen in blood vessels and where is it located?

  • Glycoproteins
  • They are located in the plasma membrane

What are the two types of platelet glycoproteins used to bind collagen?

  • Glp1a - alpha
  • Glp1b - beta

How does the action of these two glycoproteins differ?

  • Glp1a allows the platelet to bind directly to collagen
  • Glp1b requires the use of a von Willebrand factor to bind to collagen

Where is von-Willebrand factor found?

  • It is found in endothelial cells
  • Hence, when there is damage to the endothelial layer, von- Willebrand factor uncoils and allows binding of the GlpIb receptor to it

How does the binding of platelets to collagen encourage platelet- platelet interaction?

  • It allows the platelet to conform to a more rounded shape
  • This allows it to interact with other platelets better.

What does the adhesion of platelets to collagen lead to?

Activation of platelets which results from the release of their storage granule content.

What are the two types of granule released by platelets after adhesion?

  • Alpha granules
  • Dense granules
  • There are important molecules stored within the granules of these platelets

What is the open canalicular system?

  • This is the system used by platelets to release the contents of the granules they need to release after adhesion
  • The platelet membrane is invaginated to form this system

Recall the most important dense granule that is released by platelets following adhesion to collagen. Which other molecule is produced following platelet adhesion to the endothelial membrane?

  • ADP
  • Prostaglandins - specifically thromboxane A2

How is thromboxane A2 produced?

  • It is produced by platelets using arachidonic acid
  • Arachidonic acid is a component of cell membranes

What effect do these molecules have on platelets?

  • They have positive feedback effects which allows them to increase platelet recruitment, aggregation and activation
  • This forms the platelet plug

How do ADP and thromboxane have this effect?

  • ADP binds to the p2y12 receptor
  • Thromboxane A2 binds to the thromboxane A2 receptor

What occurs as a result of this platelet activation by ADP and thromboxane A2?

  • Platelet activation leads to a change in the conformation of the GlpIIb/IIIa (2b/3a) receptor on platelets
  • This process is known as “inside out” or “flip-flopping”

What does the GlpIIb/IIIa receptor do?

The GlpIIb/IIIa receptor provides binding sites for fibrinogen

How does fibrinogen then act to progress primary haemostasis?

It binds to the GlpIIb/IIIb receptor and this leads to “outside-in” signalling which allows platelets to link together and form the platelet plug

What is another role of thromboxane A2, other than activating platelets?

It acts as a vasoconstrictor, and this promotes the formation of platelets in the initial response to blood vessel injury.

What is prostacyclin?

  • It is a prostaglandin that is used as a vasodilator in response to the dissolution of blood clots, inhibiting platelet aggregation
  • PGI2 is used in endothelial cells

How is prostacyclin produced?

  • By endothelial cells from arachidonic acid
  • This makes sense as endothelial cells will be present when the blood vessel isn’t damaged and conditions need to return back to normal.

What is the difference between a prostacyclin and a prostaglandin?

  • A prostacyclin is a type of prostaglandin that has vasodilatory effects
  • i.e thromboxane A2 is also a type of prostaglandin, but this has vasoconstrictory effects

Recall the two steps involved in thromboxane A2 synthesis from arachadonic acid.

  • Arachadonic acid → Cyclic endoperoxides
  • Catalysed by cyclo-oxygenase
  • Cyclic endoperoxides → Thromboxane A2
  • Catalysed by thromboxane synthetase

Recall the two steps involved in prostacyclin synthesis from arachadonic acid.

  • Arachadonic acid → Cyclic endoperoxides
  • Catalysed by cyclo-oxygenase
  • Cyclic endoperoxides → Prostacyclin
  • Catalysed by prostacyclin synthetase

What are aspirin and clopidogrel?

They are anti-platelet drugs that are used to reduce the risk of heart attack and strokes for those with cardiovascular disease.

How does aspirin work?

  • It irreversibly blocks the action of Cyclo-Oxygenase (COx) which is a vital component in the production of thromboxane A2
  • This means that thromoboxane A2 cannot bind to the thromboxane A2 receptor and cannot activate platelets as it normally does, reducing the formation of blood clots.

Why does aspirin not inhibit the action of prostacyclin, since it also needs COx for its synthesis?

  • Endothelial cells are able to produce COx on their own (due to them having nuclei), meaning that they are not as affected by the action of aspirin
  • Overall there is hence a net decrease in platelet aggregation and the formation of blood clots.

How does clopidogrel work?

  • It works by preventing ADP from binding to its p2y12 receptor
  • Similarly, this prevents the activation of platelets and the positive feedback loop is not achieved to recruit more and more platelets to the site of vessel damage Diagram showing the contrasting effects thromboxane A2 and prostacyclin have in haemostasis.

What is another function of von Willebrand factor?

To carry Factor VIII (Eight)

Coagulation

What happens in coagulation?

Thrombin is generated, and this converts fibrinogen to fibrin, used to stabilise the platelet plug.

Why is coagulation required?

  • Coagulation is required to stabilise the platelet plug in larger blood vessels
  • In smaller vessels, the unstable platelet plug is sufficient, but not in larger ones.

What are clotting factors?

Clotting/coagulation factors are proteins in the blood that help form blood clots to stop bleeding when there is injury to blood vessels

What are the three sites where clotting factors are produced?

  • Liver
  • Endothelial cells - von Willebrand factor.
  • Megakaryocytes (platelet progenitors)

Where are most clotting factors made?

Liver

Where is von Willebrand factor made?

Endothelial cells and megakaryocytes

What is Factor II? Which clotting factors are dependent on Vitamin K to function?

  • Prothrombin.
  • Factor II (prothrombin)
  • Factor VII
  • Factor IX
  • Factor X

Why are these clotting factors dependent on Vitamin K?

  • For the carboxylation of their glutamic acid residues in the liver
  • This allows for their activation and use in haemostasis

What is tissue factor used for?

Initiation of coagulation after endothelial damage

Where is tissue factor located?

  • Underneath endothelial cells, extravascular cells and leukocytes
  • It is exposed after vascular damage

What are the three phases involved in coagulation?

  • Initiation
  • Amplification
  • Propagation

Describe how tissue factor is involved to produce Factor IIa (initiation phase). Which factor is used to directly generate thrombin from prothrombin? Which factors are involved in initiation?

  • Tissue factor binds to Factor 7a
  • This activates Factor 9 to 9a and Factor 10 to 10a
  • This activates Factor 2 (prothrombin) to produce a small amount of Factor 2a (thrombin)
  • Factor Xa
  • Along with its cofactor factor Va (in amplification)
  • VIIa
  • IX/IXa
  • X/Xa
  • II/IIa

What does Factor VIIa do in initiation?

Factor VIIa binds to Tissue Factor (TF) to initiate the cascade of reactions in initiation

What does Factor IX/IXa do in initiation?

  • Factor IX turns into Factor IXa after being activated by factor VIIa binding to Tissue Factor (TF)
  • It does not activate Factor Xa yet, as Factor VIIIa is required for this
  • Factor IXa is hence produced in preparation for it being required in amplification

What does Factor X/Xa do in initiation?

  • Factor X turns into Factor Xa after being activated by factor VIIa binding to Tissue Factor (TF)
  • Factor Xa then converts Factor II into Factor IIa to produce a little bit of thrombin
  • Factor Va is not needed for this initiation

What does Factor II/IIa do in initiation?

  • Factor II (prothrombin) is converted to Factor IIa (thrombin) in initiation by action of Factor Xa
  • This small amount of thrombin produced by initiation can start the amplification phase

What happens in the amplification phase? Which factors are involved in amplification?

  • Thrombin produced in propagation activates cofactors 5→5a and 8→8a as well as factor 11→11a and platelets
  • Factor 11 is used to activate Factor 9 to 9a which alongside Factor 8 can be used to generate 5 times more Factor 10a from Factor 10
  • This amplifies thrombin production from prothrombin by ~10^5 leading to rapid fibrinogen cleavage into fibrin
  • This stabilises the unstable platelet plug to form an insoluble fibrin clot
  • Factor IIa (thrombin)
  • Factor V/Va
  • Factor VIII/VIIIa
  • Factor IXa
  • Factor Xa
  • Factor XI/XIa

What does Factor IIa do in amplification?

Factor IIa, produced in small amounts during initiation, is used during amplification to activate Factor V→Va, Factor VIII→VIIIa, Factor XI→XIa and platelets

How is Factor V activated, and what does Factor V/Va do in amplification?

  • The activation of Factor V→Va is done by Factor IIa (thrombin)
  • Factor Va is a cofactor that assists Factor Xa to convert Factor II→IIa during amplification

How is Factor VIII activated, and what does Factor VIII/VIIIa do in amplification?

  • The activation of Factor VIII→VIIIa is done by Factor IIa (thrombin)
  • Factor VIIIa is a cofactor that assists Factor IXa to convert Factor X→Xa in large amounts during amplification

How is Factor IX activated, and what does Factor IXa do in amplification?

  • The activation of Factor IX→IXa has already been done by Tissue Factor (TF) binding to Factor VIIa during initiation
  • Factor XI is used to activate more Factor IX→IXa. More IXa is now present.
  • Factor IXa is used in amplification alongside Factor VIIIa to convert 10^5 times more Factor X→Xa

How is Factor X activated, and what does Factor Xa do in amplification?

  • The activation of Factor X→Xa has already been done by Tissue Factor (TF) binding to Factor VIIa during initiation
  • Factor Xa, alongside its cofactor Factor Va, is used to convert large amounts of Factor II→IIa

What does Factor XI/XIa do in amplification?

  • The activation of Factor XI→XIa is done by Factor IIa (thrombin)
  • Factor XIa is used to activate Factor IX→IXa, so it can then activate X→Xa

Describe the importance of Factor VIIIa in coagulation.

  • Factor VIIIa, a cofactor, is one of the essential components, alongside Factor IXa, required for the generation of 10^5 more Factor Xa in amplification
  • Factor Xa can then be used alongside cofactor Va to make Factor IIa (thrombin) from prothrombin (Factor II)
  • Thrombin then catalyses fibrinogen → fibrin

What happens in the propagation phase?

As a result of the increased thrombin production, higher amounts of fibrinogen is converted to fibrin to stabilise the blood clot and make it insoluble.

What is a zymogen?

A proenzyme (i.e Factor II, Factor IX), those that are converted to active forms such as Factor IIa and Factor IXa

How is a zymogen converted to its active form?

  • By the splitting of one or more peptide bonds.
  • This leads to exposure of the active site of the enzyme, making the enzyme active
  • It occurs via the action of cofactors V and VIII.

What characteristic of platelets allows them to accelerate these reactions?

  • The phospholipid surface.
  • It is believed that clotting factors work on the phospholipid surfaces of platelets

What role does Ca2+ (calcium) play in coagulation?

Binding clotting factors (i.e von Willebrand factor) to the phospholipid surfaces of platelets.

Why do we need coagulatory inhibitory mechanims?

  • To prevent coagulation spreading and occurring at other sides around the body that have not been injured
  • This can lead to thrombosis

Recall three coagulatory inhibitors.

  • Antithrombin
  • Protein C
  • Protein S

How does antithrombin work? Which molecule is use to potentiate the action of antithrombin?

  • A direct inhibitor of thrombin (IIa) and Factor Xa
  • Heparin
  • Antithrombin binds to endothelial cell associated heparins to cause potentiation of its effects

What are proteins C and S?

  • Indirect inhibitors of thrombin generation
  • Protein C acts directly and protein S is its cofactor.

Explain how the protein C pathway works to inhibit coagulation.

  • Thrombin binds to thrombomodulin (endothelial cell receptor that helps anticoagulation)
  • This activates Protein C
  • Protein C inactivates Factors Va and VIIIa in the presence of Protein S
  • This means Factor II cannot be converted to Factor IIa any longer and thrombin is no longer produced from prothrombin
  • This is because Factor Va and VIIIa help Factor Xa activate Factor II to Factor IIa
  • Clotting hence reduces as thrombin cannot do fibrinogen → fibrin

What is the direct role of protein C in the inhibition of coagulation?

To inactivate Factors Va and VIIIa which prevents the activation of Factor II (prothrombin) to Factor IIa (thrombin)

What is heparin?

An anticoagulant drug that works as a potentiator of antithrombin

How does heparin work?

  • It potentiates the action of antithrombin which leads to the direct inactivation of Factors Xa and IIa.
  • It does this by its pentasaccharide chain binding to antithrombin, altering antithrombin’s conformation to make it have a greater affinity for Factor Xa
  • Inactivation of Factors Xa and IIa both directly lead to no thrombin being produced and no further clotting

Is heparin an indirect or direct inhibitor?

  • Indirect
  • It potentiates the action of antithrombin, which is a direct inhibitor. So heparin itself is indirect A heparin chain is only 4 units long. What effect will this have on its function?
  • Heparin can function to inactivate Factor Xa even when it is short, but to inactivate Factor IIa, it needs to be longer
  • The chain being short will lead to it inactivating Factor Xa, but not Factor IIa

How do longer chains of heparin work?

Longer chains (pentasaccharides) of heparin can wrap around antithrombin and thrombin and inactivate both Factors IIa and Xa

How is heparin administered?

Intravenously or by subcutaneous injection

What is warfarin?

An anticoagulant drug that works as a Vitamin K antagonist

How does warfarin work?

  • It works by competing with Vitamin K to interfere with protein carboxylation
  • This prevents the synthesis of Factors II, VII, IX and X by the liver
  • This reduces the synthesis of coagulation factors and hence reduced clotting initiation, activation and propagation

How is warfarin administered?

  • Orally
  • It takes several days to take effect

Why does warfarin take several days to have its effect?

  • Warfarin works by antagonising Vitamin K which prevents synthesis of new clotting factors
  • This means existing clotting factors will still be able to function until they run out, at which time the effects of Warfarin will be visible

What is a DOAC and how does it work?

  • DOACs (Direct Oral Anticoagulants), are orally administered drugs that directly inhibit Factor IIa (thrombin) to prevent the formation of blood clots.
  • They work more rapidly due to its direct inhibitory effects on coagulation Fibrinolytic System and Tests of Coagulation

What is plasmin?

  • The principle fibrinolytic enzyme
  • It is not specific to fibrin, so it can break down other plasma proteins such as Factors V and VIII

What does plasmin do?

Plasmin is responsible for degrading a variety of plasma proteins but its specific physiologic role is to degrade fibrin clots.

How is plasmin activated?

  • By the action of tPA (tissue plasminogen activator) alongside plasminogen binding to fibrin
  • i.e plasminogen binds to fibrin when the fibrin clot is being broken down

How does plasminogen bind to fibrin?

Plasminogen binds to the lysine residues of fibrin

What does the breakdown of fibrin lead to?

The production of FDPs (fibrin degradation products)
