Overview

What are granulocytes?

They are a type of white blood cell that have granules in them.

What are the three main type of granulocyte and their colours?

  • Eosinophil (red)
  • Neutrophil (neutral)
  • Basophil (blue)

How can we tell that a granulocyte has matured?

It gets smaller and the development of granules key to its function begins.

Describe the difference in the appearances of eosinophils and basophils.

Eosinophils are more reddish in colour than basophils which are described as basic (alkaline), giving them a blue colour

What do granulocytes often look like in people with leukaemia?

They are bigger than usual, immature and they look like myeloblasts

Describe the typical differentiation path taken from a HSC to a granulocyte.

HSC → Common Myeloid Progenitor → (Myeloblast) → (Promyelocyte) → (Myelocyte) → (Band form) → Granulocyte

What is a monocyte?

  • They are a type of white blood cell which are used to destroy pathogens
  • They can differentiate into macrophages or dendritic cells.

Diagram showing blood stem cell hierarchy

What can myeloid growth factors be used for?

  • They can be used to activate the proliferation of myeloid (granulocytes + macrophages) cells and hence are an effective treatment for patients with pathologies such as leukaemia.
  • This is only in the case where a leukaemia patient has undergone chemotherapy, and so their lymphocyte count has decreased. Chemotherapy typically kills fast-proliferating cells, so white blood cells can catch collateral damage.
  • This is important as these white blood cells are important to the immune system
  • Granulocyte colony-stimulating factor (G-CSF), Granulocyte- macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF), Macrophage colony-stimulating factor (M-CSF) are examples of growth factors

What is the main role of a neutrophil?

It is used to phagocytose (mainly bacteria/fungi) and then kill these phagocytosed bacteria/fungi.

How long does the neutrophil survive for in the bloodstream before migrating to tissues?

  • 7-10 hours.
  • After this period they will migrate to tissues via chemotaxis.

What is chemotaxis?

Chemotaxis is the movement of an organism or entity in response to a chemical stimulus.

How does the neutrophil migrate from the bloodstream to the tissues around it, in order to fight infection? Which has a longer lifespan, an eosinophil, or a neutrophil?

  • Neutrophil extravasation (diapedesis)
  • Neutrophils, via a complex pathway involving adhesion molecules such as selectins & integrins, cytokines & chemokines and the endothelium, move towards the interstitium and tissue through the endothelial membrane
  • Eosinophil.

What are the two main roles of an eosinophil?

  • To defend against parasitic agents such as helminth worms.
  • To regulate Type I hypersensitivity reactions (those allergic reactions reacted to in <24 hours)

What is a hypersensitivity reaction?

  • They are exaggerated or inappropriate reactions conducted by the immune system in response to an antigen or an allergen
  • It happens when the immune system mistakes something harmless as something that is harmful

How do eosinophils regulate these Type I hypersensitivity reactions?

They inactivate the histamine and leukotrienes released by basophils and mast cells.

What is a leukotriene?

An inflammatory mediator released by basophils

What is the role of a leukotriene?

  • They contract airway smooth muscle, increase vascular permeability, increase mucus secretion, and attract and activate other inflammatory cells
  • They just cause inflammation really.

What is a special property of basophils?

Its granules contain stores of histamine and heparin, as well as proteolytic enzymes.

What are the main roles of a basophil?

  • To act as an inflammatory mediator and induce inflammatory effects
  • To mediate immediate-type hypersensitivity reactions
  • i.e Type I

How are basophils involved in mediation of immediate-type hypersensitivity reactions?

  • The Immunoglobin-E (IgE) coated basophils release heparin and proteases
  • IgE binds to the antigens
  • This acts to mediate immediate-type hypersensitivity reactions.

How do mast cells differ from basophils?

  • Mast cells reside in tissues
  • Basophils are found freely in the circulation

What is heparin?

It is an anticoagulant that is naturally occurring and can also be administered as a drug

What does heparin do?

Heparin potentiates the action of antithrombin and hence helps to prevent harmful clots from forming in the blood

How do monocytes differ from macrophages?

  • Monocytes and macrophages are two versions of the same cell
  • A monocyte is the name of the cell when it flows freely in the circulation
  • A monocyte becomes a macrophage when it migrates to the tissue
  • It is also known as a histiocyte in this form

Recall two functions of macrophages.

  • Phagocytosis of antigenic material
  • Antigen presentation to T cells. They are antigen-presenting cells.
  • Storage and release of iron

Is it monocytes or macrophages that store iron?

Macrophages (tissue)

What are the two types of cell monocytes can differentiate into after migrating from the blood to the interstitium?

  • Dendritic cells
  • Macrophages

What is a characteristic feature of monocytes/macrophages on analysis that allows them to be easily identifiable?

  • Macrophages (not monocytes) are involved in the storage and release of iron to erythrocytes
  • Structures called ‘erythroid islands’ can form around monocytes which allow them to obtain iron from these cells.
  • This is the purple “circle” around the main cell body shown below
  • You can only see these in the tissues, not the bloodstream, because macrophages are not found in the tissues

What type of white blood cell interacts with red blood cells?

Macrophages.

How do macrophages interact with red blood cells?

  • They are involved in the storage and release of iron to erythrocytes
  • Structures called ‘erythroid islands’ can form around macrophages which allow them to obtain iron from these cells.
  • They transfer this iron in bone marrow during erythropoiesis, as macrophages are not found in the blood (they would be called monocytes)

What is a plasma cell?

A further differentiated B cell that makes antibodies to combat pathogens.

What makes the edge of plasma cells slightly darker?

  • The presence of ribosomes
  • They need this larger number of ribosomes because they are constantly producing antibodies (proteins) against pathogenic antigens

Why are lymphocytes found in the lymph nodes?

  • Blood is pushed out of the blood vessels into the interstitium and recollected by lymphatic capillaries
  • The lymphocytes help to filter blood at the lymph nodes and this blood is recycled to the circulation

What are the three types of lymphocyte produced by common lymphoid progenitors?

  • Natural killer cells (NK)
  • T lymphocyte
  • B lymphocyte

What is the difference between natural killer (NK) cells and lymphocytes morphologically?

Natural killer cells (RIGHT) are bigger than B/T lymphocytes (LEFT)

Where are B lymphocytes made?

Progenitors are made in the foetal liver before differentiating in the bone marrow (B)

What is humoral immunity?

The immune response that involves the action of antibodies which combat different antigens.

How is the development of B lymphocytes centred around the idea of humoral immunity?

  • Their development involves the rearrangement of the genes coding for the heavy and light chains
  • These different arrangements lead to different immunoglobulins (antibodies) being produced which can fight against different antigens.

How does differentiation work to produce different types of B cells? Which type of lymphocytes are involved in cell-mediated immunity?

  • Differentiation in B cells involves Ig heavy and light chain gene rearrangement
  • This way, the variable region of the antibody is different and a different type of B cell is produced
  • T lymphocytes
  • Natural killer cells

What is leukocytosis?

The term used to describe an elevated white blood cell count.

How can transient leukocytosis help a clinician?

  • It can suggest that there is a secondary (external) cause for the elevated WBC count
  • This could be an infection, and the type of WBC elevated in number can give us indication of what type of infection it is

What type of infection causes neutrophilia/monocytosis ?

Bacterial

What type of infection causes lymphocytosis?

Viral

What type of infection causes eosinophilia?

Parasitic

What does persistent leukocytosis suggest to us?

  • There is damage in the DNA that affects a precursor lymphoid cell and this is giving rise to blood cancers, causing elevated WBC levels.
  • Chronic leukaemias, lymphomas, myelomas
  • It just suggests to us that the increase in WBC levels is not due to response to pathogens, but is primary
  • There is no apparent cause for it

What is a myeloma?

A cancer of plasma cells (derived from B cells)

What is leukopenia?

  • A reduction in the total number of white cells.
  • It is usually caused by a reduction in neutrophil count (as they are the most prevalent WBC)

What is the most abundant leukocyte in circulation?

Neutrophils.

Recall three causes of neutrophilia.

  • Infection
  • Inflammation
  • Tissue damage
  • Pregnancy
  • Exercise
  • Corticosteroids

What adverse effects can neutrophilia lead to?

  • Toxic granulation.
  • Left shift

What is a left shift, and what causes it?

  • A left shift is an increase in neutrophil precursors, usually with less lobes, in the blood
  • Chronic myeloid leukaemias cause left shift. Increased demand on the bone marrow.

What are the main causes of neutropenia?

  • Radiotherapy and chemotherapy
  • In other cases, it can be caused by autoimmune disorders and severe bacterial infections.

What can neutropenia lead to?

  • Increased risk of serious infection
  • These people need urgent treatment with intravenous antibiotics

What is neutrophil hypersegmentation and what causes it?

  • This is also known as right shift
  • It is when a neutrophil has more than the regular 3-5 lobes (right shift)
  • It is caused by a lack of vitamin B12 or folic acid which prevents the cell from dividing
  • Similar to B12/folate deficiencies causing macrocytic anaemia in erythrocytes

What can cause eosinophilia?

  • Parasitic infection
  • Allergy

What can cause basophilia?

  • Usually a form of leukaemia
  • i.e Chronic myeloid leukaemia (CML)

What is monocytosis?

Too many monocytes

What can cause monocytosis?

Infection or chronic inflammation.

What causes lymphocytosis?

Normally, lymphocytosis occurs in response to a viral infection

What is the most common lymphocyte type in circulating blood?

CD4+ T (helper) cells

What causes lymphopenia?

  • HIV infection
  • Chemotherapy
  • Radiotherapy
  • Corticosteroids

What is a mutagen?

A mutagen is a chemical or physical agent capable of inducing mutations.

What are the two main types of leukaemia, and how are they classified?

  • Myeloid
  • i.e granulocytes, monocytes → Acute/Chronic myeloid leukaemia
  • Lymphoid
  • i.e lymphocytes → Chronic lymphocytic leukaemia/Acute lymphoblastic leukaemia
  • A leukaemia can fall under one of these categories based on where the mutation leading to the cancer takes place.

What happens to white blood cells in leukaemia (pathogenesis)?

  • The mutant cells have a distinct survival advantage over the non- mutant cells, and so they replace them
  • The mutant cells proliferate over time, before taking over the blood and they are dysfunctional
  • This leads to patients becoming extremely susceptible to infection

The four types of leukaemia

What is the main difference between acute leukaemias and chronic leukaemias?

  • Acute leukaemias typically occur in blast (immature) cells
  • The cells typically halt due to failure in their life-cycle and proliferate immaturely
  • Because these immature cells have a higher proliferative capacity, this can lead to propagation of the leukaemia much quicker than in chronic cases
  • Chronic leukaemias typically occur in partially mature cells
  • The cells typically have partially finished maturing and proliferate thereafter Diagrams showing the difference between lymphoblastic and lymphocytic leukaemia.
