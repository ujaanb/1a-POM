Overview

What determines an individual’s blood group?

The combination of antigens on the surface of their red blood cells.

What two conditions can be caused by antibodies acting against red blood cells?

  • Haemolytic transfusion reactions (HTRs)
  • Haemolytic disease of the foetus and newborn (HDFN)

What happens in haemolytic transfusion reactions (HTR)?

The antigens on the transfused red blood cells are recognised as foreign which leads to haemolysis of these cells using antibodies from the patient’s plasma

What happens in haemolytic disease of the foetus and newborn (HDFN)?

  • Antibodies from the mother cross the placenta and recognise the foetal RBCs as foreign which leads to haemolysis in the fetus
  • This only occurs when the mother has a different blood group to the foetus.

What are the two types of ABO antibody that can act against RBCs?

  • Naturally occurring
  • Acquired alloantibodies

What are naturally occurring antibodies?

These are ABO antibodies (i.e anti-A, anti-B) that exist naturally in humans and are used to combat antibodies on foreign RBC blood groups

When are naturally occurring antibodies first produced?

  • When the host encounters them in young age from food and microorganisms
  • This is because these sugars are very similar to those found on the antigens of RBCs

What type of antibody is usually a naturally occurring antibody?

IgM

What is a consequence of foreign blood group transfusion caused by naturally occurring antibodies?

  • Haemolytic transfusion reaction (HTR)
  • This is because the antibodies recognise the foreign antigens and cause agglutination as well as intravascular haemolysis as they kill the foreign RBCs

When are acquired alloantibodies initially produced?

  • As a result of active immunisation (alloimmunisation) to ‘non-self’ RBC antigens following exposure to RBCs from another individual
  • i.e
  • When an unfamiliar blood group is transfused to a host
  • When an unfamiliar fetal blood group crosses the placenta into the mother’s bloodstream.

What type of antibody is usually an acquired alloantibody? Which antibody can cross the placenta to cause HDFNs?

  • IgG.
  • IgG - acquired alloantibody

What is the difference between HTRs caused by naturally occurring antibodies and acquired alloantibodies?

  • HTRs caused by naturally occurring antibodies lead to massive intravascular haemolysis
  • This is because IgM antibodies are capable of fully activating complement
  • HTRs caused by acquired alloantibodies lead to mild intravascular haemolysis and delayed HTRs

What are the four main blood groups within the ABO blood group system? Which antigens are expressed by each of the cells in the blood groups?

  • A, B, AB and O
  • A (A antigen)
  • B (B antigen)
  • AB (both A and B antigen)
  • O (neither antigen)

What does the A gene code for?

The A gene codes for an enzyme that adds N-acetyl galactosamine (GalNac) to the common H antigen resulting in the A antigen

What does the B gene code for?

The B gene codes for an enzyme that adds galactose (Gal) to the common H antigen resulting in the B antigen

What does the O gene code for? Which blood group is more prevalent in the British Caucasian population? Which blood group is more prevalent in the South East Asian population? Which blood group is more prevalent in the African population?

  • The O gene produces an inactive enzyme so the H antigen remains unchanged and neither A nor B antigen can be formed . The O gene is recessive. Image showing the differences between A, B and O blood groups.
  • Groups A and O
  • Group B
  • Group O

Why may there be differences in the prevalence of blood groups in different areas?

  • Different blood groups can give you different survival advantages in different areas
  • This is because selection pressures are different, and the advantageous areas varies with geographical distribution

Recall two ways that blood groups can alter susceptibility to infection.

  • Receptors on RBCs can allow colonisation and invasion of pathogens
  • “False receptors” on RBCs can prevent pathogens binding to tissue

What is Landsteiner's law?

  • It is a law that states that whichever ABO antigens are lacking on a given person's RBCs, that person will always have the corresponding antibody.
  • This is explained due to the exposure to these antigens on food and microorganisms at a young age.

Recall two reasons why IgG antibodies that cross the placenta don’t even end up causing HDFN most of the time. Which blood group can be donated universally to all patients and why?

  • Foetal RBCs have poorly developed antigens which don’t bind well to antibodies
  • ABO antigens are found on numerous other cells (not just red cells) so any IgG ABO antibodies that have crossed the placenta can be ‘mopped-up’ by these cells without causing damage to fetal RBCs
  • Those antibodies that cross usually disappear after a few months as the baby begins to develop its own antibodies.
  • Group O
  • These contain neither A or B antigens and so there is no risk of causing acute HTRs.

When is a platelet unit described as “high-titre negative”? Which should be used, high-titre or high-titre negative platelets and why?

  • When there is a low concentration of anti-A or anti-B antibodies in their plasma and they are unlikely to attack ABO antigens on RBCs.
  • This is because platelets are usually administered with plasma (platelets are found in plasma)
  • On the contrary, high-titre platelets are contain lots of anti-A or anti- B antibodies that are likely to attack ABO antigens
  • High-titre negative platelets
  • This is because their plasma contains a lower concentration of anti- AB antibodies than high-titre platelets which means lower risk of HTR
  • NOTE: This only matters if the donor’s plasma group does not match the recipient’s plasma group. If the groups were matching, we’d have no concern as no matter how many antibodies are present in the plasma, they would not attack the host ABO antigens as the blood groups are the same.

What is FFP?

Fresh-frozen plasma.

What is the universal plasma donor group and why?

  • Group AB plasma as it contains no antibodies
  • This is because Group AB individuals have both A and B antigens in their blood, so it would make no sense for them to naturally produce antibodies against these antigens

What is main type of antigen under the Rh system?

The D antigen.

Is the gene for this antigen dominant or recessive?

  • Dominant
  • This means the RhD positive phenotype is coded for by a dominant allele.

What is the name of antibodies that act against antigen D?

Anti-D antibodies

What type of antibodies are anti-D antibodies?

IgG

Do RhD negative individuals have naturally occurring anti-D antibodies?

  • No.
  • They only develop anti-D antibodies on exposure to anti-D antigen

How long after exposure to antigen D does it take antibodies to appear?

2-5 months.

What is anti-D immunoglobulin and when is it used?

It is a treatment commonly used for pregnant RhD negative women to prevent the destruction of blood cells in their RhD positive foetus

How does anti-D immunoglobulin work? Which Rh blood phenotype can be transfused to anyone?

  • Foetal RhD positive RBCs are destroyed when a mother’s anti-D antibodies cross the placenta into the foetus
  • Anti-D immunoglobulin works by destroying any cells that have antigen D on them in the mother’s circulation, preventing the mother from producing anti-D antibodies and eliminating the risk of this cross-placental cell death.
  • NOTE: This only works in some cases, as the mother will need additional monitoring in other cases
  • RhD negative
  • This is because RhD negative RBCs have no antigen D on them and so there is no risk of HTRs. Also, RhD positive individuals have no antibodies that work against antigen D so this is insignificant anyway

Why do platelets for blood transfusion need to be of the same RhD group as the host, even though platelets do not express the RhD antigen?

Some of the components of the platelet units can contain RhD positive RBCs that can lead to development of antibody (alloimmunisation) in RhD negative patients

How does forward grouping work when testing for ABO antigens?

The A and B antibodies are added to the patient’s red cells once at a time and agglutination when an antibody is added indicates the presence of a red blood cell antigen for that antibody

How does reverse grouping work when testing for ABO antigens?

The A and B antigens are added to the patient’s serum antibodies once at a time and agglutination when an antigen is added indicates the presence of a antibody for that antigen Figure showing how forward and reverse grouping works.

How do we test for RhD groups?

  • The anti-D reagent is added to the red cells.
  • Agglutination will occur in RhD positive individuals but no agglutination occurs in RhD negative individuals.

What is the purpose of antibody screening and how does it work?

  • Red blood cells that contain all of the clinically relevant antigens are added to the patient’s serum
  • Agglutination will occur when antibody interacts with antigen which helps us know which acquired alloantibodies are present in the patient’s serum and transfuse based on this knowledge
  • Anti-human globulin needs to be added as a second reagent to make the agglutination visible in this case.

What is the purpose of a cross-match and how does it work?

  • It is used in red-cell transfusions to assess a recipient’s compatibility with the donor’s red cels
  • The red cells are added to the patient’s serum and agglutination is looked out for
  • Agglutination means there is the presence of an antibody which will reject the red cells and hence incompatibility
  • No agglutination means there is no antibody which will reject the red cells and hence compatibility

What is the difference between antibody screening and cross-match testing?

Cross-match is different from antibody screening because whilst antibody screening looks for the presence of all acquired alloantibodies, but cross-match only looks to see if the antibodies against the transfused RBC antigen is present

What is the difference between “group and screen” and “infection” testing?

  • Group and screen testing involves the testing for antibodies which may cause rejection
  • It also confirms the ABO and Rh blood groups of the patient
  • Infection testing is more directed at blood-borne viruses and infections that may pose a threat to the recipient. Image showing tests done on blood donors in the UK. ZONE BELOW

What is apheresis?

A technique used to separate blood components from each other

Describe how apheresis works.

Blood from the donor passes through the machine which separates out the particular component required (e.g. red cells, platelets, or plasma) and returns the remainder of the blood to the donor.

Why are red cell transfusions usually required?

  • Red cell transfusions are required to increase the haemoglobin concentration in the recipient’s blood
  • 1 unit of red cells usually results in an increase in the haemoglobin by 10g/L in an average adult (weighing 70kg)

How are red cells stored?

Red blood cells (RBCs) are stored in a fridge at 4ºC.

What is the shelf-life of red blood cells?

35 days.

Why are platelet transfusions usually required?

Platelet transfusions are required for the treatment of bleeding or to reduce the risk of bleeding in patients with low platelet counts (thrombocytopenia) or platelet dysfunction (e.g. due to certain drugs such as antiplatelet medication)

What are the two main methods of platelet donation?

  • Pooled platelets - platelets separated from 4 whole blood donations are pooled together to make up 1 unit of platelets
  • i.e the primary aim of the donation was not platelet donation
  • Apheresis - a single donor donates 1 unit of platelets through apheresis
  • i.e the primary aim of the donation was platelet donation

How are platelets stored?

  • Platelets are stored at room temperature (22ºC)
  • Platelets require constant agitation to ensure the platelets are continuously oxygenated within their gas permeable containers.

What is the shelf-life of platelets?

7 days.

Why are FFP transfusions usually required?

  • FFP contains all the coagulation factors so transfusions of FFP are usually conducted to reduce the risk of bleeding.
  • It can be used in patients with coagulopathies such as increased

PT/APTT

What is the shelf-life of FFP/cryoprecipitate?

3 years.

How is FFP/cryoprecipitate stored?

  • FFP must be stored at 4ºC
  • However, it has a shelf-life of 3 years at -25ºC
  • Once thawed, FFP must be used within 24 hours

Why are cryoprecipitate transfusions usually required? Which coagulation factors are found in cryoprecipitate?

  • Cryoprecipitate contains fibrinogen, Factor VIII, von Willebrand factor (VWF) and Factor XIII.
  • Cryoprecipitate transfusions can hence be used to treat or reduce the risk of bleeding
  • Fibrinogen
  • Factor VIII
  • von Willebrand factor (VWF)
  • Factor XIII. In response to what is cryoprecipitate usually used?
  • Low fibrinogen
  • Fibrinogen dysfunction.

What are the two main methods of cryoprecipitate donation?

  • Pooled donations - 5 whole blood units put together
  • Single donation

How is cryoprecipitate made?

  • Cryoprecipitate is made by slow thawing of FFP overnight until there is a precipitate.
  • This precipitate is then re-suspended in a small volume of plasma and frozen to maintain the activity of the clotting factors. Image showing the use of all the blood component transfusions.

What is the use of human albumin solution (HAS)?

  • To increase plasma/albumin levels of the recipient.
  • It can be done
  • To restore plasma volume in patients with plasma volume loss e.g. due to burns or trauma
  • To replace plasma in plasma exchange e.g in the treatment of autoimmune disorders
  • To initiate diuresis in patient with low albumin

What is the use of immunoglobulin solution?

This contains antibodies to viruses that need to be fought against in the recipient

What are the two types of immunoglobulin solution?

  • Normal immunoglobulin
  • This contains antibodies to viruses that are common in the population.
  • Specific immunoglobulins
  • This is made from selected donors with high antibody levels to the target of treatment

What are clotting factor concentrates?

  • These are concentrates used to replenish the level of a single or multiple clotting factors
  • i.e Fibrinogen concentrates, single factor concentrates

What do Prothrombin complex concentrates (PCCs) contain?

Factors II, VII, IX and X.

What can Prothrombin complex concentrates (PCCs) be used to combat?

  • Prothrombin complex concentrates (PCCs) can be used to reduce the effect of anticoagulants such as warfarin.
  • This is because it contains all the liver-synthesised clotting factors that rely on Vitamin K for carboxylation, and so it can counter the effects of warfarin as a vitamin K antagonist

What is an alternative to cryoprecipitates that can be used to replace fibrinogen in patients?

Fibrinogen concentrates provide an alternative to cryoprecipitate for replacing fibrinogen
